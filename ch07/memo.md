## 1. Cade's Rebellion, 1450

**[CONSENSUS]** The rising organised across Kent in late May–early June 1450;
c. 5,000 encamped at Blackheath, Cade styling himself "John Mortimer,"
"Captain of Kent." A royal detachment under the Staffords was ambushed and
destroyed at Sevenoaks (**18 June**); the rebels entered London (**3 July**),
gave Lord Saye and Sele (the Treasurer) a sham trial and beheaded him, with
his son-in-law Crowmer, ex-sheriff of Kent (**3–4 July**); the Londoners and
the Tower garrison drove the rebels back across London Bridge in a night
battle (**5–6 July**); pardons negotiated by Bishop Waynflete dispersed the
host (6–8 July); Cade, his pardon void (issued to "Mortimer"), was taken
mortally wounded near Heathfield (**~12 July**) and died on the cart to
London. (britainexpress, thewarsoftheroses, T3; Britannica via snippet —
confirm in browser; Wikipedia T4 pointers.)

**The demands — restoration, in their own words. [CONSENSUS, T1]** The
*Complaint of the Poor Commons of Kent* (Wikisource, from Stow): the king is
surrounded by men "fals and of nowght browght up"; no justice "with out
bribes"; his counsellors claim him "a bove his lawys"; "his marchandyse is
lost… the see is lost, Fraunce is lost"; the king "may not pay for his mete
nor drynke"; named Kentish extortioners (Slegge, Crowmer, Isle, Est); and the
demand that the king take "the Duke of Yorke" and other true lords into his
council. **No article demands deposition.** The Merfold risings (Sussex,
autumn 1450) — which reportedly *did* speak of killing the king — supply the
contrast proving Cade's conservatism. The second manifesto (the "Articles"/
"Bill of Requests") has no open transcription — **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
Harvey, *Jack Cade's Rebellion of 1450*; Kaufman, *Sourcebook*, 2019]**.

**Composition. [CONSENSUS on the skeleton; percentages gated]** The pardon
list includes 1 knight, 2 MPs, 18 squires, 70+ gentlemen; the circulating
occupational breakdown (~15% craftsmen/traders, ~15% husbandmen, ~13%
yeomen, **~6% labourers**, ~4% hundred-constables) derives from Harvey's
pardon-roll analysis — anchor the percentages to her book **[UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL: Harvey]**. Bohna's argument that the rising mobilised *through
the county's official muster structures* — "the militia of the county
embodied" — is the sharpest scholarly statement of the propertied,
office-holding character **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Bohna, *EHR* 118
(2003)]**. Clergy joined; returning Normandy soldiers swelled the ranks.

**Context and aftermath. [CONSENSUS]** Cade sits inside ch05's crisis year:
Moleyns lynched (Jan 1450), Suffolk murdered at sea (2 May), Aiscough
murdered (June), Normandy completing its fall (Cherbourg, 12 Aug) —
consistent with ch05/ch06. Aftermath: pardons held for most; continued Kent
unrest into 1451; the judicial "**harvest of heads**" — the term is
contemporary, from Gregory's Chronicle (verified) — with the king personally
present at the Canterbury sessions. Institutionally, essentially nothing was
conceded; but the rebellion's grievances became Yorkist propaganda through
the 1450s. **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Cade is the type-case: middling composition,
restorationist demands, fiscal-misgovernment grievance, absorbed by pardon
and repression — shaping the politics that followed without redirecting the
consolidation to come.

## 2. The Praguerie, 1440

**[CONSENSUS]** The princes' revolt — Bourbon, Alençon, La Trémoille, with
the sixteen-year-old Dauphin Louis as figurehead — against Charles VII,
February–July 1440, named for the Hussite Prague risings. **The fiscal-
military connection is precise and verified:** the ordonnance of **2
November 1439** (from the Orléans Estates) forbade raising troops without
royal permission, reserved captaincies to the king, and established the
taille as an effectively permanent tax to pay for royal troops — the
document the revolt rose against; mercenary captains "who felt their
livelihood threatened" joined the princes (Britannica via snippet; 1911 EB
via Wikisource; Herodote.net, T3). Richemont and the king broke the revolt
in months; peace at **Cusset, July 1440**, on notably generous terms
(pardons, a pension for Bourbon, the Dauphiné for Louis) — and the
ordonnance was then *enforced*, with the compagnies d'ordonnance following
in 1445 (ch10's material). **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The Praguerie is the purest
case in the set: a revolt *explicitly against the emerging fiscal-military
state*, defeated, bought off — and the state-building proceeded.
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Contamine for the scholarly account.]**

## 3. The Ciompi legacy

**[CONSENSUS]** Florence, summer 1378: the unguilded wool workers (only
~200 of ~14,000 in the industry held guild membership) rose out of a
factional dispute, seized the palace (**22 July**, Michele di Lando made
Gonfaloniere), and won three new guilds (**30 July**) — representation for
the popolo minuto for the first time. The radical rump was crushed in the
Piazza (**31 August**); the Ciompi guild was abolished at once, the other
two by **1382**, opening the Albizzi oligarchic regime (1382–1434) that
Cosimo de' Medici's return (1434) converted into Medici hegemony (WGA;
Britannica via snippet; Hanover Historical Review open PDF, T2/T3). The
chapter's interpretive claim — that Italian propertied-anxious politics was
**absorbed rather than defeated** by the emerging signorie, the fear of 1378
feeding the drift to Medici dominance — is Najemy's framing **[UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL: Najemy, *A History of Florence*]**; open sources confirm the
skeleton, not the argument. *Note:* the Ciompi themselves were poorer than
the chapter's modal form — the *legacy* (the propertied classes' fear and
the oligarchic/signorial absorption) is what the chapter uses; state this
honestly rather than recruiting the wool workers to the propertied middle.

## 4. The German pattern

**[CONSENSUS, institutional open sources]** The **Bundschuh** conspiracies
(Upper Rhine): Sélestat 1493 (fiscal grievances: taxes, debt, arbitrary
justice, costly ecclesiastical privileges); Joss Fritz's Untergrombach 1502
("no lord but the Emperor"; church property to be divided), Lehen 1513, and
Lower Alsace 1517 — all betrayed and suppressed (DHIALSACE / BNU Strasbourg,
T2-adjacent institutional). **"Armer Konrad," Württemberg 1514** — the
cleanest fiscal trigger in the set: Duke Ulrich's new consumption taxes on
meat, wine and grain (with manipulated weights), revolt in the Remstal (May),
suppressed by July — and the **Treaty of Tübingen (1514)**: the estates
assumed ducal debt in exchange for constitutional rights, the concession-
within-consolidation pattern in miniature (Landesarchiv Baden-Württemberg,
T1/T2). All of it foreshadows 1525, which the chapter only gestures at.
**Correction per §0.4:** the outline's "urban revolts of the 1460s and
1470s" cannot be sourced; the documented urban cases are Cologne 1481, 1512,
1513. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Blickle; Scott & Scribner, for the
scholarly programmes.]**

## 5. The form's structural features

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT, assembled from the cases]** Across the set: leadership
and mass from the middling-propertied (Cade's pardon rolls; the Praguerie's
princes-with-popular-support; the Bundschuh's village office-holders) —
with the Ciompi as the marked poorer exception; demands framed as
**restoration** (the Complaint's good-governance petitions; "no lord but
the Emperor"; the gilets jaunes' "refoundation" of representation) rather
than revolution; grievance centred on **fiscal reach and corrupt
administration**; and the outcome — **absorption through selective
concession** (Cusset's pensions; Tübingen's rights-for-debt; the three
guilds granted then dissolved) alongside exemplary repression (the harvest
of heads), with consolidation proceeding.

## 6. The modern parallel — at pattern level, recalibrated

**[CONTESTED — run exactly as §0.1 dictates]** The framework: Mudde's
thin-centred ideology (the pure people vs the corrupt elite, *Government &
Opposition* 2004); Norris & Inglehart's cultural-backlash thesis (RWP16-026,
open, read in full) against the economic-insecurity thesis (Fetzer's
austerity-Brexit *AER* 2019), with Margalit (*JEP* 2019) as the best single
citation for the settled middle position (both, interacting; the field's
"modest" verdict on pure economic explanation). Composition per §0.1 —
petty-bourgeoisie/homeowner core for right-authoritarian populism (N&I;
Green & Pahontu; Silver/Manza-Crowley), M5S the contradiction, gilets
jaunes the complication, AfD the named exchange. Grievance content:
"take back control" as the Leave voters' own #1 reason (Ashcroft, 49%);
carbon-tax aversion rooted in distrust of the taxing state (Douenne &
Fabre, *AEJ:EP* 2022); the gilets jaunes demanding "refoundation," not
abolition, of representation. The Lipset/Bell petite-bourgeoisie lineage
(squeezed between big business and organised labour) gives the comparison
scholarly ancestry in N&I's own framing. **The comparison is between the
pattern of propertied-anxious revolt-under-consolidation across cases —
never Cade-to-Trump** (the named objection, held throughout).

## 7. The critical asymmetry — shape, not prevent

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT (c), with its sharpest exhibits]** The medieval revolts
shaped settlements — Tübingen's constitutionalism, Cusset's pensions, the
Yorkist absorption of Cade's grievances — and did not stop the artillery
state (chs. 10–11). The modern wave, on the evidence gathered: Brexit
delivered, and the UK tax take heads to "close to a post-war high" (OBR,
March 2024, T1); the gilets jaunes won the carbon-tax retreat and the
December 2018 package (cost figure unpinned — flag) while the programme
continued; populists in power damage institutions and growth (Funke et al.,
*AER* 2023) but nowhere in the gathered record durably shrink the state's
fiscal-administrative reach. Present as the book's inference, falsifiable in
Appendix C terms: a populist wave that durably *reduced* fiscal reach would
count against it. Forward-leaning close into ch08 (the plural legal
environment the consolidating state will also subdue).
