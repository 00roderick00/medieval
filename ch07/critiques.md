# Critiques — Ch. 7: Cade and the Communes

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 surfaced in
research — Objection 2 is the one the research itself forced. Each is
steelmanned before the answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — Trivial or misleading (the named objection)

**Steelman.** Comparing Cade to modern populism is either trivial or
misleading. Trivial: every popular revolt in recorded history features
anti-elite grievance, corruption complaints, and restoration rhetoric — a
pattern that catches the Gracchi, the Pilgrimage of Grace, Poujadism, and
Occupy explains nothing. Misleading: the differences dwarf the similarities —
Cade's Kent faced a bankrupt personal monarchy without police, media, or
elections; modern populists operate through universal-franchise parties
inside consolidated bureaucratic states. A fifteenth-century rising that
killed the Lord Treasurer and a referendum are not instances of one thing.
The comparison flatters the book's schema and Shakespeare's Cade, not the
evidence.

**The chapter's answer.** The useful comparison is not Cade-to-Trump but the
cross-case **pattern of propertied-anxious revolt under fiscal-military
transition** — and the pattern is specified tightly enough to exclude most
revolts: (a) composition centred on the anxious middle rather than the
destitute (pardon rolls; petty-bourgeoisie findings); (b) demands framed as
restoration of good governance, not overthrow (the Complaint's own words;
"take back control" as the Leave voters' stated first reason); (c) grievance
centred on fiscal reach and corrupt administration (the taille; the carbon
tax); (d) outcome — absorption through selective concession plus exemplary
repression, with consolidation proceeding. The Gracchi and Occupy fail
limbs (a) and (d); the Pilgrimage of Grace arguably *fits* — which is not an
embarrassment but confirmation that the form recurs where the conditions
recur. The differences the objection lists are stated as disanalogies in the
draft (§6 discipline); the claim is a pattern-recurrence claim, not an
identity claim, and it generates a falsifiable expectation (Objection 4).

## Objection 2 — The composition evidence cuts against the claim (forced by research)

**Steelman.** The chapter's load-bearing empirical claim — populist support
comes "from the propertied middle rather than from the truly poor" — is
contradicted by the chapter's own gathered evidence. Five Star's odds rose
1.68× with *precarity* and peaked in the distressed South; the gilets jaunes
were working/lower-middle, low-credential, peri-urban; the AfD's class base
is a live dispute (Lengfeld vs Lux); and even Brexit shows Leave *rates*
highest among council tenants and C2DE grades. Meanwhile the medieval set
has its own misfit: the Ciompi were unguilded wool workers — the poor, not
the propertied. The chapter is running a selective average over cases that
disagree.

**The chapter's answer.** Adopt the recalibration rather than resist it —
the memo (§0.1) already does. The defensible claim, in the evidence's own
terms: **the modal core of right-authoritarian populism is the anxious
middle — petty bourgeoisie, homeowners, median-plus incomes — while the
destitute and welfare-dependent are under-represented among its voters**
(Norris & Inglehart's verbatim finding; Green & Pahontu's homeowners;
Manza & Crowley's primary electorate), with the rate-vs-bulk distinction
stated for Brexit, M5S marked as the left-populist exception, the gilets
jaunes given the homeownership-in-the-periphery framing rather than an
income framing, and the AfD cited as an exchange. On the medieval side, the
Ciompi are used for their *legacy* (the propertied classes' fear and the
signorial absorption), not recruited to the propertied middle. The scope
narrows; the claim survives narrowing — and a claim that survives its own
stress-test at reduced scope is worth more to the book than the broad
version was. [This mirrors the ch01/ch06 pattern: win on the weaker,
harder-to-deny formulation.]

## Objection 3 — Cade rose against a weak state, not a consolidating one

**Steelman.** The chapter's frame — revolts "reacting to the fiscal-military
and administrative pressures of the emerging state" — fits the Praguerie
(explicitly against the 1439 ordonnance and the taille) and the German cases
(new consumption taxes, ecclesiastical exactions). It does not fit its
title case. Cade's Kent rose against *failure*: a crown that had lost
Normandy, could not pay for its "mete nor drynke," and whose local officers
extorted because central discipline had collapsed. That is a revolt against
hollow sovereignty (ch. 5's condition), not against consolidation. Filing
both under one form conflates opposite targets — the state too weak and the
state too strong.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the asymmetry openly — it is real, and the
draft should state it as a structural feature, not hide it. The form is
defined by composition, framing, grievance-type, and outcome (Objection 1's
four limbs), not by the strength of the target: the propertied middle rises
when the *fiscal-administrative relationship between centre and locality
breaks its expected terms* — whether by predatory weakness (Cade: extortion
amid collapse) or by consolidating extraction (the Praguerie: the taille;
Armer Konrad: the excise). The outline's own wording licenses this: the
revolts sought "either to restore an older constitution or to constitute a
new political community below the level of the crown." The two targets then
do different work in the book: Cade shows the anxious middle reacting to the
*crisis* (Part II's diagnosis), the Praguerie shows it reacting to the
*cure* (Part II–III's consolidation) — and the asymmetry itself supports the
chapter's close: whichever the target, the revolts shaped and did not
prevent. The draft should make this two-target structure explicit rather
than let a reviewer discover it.

## Objection 4 — "Shape but not prevent" is unfalsifiable hindsight

**Steelman.** The chapter's closing claim — revolts shape the settlement but
do not prevent it — is constructed to be true whatever happens. If a revolt
is crushed, consolidation proceeded; if it wins concessions, it "shaped" the
settlement; if it wins power (Brexit), the state's continued fiscal reach
proves it prevented nothing. No outcome could count against the claim. That
is not a pattern; it is a ratchet of interpretation. And it is asserted by
no source — the memo concedes it is the book's own inference.

**The chapter's answer.** Give the claim falsification conditions and an
indicator, which the book's apparatus exists to do. The claim fails if a
propertied-anxious wave durably *reduces* the state's fiscal-administrative
reach — operationalised in Appendix C (C7-1): a sustained post-populist fall
in tax take/GDP or the dismantling of a legibility instrument (mandatory
digital ID, reporting regimes) attributable to the wave. The gathered record
shows the opposite exhibit (OBR: post-Brexit tax take near a post-war high),
and Funke et al. show populists in power damaging institutions without
shrinking reach — but the claim is stated as the book's inference, dated,
and staked on the indicator. Historically the same test holds: Tübingen
*constrained* the duke (rights-for-debt) yet Württemberg's fisc grew; the
1439 taille outlived the Praguerie by three and a half centuries. If a
counter-case exists, Appendix C is where it will show.

## Objection 5 — The German limb is thin and the outline's decade is wrong

**Steelman.** The chapter's "German pattern" rests on conspiracies that never
rose (the Bundschuh plots were all betrayed), one six-week Württemberg
revolt, and an outline claim — "urban revolts of the 1460s and 1470s" — for
which the research found no named instance (the documented Cologne risings
are 1481, 1512, 1513). A pattern-argument that includes a limb of
non-events and an unsourced decade invites the ch06 verdict on loose
specifics.

**The chapter's answer.** Correct the decade (the ch06 discipline): the
draft uses the documented cases — Cologne 1481/1512/1513 within the Gaffel
constitution — or waits for the gated urban historiography (retrieval #7),
and does not assert the 1460s–70s claim. The Bundschuh's evidentiary value
is then stated for what it is: the *programmes* (fiscal grievance, "no lord
but the Emperor," church property divided) and the propertied-village
leadership (Schultheißen, former mayors) are documented in institutional
sources (DHIALSACE) even though the risings were pre-empted — conspiracies
reveal composition and demands as well as risings do, and their serial
suppression (1493–1517) followed by 1525 is itself the build-up the chapter
needs. Armer Konrad supplies the realised case with the cleanest fiscal
trigger and the Tübingen settlement. The limb stands, corrected and
downsized.
