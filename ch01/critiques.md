# Critiques — Ch. 1: The Return of the Middle Ages

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 were surfaced
during research. Each is steelmanned before the chapter answers it (§6). The
Perplexity second-opinion run on the draft is logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The medievalist's flattening complaint (the named objection)

**Steelman.** "Overlapping sovereignty c. 1200" is a slogan that flattens a
period of enormous variation. There was no single "medieval order": the
England of Henry II, with its precocious common law, itinerant justices and
Exchequer, was already a proto-state with a reach no thirteenth-century German
princeling approached; Capetian France was centralising; Norman Sicily was a
bureaucratic monarchy. To lump these with the fragmented Rhineland and
communal Italy under one "overlapping authority" heading is to select the most
fragmented cases and call them the norm — the very cherry-picking the book
accuses the neomedievalists of. Worse, the modern "overlapping jurisdiction"
being compared (platforms vs states) is a matter of *regulatory* competition
among functioning bureaucracies, not the *personal, parcelised* authority of
the medieval world; the analogy equivocates on "overlapping."

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the variation openly and narrow the claim, as
the outline directs: the baseline is not "the Middle Ages" but the specific,
unusually plural mid-thirteenth-century Rhineland and northern Italy —
precisely because those are the times and places the modern comparison
illuminates, and precisely *not* the centralising England or France that the
book's Part II will need as the *consolidators*. This is not cherry-picking but
case selection with its reasons stated: the chapter wants the dispersed pole of
a spectrum it will later show consolidating.

**Residual exposure — flagged, not hidden.** The northern-Italian half of this
defence is well sourced (communes; Guelph/Ghibelline; the 1250–73 Great
Interregnum). **The Rhineland half is currently under-sourced:** the claim that
the Rhineland was *exceptionally* fragmented specifically c. 1250 was not found
in open sources, which document the *early-modern* Kleinstaaterei instead
(sources.md RETRIEVAL #7). Until a regional monograph is retrieved, the draft
should either lean the defence on northern Italy or state the Rhineland claim
with an explicit citation once obtained — it must not assert "exceptionally
fragmented c. 1250" for the Rhineland on present evidence. On the "overlapping"
equivocation: the book answers it not here but by its whole method — it does
not rest on the *word* "overlapping" but on the *mechanism*; the resemblance is
scaffolding, the mechanism is the argument (CLAUDE.md §2). Any drafted passage
that leans on the resemblance rather than the mechanism is flagged
[ANALOGY-ONLY].

## Objection 2 — Cerny's checklist is a sieve that catches everything

**Steelman.** A six-point checklist loose enough to be "satisfied" by the
2020s was loose enough to be satisfied by the 1990s, the 1930s, or any period
of visible disorder. Multiple competing institutions, fragmented identities,
grey-zone economies, entrenched property — name a century without them. A
diagnostic that cannot fail is not a diagnostic; running it against the present
and finding it "confirmed" proves nothing.

**The chapter's answer.** The checklist is not offered as proof; it is the
*diagnostic* stage the book will supersede. Part I deliberately accepts the
neomedievalists' own instrument and shows the present satisfies it — because
the book's disagreement with them is not about the diagnosis (which it grants)
but about the *prognosis* (dispersal as terminal vs transitional). The
falsifiable content is not in the checklist; it is in the *mechanism* (Part II)
and the *indicators* (Appendix C), which can fail. The chapter should say this
plainly: the checklist earns its place by being the literature's, not the
book's, and by setting up the question the literature never answered.

## Objection 3 — Characteristic 2 refutes the whole frame

**Steelman.** Cerny's second characteristic — the *absence of exogenous
territorialising pressure* — is contradicted by the present. Since roughly
2017 the world has seen exactly such pressure: renewed great-power
competition, NATO's post-2022 consolidation, all members driving defence
spending up. If the defining neomedieval condition (no external threat forcing
consolidation) has lapsed, then by the literature's own criterion we are not in
a neomedieval world at all, and the chapter's "diagnosis confirmed" collapses.

**The chapter's answer.** This is the chapter's best turn, and it should lead
with it rather than defend against it. Cerny's own logic is that *without*
exogenous pressure there is no consolidation — the pressure is the trigger, not
a symptom. Its partial return is therefore not evidence against the book; it is
the leading edge of the book's *predicted re-consolidation*, the
re-territorialising force Part III argues is driving the Tudor turn. The
weakest-fitting neomedieval characteristic is weak in exactly the place, and at
exactly the time, the pendulum begins its return swing. The objection, pressed
hard, hands the book its Part III thesis a hundred pages early — so the draft
should let it, with a forward-pointer, not smother it. (Calibration: state this
as *consistent with* the thesis, not as proof of it; the indicators decide.)

## Objection 4 — Bull and Friedrichs are being quoted at second hand

**Steelman.** The chapter's two foundational authorities are not in the bundle,
and the memo concedes their primaries were unreachable; the formulations come
from a single secondary article (Baaz). To build the book's opening on
second-hand quotation of its two most important predecessors is exactly the
provenance failure the doctrine forbids — and if Baaz has paraphrased or
mis-paginated, the error propagates into the book's foundation.

**The chapter's answer.** The doctrine's rule is "never cite a gated work from
memory as if consulted" (§4) — and the chapter does not. It cites a specific,
open, peer-reviewed source (Baaz) that reproduces the passages *verbatim with
page numbers*, corroborated by a second open source (Gibbins) and by
independent search. That is legitimate scholarly practice (citing a reliable
intermediary), not fabrication. The exposure is real but bounded: the exact
heading order and Bull's verbatim caution wording are marked [UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL] and will not be quoted until the primary is in hand. The draft must
not upgrade "quoted in Baaz" to a bare "Bull writes" until then. This is the
ch06 discipline (cite the open intermediary; retrieve the primary for the
load-bearing quote) applied to the humanities.

## Objection 5 — "The state as anomaly" overstates a contested case

**Steelman.** The chapter's closing move — that the sovereign state is a mere
contingent settlement that can dissolve — rests on the revisionist Westphalian-
myth literature (Osiander, Teschke), which is itself contested. Andrew Latham
and others argue medieval and modern sovereignty differ in degree, not kind;
if so, the state is not an "anomaly" poised to vanish but a durable
institutional form, and the book's whole "return swing" loses its pivot.

**The chapter's answer.** The book does not need the maximal claim (the state
as freak accident) and should not make it. It needs only the minimal,
uncontested one: the sovereign state is a *settlement produced by specific
pressures*, so that *if those pressures change, the settlement can change* —
which even Latham's "variation on a theme" concedes. The draft states both
sides (Osiander/Teschke vs Latham) and rests the argument on the weaker
version, exactly as the chapter's treatment of Cerny rests on the diagnosis it
can grant and the ch06 answer to Rogers rests on his own fiscal concession.
The book's strength throughout is that it wins on the weaker, harder-to-deny
version of each claim.
