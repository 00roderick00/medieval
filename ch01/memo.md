## 1. Bull's hypothesis and Bull's caution

**[CONSENSUS, via open secondary]** Bull's "new mediaevalism" is the book's
point of departure. In *The Anarchical Society* (1977), ch. 10, he entertained
that "sovereign states might disappear and be replaced not by a world
government but by a modern and secular equivalent of the kind of universal
political organisation that existed in Western Christendom in the Middle Ages"
— a system whose "central characteristic" is "a system of overlapping
authority and multiple loyalty" (Bull 1977: 245, quoted verbatim in Baaz,
*Scandinavian Studies in Law* 62, open). He identified **five** trends as
*prima facie* evidence: the regional integration of states; the disintegration
of states; the restoration of private international violence; transnational
organisations; and the technological unification of the world (Bull 1977:
245–266, via Baaz). **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL** for the exact order and
capitalisation of Bull's own subsection headings, and for his verbatim
concluding-caution sentences: Baaz paraphrases the caution; the "I do not
myself…/insufficient evidence" wording could not be quoted from open sources.**]**

**Bull's caution [CONSENSUS, via open secondary].** Having reviewed the five
trends, Bull "reache[d] the conclusion that there was after all not sufficient
evidence for the emergence of a new medievalism in the mid-1970s" (Baaz,
paraphrasing Bull 1977: 254–266). **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The chapter takes this
caution as its honest hinge and inverts it exactly as the Introduction's
"pendulum wager" does: the evidence Bull found insufficient in 1977 is now
sufficient — but for a different conclusion than a stable neomedieval order.
The draft should treat Bull not as a strawman but as the founder whose
doubt was correct for his decade and is not correct for ours (positioning
discipline, CLAUDE.md §3).

## 2. The pan-European baseline circa 1200

**[CONSENSUS]** The substrate against which the modern comparison runs is the
parcelised, non-exclusive sovereignty of the high Middle Ages — Perry
Anderson's "patchwork of overlapping and incomplete rights of government…
inextricably superimposed and tangled, and in which… plural allegiances,
asymmetrical suzerainties and anomalous enclaves abounded" (*Lineages of the
Absolutist State*, 1974, pp. 37–38; open via the ISEG Lisbon PDF per
reading-list.md; canonised for IR by Ruggie, "Territoriality and Beyond,"
*International Organization* 1993, open). The modal units coexisting c. 1200:
the papal monarchy, the Holy Roman Empire, local lords, free/imperial cities,
military orders, and urban/merchant leagues (Latham, summarised open at
medievalists.net, T3).

The two universalisms, per the brief's comparators:
- **The papal monarchy** at its apex under Innocent III (r. 1198–1216), the
  pope claimed as "ultimate arbitrator in both spiritual and temporal affairs"
  (New World Encyclopedia, T3/T4). A scholarly statement of Innocent as the
  apex of the papal-monarchy claim needs **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Colin
  Morris, *The Papal Monarchy*]**.
- **The Holy Roman Empire** as universal but contested authority — its princes
  held *Landeshoheit*, a territorial authority "approximating but never equal
  to full sovereignty" (encyclopedia.com; medievalists.net, T3).

Supporting modal units [CONSENSUS, with caveats]: free imperial cities
"subject to no one but the emperor"; the military orders (Templars,
Hospitallers, Teutonic Knights) as supranational actors with papal patronage
and, in the Teutonic case, their own territorial state (popular sources, T3 —
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Nicholson/Forey]** for a scholarly statement); the
Hanseatic League as a commercial actor that "negotiated with kings as
near-equals" (worksinprogress.co; Britannica, T3). **Caveat to carry:** the
Hansa crystallised as a named confederation across the 13th–14th c. — c. 1200
it is *nascent*; the northern-Italian communal/Lombard leagues are the better
strictly-1200 instance. And the vivid HRE fragmentation counts (25 secular
principalities, ~90 ecclesiastical territories, ~70 free cities) are given for
**c. 1450, not 1200** — do not misdate them (the ch06 "don't misdate the
Guicciardini quote" discipline applies).

**The narrowed comparator baseline [CONTESTED / under-sourced].** Per the
outline's answer to the flattening objection, the chapter narrows to the
mid-13th-century Rhineland and northern Italy. Northern Italy is well
supported: expansionist communes, the Guelph (pro-papal)/Ghibelline
(pro-imperial) contest as "a particularly important aspect of the internal
politics of medieval Italy," and the power vacuum after Frederick II's death
(1250) — the "Great Interregnum" 1250–1273 (Britannica; Met Museum timeline,
T3; scholarly anchor **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Philip Jones, *The Italian
City-State*; Wickham]**). The Rhineland claim is the weak point — see §0.3 and
critiques.md.

## 3. Friedrichs's reconstruction — the analytical engine

**[CONSENSUS, via open secondary]** Friedrichs supplies the frame the chapter
runs on. He defines the medieval order as "a system of overlapping authority
and multiple loyalty, held together by a duality of competing universalistic
claims" (Friedrichs, "The Meaning of New Medievalism," *EJIR* 7:4, 2001: 475,
quoted verbatim in Baaz, open). He then translates the medieval **Empire /
Church** pair of universalisms into a modern pair: the (territorial)
**nation-state system** and the (non-territorial) **transnational market
economy**, read as "competing but independent… hegemonic projects" (Friedrichs
2001: 486–490, via Baaz; corroborated in the open Gibbins article). **[BOOK'S
ARGUMENT]** This duality is the chapter's analytical engine — and the book's
Part I correction sits precisely here: Friedrichs's second universalism, the
"market," is treated by the book not as a market but as a set of *institutional
universalisms* (the platforms, Chapter 2), which is where the diagnosis is
extended rather than merely accepted. The exact sentence-level Empire/Church→
state/market wording is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Friedrichs 2001, pp.
475–476, 486–490]**, though the two blockquotes above are verbatim via Baaz.

## 4. Cerny's six characteristics, applied

The chapter runs the checklist against the present. **Enumeration caveat (§0.1):
the six-fold list is Cerny 1998 (RETRIEVAL); the bundled 2005 gives five.**
Modern evidence per characteristic (solid anchors starred):

1. **Multiple competing institutions / overlapping jurisdictions.** ★ EU DMA
   gatekeeper designations over US platforms (six gatekeepers designated 6 Sep
   2023; EU DMA portal, primary T1); the GDPR / US CLOUD Act / China PIPL
   "compliance trilemma" of conflicting obligations on the same data (analysis,
   T3 — cite the underlying statutes as primary). **[CONSENSUS]**
2. **Absence of exogenous territorialising pressure.** **[CONTESTED — and
   on-thesis.]** This is the weakest-fitting characteristic for the 2020s:
   NATO's post-2022 consolidation and renewed great-power competition partly
   *contradict* it (Foreign Policy; NDU, T2/T3; verify the "all 32 members at
   2% GDP" figure at nato.int before print — **NOT FOUND at primary level**).
   **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The draft should turn this: Cerny's own point was that
   *without* exogenous pressure there is no consolidation. The partial return
   of that pressure is not a failure of the diagnosis but the leading edge of
   the book's *predicted re-consolidation* — the re-territorialising force that
   Part III argues is driving the Tudor turn. The weakest neomedieval
   characteristic is weak precisely where the pendulum is starting back.
3. **Uneven consolidation of spaces, cleavages, inequalities.** ★ World
   Inequality Report 2022 (primary): richest 10% hold 76% of wealth, take 52%
   of income; poorest half own 2%. Charter-city enclaves: Próspera v. Honduras
   (~$11bn ISDS claim; Honduras voided all ZEDEs 20 Sep 2024; UNCTAD, primary).
   **[CONSENSUS]**
4. **Fragmented loyalties and identities.** ★ Pew 2023–24 (36 countries,
   65,000+): sharp declines since 2016 in viewing birthplace/customs/religion
   as central to "truly belonging" (primary survey). **Flag:** no clean 2020s
   quantified measure of "platform-mediated identity" exists — frame that
   qualitatively (**NOT FOUND**). **[CONSENSUS on the survey; the platform-
   identity extension is BOOK'S ARGUMENT.]**
5. **Entrenchment of property rights (esp. intangible).** ★ UNCTAD: cumulative
   treaty-based ISDS cases reached 1,332 by end-2023 (primary); Pistor's *Code
   of Capital* as the frame for IP/data as legally-coded capital. Ocean Tomo's
   "intangibles = 90% of S&P 500" is **[CONTESTED estimate — flag]**.
6. **Legal grey zones and the black/informal economy.** ★ EU Tax Observatory
   2024: ~10% of world GDP held offshore (primary; note the honest reading —
   automatic info-exchange has *reduced* the untaxed share since 2013).
   Shadow-economy ~32% of GDP (Medina–Schneider, IMF — **[CONTESTED model
   estimate]**); illicit crypto volumes (Chainalysis — **[CONTESTED lower-bound
   estimate]**).

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The checklist is the diagnostic instrument, not the
argument; running it demonstrates the dispersal Part I accepts, so that Part II
can show what the literature never wrote — the mechanism that ends it.

## 5. Slaughter's networked-government alternative

**[CONSENSUS, bundled]** Slaughter's *A New World Order* (Princeton, 2004), chs.
5–6 (bundled; see corpus/bundled/index.md) is the optimist's read of the same
phenomena: the state is not disappearing but *disaggregating* into
transnational networks of courts, regulators and legislators, presented as
"the architecture of a new world order." **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The chapter
treats Slaughter as important and wrong — the networks she identified were
real, but they are the connective tissue of the *dispersal phase*, not the
architecture of a settlement; where she sees networks dispersing power, the
book's mechanism predicts the underlying stacks concentrating it (the "important
and wrong" framing is developed in the bundled index and belongs, in fuller
form, to Chapter 12's "two open gaps").

## 6. The state as the anomaly

**[CONTESTED — sides named]** The chapter's closing move: the Westphalian
sovereign state is a specific, late, contingent settlement, not the natural
condition of European politics. Two open, citable anchors support this:
Osiander, "Sovereignty, International Relations, and the Westphalian Myth,"
*International Organization* 55:2 (2001) — the sovereign-state narrative is a
19th–20th-c. retrojection, and the 1648 treaties "almost exclusively concerned
[with] the Holy Roman Empire," not the founding of a sovereign-state system
(open full PDF, T2); and Teschke, *The Myth of 1648* (2003) — modern
territorial sovereignty postdates 1648, geopolitics remaining dynastic/
absolutist until the rise of capitalism (open full text, T2, informal host).
**The other side, named:** Andrew Latham argues late-medieval vs early-modern
sovereignty was "more a variation on a theme than a difference in kind"
(medievalists.net, T3) — i.e. the "state as anomaly" move is contested, not
settled. The draft states both, and rests the book's claim on the weaker,
uncontested version (the settlement can change because it *is* a settlement),
exactly as the ch06 answer to Rogers rests on the weaker, uncontested claim.
