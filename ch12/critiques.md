# Critiques — Ch. 12: Not Modernity, But Tudor

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test (determinism). Objections
2–5 surfaced in research and synthesis. Each steelmanned before the
answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The determinism complaint (named)

**Steelman.** Twelve chapters of mechanism add up to a machine: weapon
capital-intensity in, Tudor settlement out. The disclaimers are ritual —
every "conditional" prediction is followed by a chapter arguing the
conditions obtain. A theory that explains the 1450s, the 2020s, four
blocs, the losses in court, the delays in Brussels, and the drones in
Donbas explains everything and forbids nothing. History's actual drivers
— contingency, personality, accident (a battle-death at Nancy; an
election; a pandemic) — appear only as "failure modes" absorbed into the
model.

**The chapter's answer.** The wager is conditional and the conditions
are now *sharper than the outline's*, because the research narrowed
them. The Introduction's four failure conditions stand, and Appendix C
has grown their operational form: the thesis fails if decisive
capability genuinely proliferates (C6-1/C10-2), if bloc-internal fiscal
reach falls (C11-2), if platform universalisms consolidate independence
(C2-3/C3-2 — with the Anthropic arc logged as the live test), or if
state-platform adjudication migrates out of state courts (C11-3). Nancy
is in the book precisely as contingency: one battle-death unwound
Burgundy's apparatus — into other crowns' inheritance, which is the
point; contingency redistributes the settlement, it has not yet
reversed the mechanism. And the book's evidentiary signature cuts
against the everything-fits charge: at every contested joint the
mechanism is attested by its opponents (the sceptics-concede thread),
and the memos have repeatedly *rejected* claims the model would have
liked (the 70% intelligence-budget figure; the signorie asymmetry; the
1460s urban revolts; the 96% drone claim). A machine does not discard
its own fuel.

## Objection 2 — The drones have already decided it: the low end is the decisive layer

**Steelman.** The chapter's own numbers: 60–70% of destroyed systems,
~70% of casualties, >60% of strikes — the longbow is not "raising the
price of aggression," it is doing the killing. The March 2025 episode
proves only that Ukraine is a dependent client, not that the decisive
layer decides; Russia's line has been held for years by FPVs and
artillery, not by anyone's constellation. If the majority instrument of
attrition costs $400, the capital-intensity thesis has the sign wrong.

**The chapter's answer.** Three moves, all in the memo's record. First,
measurement discipline: those are attribution shares within a war both
of whose sides are *kept fighting* by the concentrated layer — the
natural experiment ran in March 2025, when a week's pause in the
patron's intelligence, imagery and aid degraded the drone-rich army's
deep fires and air defence within days. Attribution of casualties is
not attribution of war-decision. Second, the cheap layer at scale is
not cheap: 2.2M and ~3M drones a year are procurement programmes;
Russia's Shahed campaign is an industrial complex with a documented
6,000-unit production target — the longbow, scaled, becomes exactly the
fiscal-military object the thesis describes (the francs-archers point).
Third, the specialist literature the chapter quotes makes the bounded
claim, not the maximal one: UAV complexes "do not eliminate the
requirement for traditional artillery" and rest on EW/ISR foundations
(Bronk & Watling). The counter-position (Hammes) is staged on its own
terms; what would vindicate it is a full-stack adversary *defeated* by
the distributed layer without patron scaffolding — the indicator stays
open, and Ukraine has not yet supplied it.

## Objection 3 — Two-plus-two is not four: the bloc count is special pleading

**Steelman.** The chapter's own §3 concedes it: two polities pass the
1442 test; the EU fails three layers of four and is kept in the set by
a promise ("complete the settlement or become a client"); India is in
on a conditional. A prediction of "three or four blocs" that survives
whether the EU consolidates, fails, or splits — and whether India
arrives or not — is not a prediction. Choose: if the EU is Tudor, the
capital-intensity criterion is hollow; if it is not, the book predicts
two empires and should say so.

**The chapter's answer.** The set was never defined by present passing
of the 1442 test but by the conjunction of candidacy conditions (ch10
§7: fiscal scale, legibility infrastructure, canonical capacity), and
the chapter's honesty about the EU is the argument, not an escape
hatch: the EU is in the set as the *test case* — the one candidate
whose trajectory discriminates between the book's mechanism and its
rivals. The Depreter refinement makes the prediction concrete rather
than unfalsifiable: apparatus without a continuous political centre
does not consolidate — so the EU either builds the substrate under the
external pressure now applied (ReArm, the 5% pledge, SAFE, CORE — all
dated, all trackable) and validates the mechanism, or fails to and
validates the mechanism's *negative* — becoming the chapter's
predicted canonical province. What the frame forbids is the EU
consolidating *without* fiscal-military substrate — canon alone
sufficing — which is also the outcome its rivals (the regulatory-power
literature) predict. The bloc count is two now, two contested; the
appendix scores it annually; and "two empires" is what the book says
the world looks like if the contested pair fail — it is in §3, not
hidden.

## Objection 4 — The insider's brief: the implications section corrupts the analysis

**Steelman.** The book is written by a strategic-services principal for
insurers, endowments, sovereign funds and family offices, and its
conclusion helpfully discovers that value migrates toward exactly the
positions such clients can buy: licensed, bloc-interior,
procurement-adjacent. The historian's essay ends as a marketing deck;
the analysis is fitted to the audience. Even the restraint ("directional,
never actionable") is the compliance officer's phrasing, not the
scholar's.

**The chapter's answer.** The conflict is real and the book's answer is
structural, not rhetorical: the implications section is derived *after*
and *from* the historical pattern (the dissolution ledger; the
franchise extinctions; the chartered forms), is stated at the level of
pattern, and is fenced by a doctrine rule (§9) the memo quotes — no
instruments, no timing, no allocation. The direction of inference is
auditable in the apparatus: every load-bearing claim in §§1–5 carries
its chapter-of-origin and tier, none originates in the implications
section, and the Appendix C indicators that could falsify the thesis
were fixed (chs. 1–11) before §6 was written. The reader who suspects
the deck can score the wager without buying anything: that is what the
indicators are for. And the coda's method note discloses the
constituency openly — the book's defence against the charge of writing
for its clients is that it tells them, in print, how it could be wrong.

## Objection 5 — The settlement has no date: "one to three decades" is unfalsifiable patience

**Steelman.** Castillon to the Act of Supremacy is eighty years; the
book claims the modern mechanism runs faster but gives itself "one to
three decades," renewable. Any adverse decade can be absorbed as
"transition"; any consolidating event confirms. The pendulum image does
the quiet work: a pendulum always comes back eventually, so the thesis
is safe forever.

**The chapter's answer.** The wager is dated where it can be and staged
where it cannot. The Introduction fixes a fifteen-year horizon for the
four failure conditions (drafted as a discrete numbered passage,
quotable — ch00-intro); Appendix C's indicators are annual series with
sourced baselines, so drift is visible long before any settlement
"arrives"; and several have short fuses the book does not control —
the EU's assertion-to-enforcement lag (C11-1), the frontier-lab
independence axis (C3-2, with a live litigation), the munitions
delivery gap (C10-3). The eighty-year comparison also understates what
the memos actually found: the fifteenth-century sequence from fiscal
grant to standing companies to decisive campaign ran 1439→1445→1449–53
— a *decade* at the mechanism's core, with the constitutional
completion trailing. The book's claim is that the mechanism's core is
observable within the horizon, not that the full constitutional
furniture arrives on schedule; if by the horizon the indicators have
not moved the mechanism's way, the thesis fails on its own published
terms — and the failure conditions are printed in the first chapter
precisely so that patience cannot be pleaded later.
