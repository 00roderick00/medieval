# Research Memo — Ch. 5: The Undermighty Kings

**Chapter weight:** ~11,000 words (Part II, opens "The Crisis"). **Memo
target:** 4–6k. **Function:** establish hollow sovereignty — the crown as a

is a *systemic failure mode* of personalised constitutions under increased
fiscal-military demand, not a run of unfortunate accidents.

## 1. Henry VI, treated straight

**[CONSENSUS]** Henry VI collapsed into a catatonic stupor in **early August
1453** at Clarendon — unresponsive, fed and moved by others, and unable to
recognise his newborn son Prince Edward (born Oct 1453) when presented around
New Year 1454; he recovered around **Christmas 1454**, an incapacity of roughly
eighteen months. The reported trigger was the news of **Castillon** (17 July
1453) and the collapse in Gascony — the *traditional/chronicle* precipitant,
not a proven clinical cause (History Today, T3; British Journal of Psychiatry,
Cambridge Core, T2 — abstract open, full text **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]**).
The loss chronology is the two-stage one of §0.1. Richard of York was appointed
**Protector and Defender of the Realm on 27 March 1454**; on the king's
recovery the first protectorate was terminated (Feb 1455), Somerset freed, and
York's measures reversed (Britannica; World History Encyclopedia, T3).

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The First Battle of St Albans (**22 May 1455**),
conventionally the opening of the Wars of the Roses, came *after* the incapacity
and the failure of the improvised regency — which supports the framing of the
wars as a **symptom** of the sovereignty vacuum, not its cause. The interpretive
claim (kingship crisis, not dynastic ambition, as the driver) is John Watts's
signature thesis and is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Watts, *Henry VI and the
Politics of Kingship*; Griffiths, *The Reign of King Henry VI*]** — the two
scholarly authorities for this section; cite them for the framing, not the
encyclopedias.

## 2. Charles VI in retrospect — the deeper case

**[CONSENSUS]** The earlier and more instructive incapacity. Charles VI's first
psychotic attack came on **5 August 1392** in the forest near Le Mans, where he
killed about four of his own escort; further episodes followed (1393; six in
1399). **The load-bearing point is intermittency** [BOOK'S ARGUMENT]: because
Charles periodically regained authority, no stable formal regency could be
constructed — regents assumed power without formal transfer during attacks,
and rival relatives manoeuvred during lucid intervals. This is why Charles VI
is the *deeper* case than Henry VI: intermittent incapacity generated a
constitutional crisis that total incapacity would have resolved cleanly (World
History Encyclopedia, T3).

The pathological form of the substitute-executive problem was the
**Armagnac–Burgundian civil war** (c. 1407–1435): the assassination of Louis,
Duke of Orléans, by John the Fearless's men (**23 November 1407**) opened the
war; the retaliatory assassination of John the Fearless at Montereau (**10
September 1419**) drove his heir Philip the Good into alliance with England,
producing the **Treaty of Troyes (21 May 1420)** — the Dauphin (later Charles
VII) disinherited, Henry V made heir and regent and married to Catherine of
Valois. [CONSENSUS] (encyclopedia.com, T3.) Scholarly citations **[UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL: Famiglietti, *Royal Intrigue*; Vaughan, *John the Fearless* /
*Philip the Good*]**.

*Note the loop back to ch06:* Troyes is why an infant Henry VI inherited a
double monarchy he could never execute; the crisis Henry inherited was
manufactured by Charles VI's. The chapters interlock — the draft can say so.

## 3. The Ottoman comparison

**[CONSENSUS]** Introduced here as a placeholder; developed in Chapter 11. The
same over-mighty-subject / weak-centre problem, with a *different solution*.
Murad II **abdicated in 1444** in favour of the ~12-year-old Mehmed II (after
the peace of Edirne/Szeged), returned to command at **Varna (10 Nov 1444)**,
and **resumed the throne in 1446** after a Janissary revolt (the Buçuktepe
rebellion) engineered by Grand Vizier Çandarlı Halil Pasha ended Mehmed's first
reign; Mehmed's **second accession** came on Murad's death (**18 Feb 1451**),
leading to the fall of **Constantinople (29 May 1453)** and stabilisation
(EBSCO research starter; worldhistoryedu, T3).

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The **devşirme** — the levy of Christian boys converted
and trained as *kul* ("slaves of the Porte"), forming the Janissaries and the
administrative class — is the Ottoman *solution* to the over-mighty-subject
problem: a loyal service class **manufactured from raw material** rather than
negotiated with an entrenched hereditary nobility. İnalcık's framing (the *kul*
system as "the most potent factor favouring Ottoman unity and centralized
administration") is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: İnalcık, *The Ottoman Empire:
The Classical Age*; Kafadar, *Between Two Worlds*]** — the figures currently
circulate second-hand. The chapter names the devşirme; Chapter 11 develops it
as the most radical of the consolidation variants.

## 4. Hollow sovereignty as a modern condition

The chapter's contemporary payoff, distinguishing two forms (per the outline).

**Territorial hollow sovereignty** (the state cannot control its territory):
- **Somalia** — Fund for Peace Fragile States Index #1 (2024; score ~111.3/120
  — a composite *ranking*, flag); Al-Shabaab controls large rural areas of
  south-central Somalia and runs courts and predictable taxation (EU Agency for
  Asylum country guidance, T2/official; UNODC on terror financing, T1/UN;
  revenue ~$100–150M and territorial "~70%" are **estimates — flag**).
- **Lebanon** — the World Bank's "deliberate depression" (real GDP −19.2% in
  2020), a crisis it calls elite-orchestrated (World Bank, T1/primary);
  Hezbollah as "a state within a state" (Hudson, FDD; and Arab Center's sharper
  "state within a *non*-state," given the government's inability to deliver
  services — cite in balance, T3).
- **Libya** — rival governments (GNU Tripoli vs GNS east, Haftar's LNA); no
  single effective central authority (UN Security Council Report; CFR Global
  Conflict Tracker, T2/T3; the ~1/3–2/3 split is approximate — flag).
- Framing: RAND "ungoverned territories"; Clunan & Trinkunas, *Ungoverned
  Spaces?* — the scholarly reframing to *alternatively* governed spaces (who
  governs, not whether). [CONSENSUS on the concept.]

**Functional hollow sovereignty** (a capable state that cannot execute in
specific domains):
- **US procurement** — GAO (T1/primary): average time to field a major weapon
  system now exceeds 12 years; MDAP delivery slipped from ~8 to ~11 years; the
  Constellation-class frigate delayed 3+ years with construction stalled; Navy
  shipbuilding delays of 12–36 months across programmes ($2.4T is a *planned-
  investment* figure — flag).
- **EU defence** — IISS strategic dossier on Europe's capability gaps and US
  dependence (T2/specialist); the **Draghi report**: 78% of EU defence
  procurement (June 2022–June 2023) went to non-EU suppliers, 63% to the US
  (**cite the Draghi report primary, Sept 2024 — currently via secondary; pin
  before print**).
- **Japan's demographic incapacity** — Statistics Bureau (T1): total population
  123.8M (Oct 2024), the Japanese-national population down 898k in a year; TFR
  a record-low 1.15 (2024); births below 700,000 for the first time (~686k);
  ~30% aged 65+ (**pin TFR/births to MHLW Vital Statistics; dependency ratios
  to OECD/UN before print**).

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Both forms are neomedieval in structure: a legally-
recognised central authority that cannot execute. Cerny's "durable disorder"
(bundled; corpus/bundled/index.md) is the frame — hollow sovereignty as a
Cerny characteristic, not an accident.

## 5. The specific dynamic — over-mighty subjects precede undermighty kings

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The causal direction is the chapter's sharpest claim:
undermighty kings do not *produce* over-mighty subjects; the causation runs the
other way. Over-mighty subjects — magnates whose retinues, fiscal bases and
clienteles rival the crown's — emerge first (this is Chapters 3–4's material,
the affinity and the condottiere), and *then* a weak crown cannot resist them.
The medieval evidence is the Beaufort/York/Neville affinities around the
incapacitated Henry VI and the ducal houses around Charles VI.

The modern analogue [CONTESTED framing, sourced]: continental-scale platform/
tech actors with independent infrastructure and political influence precede,
and now constrain, a federal government that cannot execute against them —
Bremmer's "technopolar" moment (a handful of tech firms rivalling states for
geopolitical influence; *Foreign Affairs*, T3) and Carnegie's "Corporate
Geopolitics: When Billionaires Rival States" (firms as "digital leviathans"
performing functions "that increasingly mirror those of states," including
"core military functions—command and control, reconnaissance, analytics";
Csernatoni, Carnegie, 2025, T3). **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** This is the structural
parallel Part III will need — the over-mighty subject of our age is the
platform, and the undermighty king is the state that let it grow. The disanalogy
must be stated (a magnate's retinue is not a data centre); flag the analogy
joint [BRIDGE].
