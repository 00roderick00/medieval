# Critiques — Ch. 5: The Undermighty Kings

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 were surfaced in
research. Each is steelmanned before the chapter answers it (§6). Perplexity run
logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The medical-specificity complaint (the named objection)

**Steelman.** Henry VI's incapacity and Charles VI's were different conditions,
and comparing them as instances of one thing — "hollow sovereignty" — flattens
both medical and political history. Henry VI suffered a single sustained
catatonic stupor (roughly eighteen months, total withdrawal); Charles VI
suffered recurrent psychotic episodes with lucid intervals over thirty years.
These are clinically distinct (a depressive/catatonic breakdown vs a recurrent
psychotic disorder), and their politics were distinct too: Henry's England
produced a protectorate and then civil war; Charles's France produced a
factional regency and a civil war of a different shape. To fold them into a
single "systemic failure mode" is to let a slogan do the work that careful
comparison should — and to imply a determinism (personal monarchy → collapse)
that the varied outcomes do not support.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the medical distinction openly — indeed *use*
it. The comparison is explicitly **not** at the level of medical etiology but of
**institutional response**: the failure of regency mechanisms, the emergence of
factional civil war, the erosion of central fiscal capacity. That the two
incapacities were clinically different and still produced structurally similar
institutional crises is the argument, not an embarrassment to it: it shows the
failure mode is a property of the *personalised constitution* under load, not of
any particular illness. This is the outline's conceded disanalogy (CLAUDE.md
§6), and the draft must preserve it — stating the clinical difference *first*,
then the institutional convergence. Indeed Charles VI is the *stronger* case
precisely because his intermittency (not his diagnosis) is what prevented a
stable regency: the mechanism is constitutional, not clinical. The determinism
charge is answered by the book's standing calibration — the claim is
conditional (personalised constitutions fail *under increased fiscal-military
demand*), not that every mad king brings ruin.

## Objection 2 — The modern "hollow sovereignty" cases are heterogeneous

**Steelman.** Somalia, Lebanon, Libya, US procurement dysfunction, EU defence
dependence, and Japanese demography are wildly different phenomena — state
collapse, elite-engineered financial ruin, civil war, bureaucratic
inefficiency, alliance politics, and a birth rate. Calling all six "hollow
sovereignty" is a category so broad it explains nothing; a failing frigate
programme is not Mogadishu. The chapter smuggles a rhetorical unity over an
analytic grab-bag.

**The chapter's answer.** The chapter *already* draws the distinction the
objection demands — between **territorial** hollow sovereignty (the state cannot
control its territory: Somalia, Libya, parts of Lebanon) and **functional**
hollow sovereignty (a capable state that cannot execute in a domain: US
procurement, EU defence, Japanese demographic constraint). The unifying claim is
precise and falsifiable: in each, a legally-recognised central authority
*cannot execute a function it nominally holds*. That is not "everything is
hollow sovereignty"; it is a specific structural predicate that some polities
and some domains satisfy and others do not (a functioning defence procurement,
a state that controls its capital, do not). The draft must keep the two forms
labelled and resist letting the vivid territorial cases (Mogadishu) borrow
gravity for the functional ones (the frigate) — they are analogous in structure,
not in severity, and the chapter says so.

## Objection 3 — Anachronism: platforms are not magnates

**Steelman.** The claim that "over-mighty subjects precede undermighty kings,"
carried to the present as "platform actors precede the weak state," equivocates.
A fifteenth-century magnate commanded armed retainers who could and did fight
the crown's forces; a technology firm commands data centres, code, and market
share. The former is a rival to the state's monopoly of violence; the latter is
a rival to nothing the state ultimately controls (the state can regulate, tax,
or break it, as the Chinese case shows). Reading Meta as the Duke of York is a
literary flourish, not an analysis.

**The chapter's answer.** State the disanalogy in the text (a retinue is not a
data centre; a firm holds no lances) and rest the parallel on the *structural*
predicate the book actually needs: a concentration of capability, independent
fiscal base, and clientele that a central authority cannot at present
*effectively* discipline. The chapter does not claim platforms wage war; it
claims they occupy the position of the over-mighty subject — power that grew
inside a weakening centre and now constrains it — and it uses reputable analysis
(Bremmer's "technopolar," Carnegie's "corporate geopolitics," which explicitly
notes firms performing "command and control, reconnaissance, analytics") to
show the point is not the book's invention. Crucially, the objection's own
example — China *breaking* its platforms (Chapter 2's crackdown) — is the book's
Part III thesis, not a refutation: the over-mighty subject can be
re-subordinated, and the book predicts it will be. The parallel is licensed as
structure, flagged [BRIDGE] at the analogy joint, and any passage that leans on
resemblance rather than the structural predicate is cut (§2).

## Objection 4 — Selecting the fragmented cases (the ch01 flattening objection, redux)

**Steelman.** As with the c. 1200 baseline (Chapter 1), the chapter selects the
mid-fifteenth century's *failures* — Henry VI, Charles VI, the Ottoman
succession crises — and calls hollow sovereignty "the modal condition of
European monarchy." But the same decades contain counter-cases: Charles VII's
France was *recovering* central capacity precisely as Henry VI's England lost
it (Chapter 6's whole point); Portugal was stable; Aragon was expanding. "Modal"
is doing unearned work.

**The chapter's answer.** Narrow the claim as Chapter 1 does: hollow sovereignty
was the *modal condition of the personalised monarchies under acute
fiscal-military strain in the second quarter of the fifteenth century* — not of
every polity everywhere. The recovering France is not a counter-case but the
**pivot**: the chapter's purpose is to establish the crisis that the artillery-
state response (Chapters 10–11) resolves, and Charles VII's France is where the
resolution begins. The draft should name France explicitly as the polity moving
the other way, so the reader sees the crisis and its cure in the same frame,
rather than claiming a false universality of collapse.

## Objection 5 — The 1453 "triple hinge" is coincidence dressed as pattern

**Steelman.** Castillon (July), Henry VI's collapse (August), and the fall of
Constantinople (May) all falling in 1453 is a striking coincidence — but a
coincidence. Their causes are unrelated (a French artillery victory, a
psychiatric event, an Ottoman siege). Presenting them as a single "year the
order broke" is exactly the atmospheric, pattern-seeking history the book
elsewhere disavows.

**The chapter's answer.** Use the resonance for narrative framing only, and say
so — the three events are causally independent and the draft must not imply
otherwise. What they *do* legitimately share is that each exposed a personalised
sovereign order failing to execute under pressure — one to artillery, one to
incapacity, one to a siege train (Constantinople fell partly to Ottoman
bombardment, linking it to Chapter 6's mechanism). The 1453 convergence is a
[BRIDGE] device, Roderick's to set, and it earns its place as illustration, not
as evidence. If the disanalogy of causes cannot be held clearly, the triple-
hinge framing should be cut rather than allowed to imply a hidden unity.
