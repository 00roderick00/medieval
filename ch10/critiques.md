# Critiques — Ch. 10: The Artillery State

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test (the techno-optimist's
private-financing complaint). Objections 2–5 surfaced in research — two of
them (Curry's reversed causation; the Hale–DeVries dissent) from sources
now in the repo, quoted on their own terms. Each steelmanned before the
answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The techno-optimist complaint (named): the modern stack is privately financed

**Steelman.** The fifteenth-century parallel fails at its load-bearing
joint. The Bureau train was paid for by the taille; there was no private
siege-train industry. The modern stack is the opposite: TSMC, ASML,
Nvidia, SpaceX and the hyperscalers are private firms financed by
revenues and capital markets; the frontier AI labs raise more in a
single private round than the CHIPS Act disbursed to any firm; Starlink
was built on commercial broadband economics, not appropriations. Where
Charles VII taxed a kingdom to buy guns no one else could afford, the
modern state *subsidises at the margin* an industry that exists without
it. The capital-intensity claim may hold; the *fiscal-military* claim —
permanent public finance as the precondition of decisive capability —
does not, and with it goes the mechanism.

**The chapter's answer — from the legislation itself, with one
concession.** The chapter's §7 distinguishes the civilian stack from the
military-relevant stack and documents that the latter is publicly
financed at permanent-appropriation scale, from primary instruments: the
CHIPS appropriation ($52.7bn, with §103 guardrails converting subsidy
into jurisdiction — a ten-year expansion clawback is not industrial
policy, it is enclosure); the Secure Enclave programme (up to $3.5bn of
the $39bn walled off for DoD supply); the August 2025 conversion of
Intel's grants into a ~10% federal equity stake — the paymaster becoming
owner; the NRO's $1.8bn Starshield constellation and SDA's tranche
awards (the state buying its own proliferated LEO layer rather than
renting Starlink's); El Capitan's NNSA mission statement; the Big Fund's
RMB 687bn across three phases in a party-state where the
public/private boundary is definitionally porous; and the munitions
base, which no private demand sustains at all. Ukraine-era procurement
shows the direction of dependence: commercial capability becomes
decisive only when the state contracts, clears, and (increasingly)
co-owns it. **The concession, stated plainly:** hyperscale AI training
compute is today majority-privately financed — the one layer where the
objection bites. The chapter does not claim otherwise; it flags the
frontier labs as the standing anomaly (ch03's proto-condottiere case,
tracked at C3-2/C2-3) and notes that the state's response — clearances,
Secure Enclave-style enclaves, equity — is precisely the absorption
pattern the book predicts. If that layer's financing *stays* private and
its capability *stays* decisive and unabsorbed, the thesis loses a limb;
Appendix C carries the indicator.

## Objection 2 — Curry's reversed arrow: strong states produce military revolutions, not the reverse

**Steelman.** The best recent specialist on exactly this campaign
concludes against the chapter's causal direction. Curry (in repo, pp.
187–88): "Behind the English success in earlier decades, and the French
success in 1449–50, was political will and also resources… A military
revolution needs a strong, well resourced government to produce it.
Historians therefore need to look at the prerequisites rather than the
effects… These condition the kinds of armies and weapons which states
can deploy, **not the other way around**." The chronology agrees with
her: the taille grant (1439) and the companies (1445) predate the
Normandy campaign (1449–50) and Castillon (1453). The fisc built the
gun; the chapter has the gun building the fisc. On this reading the
book's mechanism chapter has its arrow backwards, and the whole
predictive apparatus of Part III inherits the error.

**The chapter's answer.** Accept the chronology — the chapter states it
itself (§6) — and press the objection to its own next sentence. Curry
continues: "The artillery revolution was even more the product of such
control and resource since, unlike provision of troops, **it could not
be provided by any other organisation than the state, or at least not at
the level required to make an impact**" (p. 188). That sentence is the
chapter's threshold claim, conceded inside the objection: infantry could
be (and was) raised by magnates, cities, and cantons; a first-rate siege
train could be held by *no one* below the crown-scale fisc. The
chapter's formulation is a loop with a threshold step, not a
one-directional arrow: pre-existing fiscal capacity built the train
(1439→1445→1449); the train then made that capacity decisive and the
loop irreversible — suppressing the intermediate authorities whose
existence had previously capped the fisc (Rogers's spiral, in a
sceptic's mouth at DeVries p. 128). This is ch06 reviewed decision 4
extended: the debate with Rogers was over *ranking* revolutions, the
debate with Curry is over *sequencing within an agreed loop* — a quarrel
over tempo and agency inside an agreed direction of travel, staged as
the material rather than the obstacle (the ch09 Objection 1 discipline).
And Curry's own p. 188 sequel — "it was not until there was permanent
taxation… that there was the real development of standing armies" — is
the chapter's §2 in her words.

## Objection 3 — The Hale–DeVries dissent: the artillery-centralisation case is "feeble," and England disproves it

**Steelman.** Hale, quoted approvingly by DeVries (p. 129, in repo):
"The case for the suggestion that artillery was an instrument
centralizing power is… feeble. Governments… insisted on making the
manufacture of artillery a monopoly. But though an occasional rebellious
magnate may have been brought to heel by royal cannon (but never just
because of cannon) the complex shifts towards more effectively
centralized forms of government began before cannon were effective or
readily transportable and can be explained without reference to
gunpowder weapons." DeVries's own case study then delivers the
counter-example the thesis cannot digest: England, where the crown held
an effectively absolute gun monopoly from Edward III — a century of
exactly the technological centralisation the chapter prizes — and
nevertheless collapsed into fifty years of civil war in which subjects
shot at each other with their own artillery. If artillery centralised,
England should have been the most centralised polity in Europe. It was
the least stable.

**The chapter's answer — the dissent's own conclusion, then the
exception's own logic.** First, the concession within the dissent:
DeVries's article concludes that "France and Burgundy **do follow a
pattern showing the decline of feudalism and the rise of the central
state which conforms to that proposed by Geoffrey Parker and others**.
Local control and ownership of these weapons was removed by the central
state, which would later return to use the weapons against those who had
once owned them" (p. 145). Two of his three cases conform; the quarrel
is over the third. Second, the third case proves the coupling, not the
counter-claim. The chapter's mechanism was never "guns centralise" — it
is a *conjunction*: capital-intensive decisive force **plus** the
fiscal-administrative capacity to sustain it. England had the monopoly
without the substrate: no taille, no standing companies, a crown living
off diminished ordinary revenue and one-off grants (Curry p. 188 on
permanent taxation arriving only under Henry VIII), and after 1435 a
collapsing French revenue base. Under dynastic crisis the monopoly
dissolved — "not only had he lost control over gunpowder holdings in his
kingdom, he had in fact lost his kingdom" (p. 145) — which is ch05's
hollow sovereignty told in ordnance. Where the substrate existed
(France), one battle-death of the *dynast* (Nancy) transferred the
apparatus intact to the crown and the Habsburgs; where it did not
(England), the apparatus itself dissolved. The exception varies the
independent variable and gets the predicted result. Hale's residual
point — centralisation "began before cannon" — is absorbed by the
loop-with-threshold answer to Objection 2.

## Objection 4 — The Swiss objection: cheap distributed force beat the artillery state, and its heirs are watching

**Steelman.** Grandson, Morat, Nancy: in ten months, peasant and burgher
pike squares — cheap to raise, cheap to sustain, no arsenal, no
technical service, no permanent tax — destroyed the best
combined-arms army in Latin Europe and killed its prince. The
"anti-determinism inoculation" cannot be quarantined to one chapter
section: it is the whole case. If disciplined mass infantry could beat
the capital-intensive state at the height of the artillery revolution,
then the modern equivalents — the FPV drone, the loitering munition, the
$500 quadcopter against the $10M vehicle — can beat the compute-foundry-
satellite state now, and Part III's prediction fails on the book's own
fifteenth-century evidence.

**The chapter's answer.** The Swiss are in the chapter *because* the
objection is real, and the answer is the outcome the objection skips:
the Swiss won the battles and **built no state** — near-dissolution over
the spoils at Stans (1481), no permanent taxation, no standing army, and
a subsequent business model of renting the instrument to the fiscal
states (the pension and capitulation economy; the first formal French
troop convention, 1521). Pike squares forced no institution-building on
the polity that fielded them; siege trains did. Decisive-force
technology consolidates sovereignty only through the fiscal and
administrative institutions it *compels* — which is why the Swiss
victories rearranged dynasties (Burgundy's apparatus passed to France
and the Habsburgs) without producing a Swiss consolidation. The modern
translation is ch12's asymmetry, foreshadowed here with the discipline
stated: the cheap-precision layer raises the price of aggression and
distributes tactical denial (Ukraine as the demonstration), but it
builds no bloc-scale industrial fisc; the decisive layer — the one whose
absence cannot be improvised — still does. Two honesty notes carried
from the memo: the battlefield mechanism at Grandson/Morat was speed and
surprise, not pikes-beat-guns in the abstract (the traverse/reload
mechanism is unsourced inference pending Depreter/Vaughan); and the
Swiss *did* win a form of durable independence — the claim is about
state-form, not about freedom. The chapter must not smooth either point.

## Objection 5 — The four-polity claim is the book's own construction, and the EU falsifies it from inside

**Steelman.** No scholarly or governmental source makes the "only three
or four polities can hold the full stack" claim; the memo admits it is
the book's synthesis. Worse, the chapter's own evidence undermines its
most awkward member: the EU's flagship fab subsidy (Intel Magdeburg,
€10bn) was cancelled in 2025; its sovereign constellation starts
services around 2030; its ammunition programme counts capacity, not
output; the Commission directly controls ~€4.5bn of its "€43bn" chips
programme. By the chapter's own 1442 test — decisive capability as a
permanent line in the central fisc — the EU fails three of four layers.
A category with one clear member (the US), one sui generis party-state
(China), one aspirant (India) and one failure (the EU) is not a
category; it is a rhetorical rounding-up.

**The chapter's answer.** The provenance is conceded by design: §0.6 and
§7 tag the four-polity claim (c), the book's own, assembled from
(a)-grade components — that is the doctrine's (a)/(c) separation working,
not a defect. The EU's failures are printed in the chapter, not
smoothed: Magdeburg, IRIS²'s timeline, and the capacity/output
distinction are all in §7 as adverse data. And the outline already
holds the answer structurally: the EU enters the four not as a military
artillery state but as the *canonical* special case — Tudor in one move
of five (ch02's regulatory church; ch11 §7; ch12's "structurally
unstable — it will either develop the fiscal-military and administrative
capacity to complete the settlement or be reduced to a client of the US
bloc"). The chapter's honest formulation is two-plus-two: two polities
pass the 1442 test today; one asserts by canon what it cannot yet build
by fisc; one is assembling the legibility stack ahead of the military
one. Falsifiability is preserved and operationalised: the membership
count and its direction are Appendix C's C6-1/C10 indicators, and the
EU's trajectory is itself one of the book's named tests, not an
embarrassment to be managed.
