exceeded here by design: this is the book's central causal chapter, and the
mechanism's evidentiary chain (institutions, figures, counter-cases, and
the modern translation) demands the length. **Function:** establish the
fiscal-military state as the *response* to the artillery revolution —
the *compagnies d'ordonnance*, the *taille*, the Bureau service as
institution, Louis XI as the mature exemplar — with the Burgundian
counter-case at full weight, the chain compressed
(technology → finance → sovereignty), and the modern sketch of the four
candidate polities.

closing sentence — the artillery revolution "priced sovereignty, and only
those who could meet the price survived as sovereigns" — and supplies the
institutional and fiscal machinery behind the price.

**Comparative register:** primary polity France; comparators Burgundy and
the Swiss Confederation (with England as DeVries's control case,

## 0. Provenance summary — read first

1. **The founding ordonnance is mis-dated in the popular record and — the
   deeper finding — the founding text is lost.** The verifiable date and
   place are **26 May 1445, Louppy-le-Châtel** (near Bar-le-Duc), not "28
   May" and not Nancy (the court was merely in Lorraine that spring). More
   important: the original ordonnance does not survive; its content is
   reconstructed from the Louppy-le-Châtel text plus the chroniclers
   (Thomas Basin, Mathieu d'Escouchy) — the subject of a 2015 *Revue
   historique* article on the "ordonnance perdue" (gated; retrieval). The
   draft should treat "26 May 1445, Louppy-le-Châtel" as the documentary
   anchor of a reform whose founding text does not survive — a nice
   discipline point for a book about administrative capacity: the
   paperwork of Europe's founding standing army is itself missing.
   **[CONSENSUS on date/place at T3/T4 convergence; the lost-text point
   T2.]**

2. **"Europe's first standing army since Rome" needs calibration.** Two
   caveats are fatal to the flat version: Charles V's 1373 force is
   called "the first standing army in post-Roman western Europe" in the
   tertiary record (it was disbanded); and the Ottoman janissaries
   predate 1445 by two generations (formed under Murad I, c. 1380). No
   open scholarly source states the "first since Rome" claim flatly. Use:
   **"the first permanent, regularly paid royal army in Latin
   Christendom"** — with the janissary caveat stated, which the book
   *wants* anyway (the Ottoman comparator is ch11's most radical
   variant). **[Correction to the outline's phrasing; flag for
   Roderick.]**

3. **"The taille became permanent in 1439" is a tertiary compression; the
   scholarly phrasing is gradualist.** The 1439 Estates at Orléans
   granted the tax and the troop monopoly (ordonnance of 2 November
   1439); permanence was a *practice* — Charles VII "continued these
   taxes through annual ordinances no longer sanctioned by the Estates,"
   and annual budgeting "gave way in 1450 to a single 'general
   statement' of finance, which… effectively institutionalized taxation
   in France" (Britannica formulation, T3). Two-step framing: 1439 the
   grant, the 1440s the practice, c. 1450 the institution. This
   *strengthens* the chapter's sequence argument (§6). **[CONSENSUS,
   gradualist form.]**

4. **Figures corrected or flagged.** (a) Revenue: the only openly
   citable arc is **~1.8M livres at the end of Charles VII's reign →
   4.6M from the taille alone in 1481** (1911 Encyclopaedia Britannica,
   verbatim; current Britannica: "about 4.5 million"); the "~1.2M under
   Charles VII" circulates only in gated scholarship. All revenue figures
   are tertiary pending Wolfe/Contamine. (b) The Paris **Bastille
   arsenal** claim in the outline is unverifiable for the fifteenth
   century (the Arsenal complex is Francis I's); cut or hedge. (c)
   **Standardised calibers**: formal French caliber standardisation is
   Francis I (c. 1525, six sizes) and Henry II (the *six calibres de
   France*); what belongs to our period is Bureau-era rationalisation
   (ch06 decision 2) and the 1494 mobile bronze train. (d) The **1464
   postal relay edict** ("édit de Luxies") is of disputed authenticity;
   drop or write "traditionally dated 1464, disputed." (e)
   **"L'universelle aragne"** is not a neutral contemporary epithet: the
   spider image is Georges Chastelain's (1467 ballade), the formula
   Jean Molinet's — **hostile Burgundian propaganda**, generalised as a
   nickname by later historians (T4 + gated T2 abstract). The draft
   should use it *as* what it is — the enemy's name for the fiscal
   state's operator — which is better material than the legend.

5. **The sceptics concede the mechanism — from three directions.** This
   is the memo's most valuable result, extending ch06 §7. (i) **Rogers**
   (in repo): the self-reinforcing artillery→tax→centralisation cycle,
   quoted at DeVries p. 128. (ii) **DeVries himself** — the
   anti-determinist — investigating political control of guns in France,
   Burgundy and England, concludes: "**France and Burgundy do follow a
   pattern showing the decline of feudalism and the rise of the central
   state which conforms to that proposed by Geoffrey Parker and others.
   Local control and ownership of these weapons was removed by the
   central state, which would later return to use the weapons against
   those who had once owned them**" (DeVries 1998, p. 145, in repo). His
   England exception is the book's own ch05 case (see §5b). (iii)
   **Curry** — the gradualist — reverses the causal arrow ("A military
   revolution needs a strong, well resourced government to produce it…
   These condition the kinds of armies and weapons which states can
   deploy, not the other way around," pp. 187–88, in repo) yet concedes
   in the same breath that the artillery revolution "**could not be
   provided by any other organisation than the state, or at least not at
   the level required to make an impact**" — the fiscal-threshold claim
   inside the objection. Both engagements are staged in critiques.md
   (Objections 2–3). And a fourth direction, from the book's *own*
   theoretical spine: **Cerny 1998** (in repo) locates the original
   consolidation in "the creative destruction of fifteenth to
   seventeenth century warfare" (p. 51) and names "the inter-dynastic
   struggles of the Hundred Years' War" as the exogenous consolidating
   pressure now absent (p. 52) — the neomedieval literature's founder
   attesting the mechanism the book runs.

6. **The four-polity claim is the book's own.** No located source makes
   the "only three or four polities can hold the full stack" argument as
   such (closest: CSET's chokepoints work; SIA/BCG's concentration data;
   Miller's *Chip War*, gated). §7 therefore tags it **(c) [BOOK'S
   ARGUMENT]** and builds it from primary-documented components. Per §9
   doctrine, this is exactly the (a)/(c) separation the memo exists to
   enforce.

## 1. The *compagnies d'ordonnance*, 1445

**The reform. [CONSENSUS core]** By the ordonnance anchored at
Louppy-le-Châtel, 26 May 1445 (§0.1): **fifteen companies of one hundred
lances**, permanently retained, salaried by the crown, quartered in
garrison towns under crown discipline, mustered and inspected —
*la grande ordonnance*, ~9,000 men of whom ~6,000 combatants. The lance:
the sources give variant compositions (man-at-arms + coutilier + page +
two mounted archers + valet = six men, six horses is the standard
scholarly formula — Contamine's; variants circulate). Pay rates,
quartering detail and the 1446 Languedoc extension (a royal act of 25
April 1446 referring to 1,500 lances for Languedoïl, 500 for Languedoc)
are carried only at T4 pending Contamine. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
Contamine, *Guerre, état et société*, for lance composition, pay,
quartering, the 1446 figures.]** Later growth: to a peak of 58 companies
/ 4,000 lances / ~24,000 men by 1483 (T4 — flag). The infantry parallel:
the **francs-archers**, ordonnance of Montil-lès-Tours, **28 April
1448** — one archer per parish, tax-exempt, Sunday practice (T2
confirmation of the date via Persée; performance poor, replaced by paid
professionals 1480–81). Disbandable only by the crown; disbanded by
nobody.

**The scholarly anchor now in repo. [T2]** DeVries p. 132: Charles VII
"developed a strong programme of gunpowder weapon acquisition and
development… **increased the royal budget to procure a larger number of
new guns, and he added heavier new taxes to his kingdom's inhabitants to
pay for this increase**." Army and artillery are the same fiscal event.

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The company is the artillery train's organisational
twin: both are standing, salaried, crown-monopolised capabilities that
cannot be raised by summons and cannot be sustained by plunder. The
chapter's definition of the artillery state is precisely this pair plus
the tax that pays for them.

## 2. The *taille* made permanent

**The grant. [CONSENSUS]** Estates General, Orléans; ordonnance of **2
November 1439**: the king alone raises troops, names captains, and
levies the taille for their pay. The political test came immediately:
the **Praguerie** (February–July 1440) — Bourbon and Alençon with the
sixteen-year-old dauphin Louis as figurehead, against precisely these
military-fiscal reforms; "outgeneraled" by Richemont, settled at Cusset
on generous terms that conceded nothing structural. The reform survived
its civil war within eighteen months of enactment. **[T3]**

**Permanence as practice. [CONSENSUS, gradualist — per §0.3]** Annual
ordinances without Estates' sanction through the 1440s; the single
"general statement" of finance from c. 1450. The machinery: the *élus*
and *élections* (inherited from the 1350s) subordinated under **four
généralités** (six by 1484), with *receveurs* below. **[Structure at
T3/T4; detail UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Wolfe, *The Fiscal System of
Renaissance France*.]**

**The yield arc. [Tertiary — flag]** ~1.8M livres at the end of Charles
VII's reign; **"In 1481 the taille alone brought in 4,600,000 livres"**
(1911 EB, verbatim) — a rough tripling under Louis XI. Then the control
experiment: the **Estates of Tours, 1484**, granted the taille for two
years only and cut it to ~1.5M — and the monarchy's machine absorbed the
cut and outlived the Estates' assertiveness; no further Estates General
met until 1560. **[T4 for the 1484 figures — flag; the political point
CONSENSUS.]** Comparative fiscal series for England/Burgundy/Venice/
Ottomans c. 1450–1500 are not openly available in citable form —
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Bonney (ed.), *Rise of the Fiscal State*;
Ormrod et al.]**; Karaman & Pamuk (open, T2) supply the c. 1500 baseline
(Ottoman revenue "greater than all European states except France and
Spain" in the sixteenth century).

**Historiography marker. [CONSENSUS]** "Fiscal-military state" is
Brewer's coinage (*The Sinews of Power*, 1989), for eighteenth-century
Britain; extended backward to 1500+ by Glete. No open scholarship applies
it to Charles VII's France — the period-native frame is Contamine's
*l'État de guerre*. The draft says openly that the book retrojects
Brewer's term, with Contamine as the period authority. **[Tag (c) for
the retrojection.]**

## 3. The Bureau service as institution

**The office and the men. [CONSENSUS core; detail tertiary]** Jean
Bureau in royal service from 1434, master of the artillery by 1439,
simultaneously a crown financial officer (ch06); Gaspard grand-master of
the king's artillery from **27 December 1444 — succeeding Pierre
Bessonneau**: the office pre-existed the brothers. Per ch06's reviewed
decision 2, the Bureaux are drafted as *institutionalisers* — DeVries p.
132 (T2, in repo) is now the anchor: "perhaps the most important feature
of Charles VII's gunpowder artillery train was its **intricate
organization and superior leadership**. Under masters of artillery Jean
and Gaspard Bureau, the French artillery holdings grew in number and
efficiency. **Duties of cannoneers were established, officers were
appointed, competence was improved and pay was increased.**" (Note
DeVries's slip: he dates Castillon "1452"; it is 17 July 1453 — cite the
substance, not the date, from that sentence.)

**The fiscal signature. [T2, in repo]** Rogers 1993: by 1442 the French
government "was spending more than twice as much on its artillery train
as it spent on more traditional war materiel" — the figure ch06 §7
established and this chapter re-deploys as the budget line that priced
sovereignty. Continuity: after Charles VII "the French artillery
holdings grew consistently larger, improving both under Louis XI… and
under Charles VIII, who used them to invade and easily conquer Italy in
1494" (DeVries p. 133). The open source to mine for Louis XI/Charles
VIII organisation and finance is **Contamine's 1964 Persée article**
("L'artillerie royale française à la veille des guerres d'Italie" —
Giovio on the 1494 train entering Rome, 36+ pieces moving at cavalry
speed); a 1479 ordinance assigning 220,000 livres for artillery
transport and the four artillery *bandes* of 1469 circulate at T4 —
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL/PARSE: full read of Contamine 1964 and Perroy
1943 (both open on Persée but not yet machine-read beyond p. 1).]**

**What not to say.** No Bastille arsenal (§0.4b); no "seven calibers of
Charles VIII" (§0.4c); no Bureau *inventions* (ch06 decision 2).

## 4. Louis XI as the exemplar

**The stress test survived. [CONSENSUS]** The **War of the Public Weal**
(League formed March 1465; Montlhéry 16 July 1465 indecisive; Treaties
of Conflans/Saint-Maur, October 1465, ceding Normandy to Berry and the
Somme towns to Charolais) — and then the claw-back: Louis declared the
treaties "null and void, having been signed under duress" and retook
Normandy within months. The princes could still win concessions in the
field; they could no longer hold them against the machine. A 2024
open-access *EHR* article ("Towns and Princely Rebellion in
Fifteenth-Century France," T2) now anchors the urban dimension: the
towns held for the crown. DeVries p. 137 adds the artillery texture
(in repo): at Montlhéry the Burgundian guns "unable to fire more than
ten salvos"; the Paris duel of August–September 1465 "may have been the
most fearsome artillery duel of the century," with Commynes's eyewitness
line — "I have never seen so much shooting in such a few days."

**The absorption of Burgundy. [CONSENSUS]** Nancy, 5 January 1477 (§5);
Louis seized the duchy of Burgundy and Picardy; **Treaty of Arras,
1482**: full sovereignty over the Duchy of Burgundy, Picardy and the
Boulonnais, with Franche-Comté and Artois as Margaret of Austria's dowry
to the future Charles VIII. The appanage system more broadly: Anjou,
Maine and Provence to the crown by 1481 (Charles IV of Maine naming
Louis heir the day before he died, 11 December 1481; the "Anjou 1471"
variant in Britannica — flag the date variance, pin to a biography).
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Kendall, *Louis XI*; Favier.]**

**The judgement of the age — T1, verbatim, now in hand.** Commynes
(Scoble translation, open at Gutenberg #77950; Book I, ch. X): "of all
the princes that I ever knew, **the wisest and most dexterous to
extricate himself out of any danger or difficulty in time of adversity,
was our master King Louis XI**." And the enemy's name for him: the
spider — Chastelain's image, Molinet's "l'universelle aragne" (§0.4e) —
Burgundian propaganda that the chapter can quote *as* the view from the
losing side of the fiscal-military threshold.

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Louis XI's France is the first polity in the
chapter's story where the crown is systematically stronger than any
subject *and the difference is a budget line*: the tripled taille, the
standing companies, the enlarged train. The Public Weal is the
over-mighty subjects' last systemic bid (cross-ref ch05), and it fails
not at a battle but at the exchequer.

## 5. The counter-cases

### 5a. Burgundy: superb artillery, lost anyway

**The apparatus. [CONSENSUS core]** Charles the Bold's ordinances —
Abbeville, 31 July 1471 (1,200 nine-man lances, regulated "training,
discipline, equipment and pay"); refined at Bohain-en-Vermandois (1472)
and **St-Maximin outside Trier (1473** — the famous drill ordinance,
often labelled "Thionville" in older literature; squadron structure,
uniforms, roll-calls, punishments); Lausanne (1476). Heimpel's verdict,
via Bachrach's open article: the reforms were "the victory of the values
of the state against the values of chivalry." The artillery: Commynes
calls it "very large and powerful," "good and beautiful"; La Marche
counts "more than 300 carts of guns, not counting his culverins or
'haquebusses' which were 'without number'"; at Neuss an eyewitness saw
culverins fired "thicker than rain" (all DeVries p. 138, T2 in repo).
Burgundy was, on paper, the best-organised army in Latin Europe.
**[Ordinance labelling to pin — UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Vaughan,
*Charles the Bold*.]**

**The defeats. [CONSENSUS on events; figures flagged]** **Grandson, 2
March 1476**: the Swiss advance's speed "did not give the Burgundians
time to make much use of their artillery," and a repositioning manoeuvre
collapsed into rout; the Swiss took the camp — the treasury, the
diamonds, and by tradition **~400 guns** (the museum/tradition figure;
the critical archival count is in Depreter — flag before printing a
number). Casualties were small on both sides (~300–400 each). **Morat,
22 June 1476**: operational surprise against a divided, unprepared army;
Burgundian dead somewhere between 6,000 and 12,000 — "perhaps a third of
his army," with the range stated. **Nancy, 5 January 1477**: Charles
killed; the body found days later, stripped, frozen, split by a halberd,
part-eaten by wolves, identified by scars and long fingernails.
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Depreter (the 2011 Persée article is open —
mine it; his monograph gated) and Vaughan for gun counts and
casualties.]**

**Why the Swiss won — honesty about the mechanism.** What the sources
actually support: *deployment speed* (Grandson), *a misread manoeuvre
becoming panic* (Grandson), *operational surprise against an undeployed
army* (Morat). The intuitive gun-side mechanism (slow traverse, slow
reload against fast pike columns) is **inference, not sourced** — if the
draft states it, hang it on Depreter/Vaughan after retrieval or cut it.
**[Flag.]**

**The deeper lesson — the anti-determinism inoculation, exactly as the
brief directs. [BOOK'S ARGUMENT, resting on sourced components]** Cheap,
disciplined, distributed force beat the best capital-intensive army in
Europe three times in ten months. And then: no Swiss consolidation. The
Confederation nearly broke apart over the spoils and the admission of
Fribourg and Solothurn — saved at **Stans, December 1481** by Nicholas
of Flüe's mediation (the *Stanser Verkommnis*) — and remained a league:
no permanent central taxation, no standing army, no chancery state. Its
military excellence was monetised instead as *export*: the pension and
capitulation economy (regular foreign payments to cantonal authorities
from the late fifteenth century; the first formal Franco-Swiss troop
convention 1521). The Swiss won the battles and sold the instrument;
the fiscal states bought it. Victory in the field produced no
sovereignty-consolidation because pike squares — unlike siege trains —
required no permanent fisc, no arsenal, no technical service, and so
*forced no institution-building on the polity that fielded them*. The
lesson the chapter draws: decisive military technology matters through
the institutions it compels, not through the battles it wins. **[The
synthesis is the book's own (c); components: Stans 1481 (T3),
pensions/capitulations (T4/T2-adjacent, Oxford fiscal-military project),
1521 convention (T2-adjacent).]**

### 5b. England: the control case from the sceptic's own article

**[T2, in repo — new to the argument since the outline.]** DeVries's
England (pp. 139–45) is the second counter-case, and it is a gift:
England had *early and exclusive royal control* of gunpowder weapons
(Edward III through Henry V — "the king alone had control… and he used
them in an almost absolute manner," p. 145) — and then, in the
mid-fifteenth-century crisis, lost it: royal artillery control collapsed
in the Wars of the Roses; private artillery war among subjects (Caister,
Nibley Green, Hornby — cross-ref ch08 §1); "after almost fifty years of
civil war not only had he lost control over gunpowder holdings in his
kingdom, he had in fact lost his kingdom" (p. 145). England shows the
mechanism by *failure of its precondition*: royal gun monopoly without
the fiscal-administrative substrate (ch05's hollow sovereignty) did not
survive contact with dynastic crisis — and the recovery of both, under
the Tudors, is ch11's opening. The exception does not weaken the
coupling; it demonstrates it from the other side.

## 6. Technology → finance → sovereignty: the chain compressed

**The chain, in the sceptics' words. [CONSENSUS on components]** Porter:
"artillery was generally too expensive for the nobility to purchase, and
hence tended to become a monopoly of the Crown. The superior military
technology of the day both gravitated to and reinforced the political
center" (quoted at DeVries p. 128). Rogers: the "self-reinforcing
cycle" — central governments could afford trains; trains suppressed
regional powers; suppression raised revenues; revenues bought bigger
trains and armies "and so on" (quoted at DeVries p. 128; established in
ch06 §7). Curry: the artillery revolution "could not be provided by any
other organisation than the state… at the level required to make an
impact" (p. 188).

**The causal-direction point — the chapter's precision. [BOOK'S
ARGUMENT, engaging Curry]** Curry reverses the arrow: strong governments
produce military revolutions, "not the other way around." The chapter's
answer, consistent with ch06's reviewed decision 4, is that the chain is
a *loop with a threshold step*. The fiscal instruments predate the
triumphs — the taille grant is 1439, the companies 1445, the Normandy
campaign 1449–50 — so the book does not claim the gun conjured the fisc
from nothing. It claims the gun did two things no earlier capability had
done: it set the entry price of decisive force above what any subject
could pay (Curry's own concession — the *only* capability that "could
not be provided by any other organisation than the state"), and it made
the crown's pre-existing fiscal-administrative machinery *decisive and
irreversible* — locking the loop in one direction. Burgundy shows the
loop running without a secure dynastic centre (one battle-death unwound
it — but into the French and Habsburg fiscs, not into dispersal); the
Swiss show battles won outside the loop producing no state; England
shows the loop broken at the fiscal-administrative link and sovereignty
failing with it. Four polities, one mechanism, three failure modes and
one success — that is the comparative shape of the chapter.

**The spine's own attestation. [T2, in repo]** Cerny 1998: the medieval
durable disorder ended through "structured competition that engendered
the emergence and consolidation of hierarchical nation-states, states
which could consolidate because they were multitasking and because they
could afford to make increasing side-payments" (pp. 58–59), under the
exogenous pressure of "the inter-dynastic struggles of the Hundred
Years' War" (p. 52), out of "the creative destruction of fifteenth to
seventeenth century warfare" (p. 51). The book's mechanism chapter can
close its historical half by noting that the neomedieval literature's
own account of *how the first dispersal ended* is this chapter's
argument stated in IPE vocabulary.
