## 1. The medieval original, done comparatively

**England — livery and maintenance. [CONSENSUS on facts; interpretation
gated.]** The fifteenth-century bond was "not tenurial but financial, not
hereditary but personal": retainers contracted by written **indenture** to
serve their lord armed and mounted, for fees and livery, the practice
proliferating from Edward III's war service onward (Oxford Companion via
encyclopedia.com, T3). The term "bastard feudalism" was coined by Charles
Plummer (1885) as an accusation; K. B. McFarlane's redefinition made payment-
for-service its essence, the **affinity** (the lord's connection of retained
gentry, lawyers and administrators) the operative political unit, and located
the disorder not in the system but in inadequate kingship — Henry VI (open
tertiary; **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: McFarlane, "Bastard Feudalism," BIHR 20
(1945) — exact text and volume/page to be confirmed against the journal;
Hicks, *Bastard Feudalism*, 1995]**). The statutory sequence is open-sourced:
1390 (livery restricted to household, officers, counsel), 1429, 1468
(extension to indentured retaining), and Henry VII's **1504** act requiring a
royal licence to retain — the crown asserting a licensing monopoly over
private force, foreshadowing Chapter 11 (**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: McKelvie,
*Bastard Feudalism, English Society and the Law*, 2020, for the enforcement
record]**).

**France — the écorcheurs. [CONSENSUS]** The "flayers": mercenary companies
left unemployed by the Congress of Arras (1435), terrorising the provinces
c. 1434–44; the crown exported the problem during the Truce of Tours (1444)
— the Dauphin led the bands to the Upper Rhine in 1444–45 — before absorbing
the best companies into the *compagnies d'ordonnance* (1445) and destroying
the rest (Wikipedia/T4 pointers citing Contamine; the JMH 2012 article on the
1444–45 expedition, abstract open). Foreshadowed only; Chapters 7 and 10 own
the reform. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Contamine for scale figures.]**

**Burgundy — clientage through ceremonial. [CONSENSUS on facts; scale
gated.]** The Valois dukes fused chivalric ceremonial with paid retinue: the
**Order of the Golden Fleece** (founded 1430 by Philip the Good at Bruges)
bound the high nobility of the composite Burgundian lands to the duke —
clientage dressed as chivalry (Britannica via snippet; EBSCO; the Low
Countries essay, T3). Court-size and expenditure figures are **NOT FOUND at
citable quality in open sources** — **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Vaughan,
*Philip the Good*]**; the draft asserts the mechanism, not numbers.

**Germany — Fehde and Landfrieden. [CONSENSUS, with one first-rank open
source.]** The noble feud was a *legal institution*: "in principle limited to
the holders of lordship, limited to certain kinds of hostile action… and
limited in duration," grounded in "the basic right of every noble to use
force to pursue his rights" (Kaminsky, *Past & Present* 177 (2002), **open
full text** — the chapter's best open scholarly anchor, building on Brunner,
gated). The *Landfrieden* ordinances (Mainz 1235) attempted restriction; the
**Ewiger Landfriede (7 August 1495)** banned the Fehde outright and erected
the Reichskammergericht — and *did not work quickly*: feuding persisted well
into the sixteenth century (Grumbach executed as a peace-breaker in 1567), a
persistence signalled by the very range of Zmora's study (Franconia,
1440–**1567**) (**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Zmora, *State and Nobility in
Early Modern Germany*, for the feud-as-state-building thesis and post-1495
statistics]**).

**Italy** — held for Chapter 4, per the brief.

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Across all four: the great subject retains private
force through a *legal instrument* the centre tolerates, licenses, or fails
to suppress — indenture, company contract, chivalric order, declared feud.
The pattern is pan-European; England is merely the best-documented.

## 2. The modern parallel — the contractor economy at scale

**[CONSENSUS, largely T1.]** McFate's thesis (bundled — the eroded mercenary
taboo as the return of Bull's "private international violence"; the US as
monopsony consumer-in-chief) is the spine; the update extends it across
domains with primary figures:

- **Scale.** US federal contract obligations ~**$755bn in FY2024**, DoD
  $445.1bn (59%) (GAO dashboard, T1). CRS: DoD FY2022 obligations $415bn,
  "more than all other agencies combined"; ~**369,000 contractor FTEs** in
  reportable service portfolios (an undercount — classified components
  excluded). In Iraq/Afghanistan 2001–20, contractors were "50% or more of
  the total DOD presence in country" (CRS IF10600, T1/T2) — McFate's 1:1
  ratio held for two decades. Current CENTCOM census: ~21,000 contractors in
  the AOR (2Q FY2024; DoD primary).
- **Intelligence contracting.** The official FY2007 figure: contractors 27%
  of the IC workforce; contractors ~45% of the in-access Top Secret
  population (ODNI FY2019 clearance report, T1). The 70%-of-budget figure is
  used only as flagged in §0.2. Booz Allen (98% government revenue) and
  Palantir (Maven Smart System: $480M in May 2024, ceiling raised to ~$1.3bn
  May 2025; 10-K figures T1, Maven via trade press reporting Pentagon
  announcements — pull the defense.gov digest as primary before print).
- **Border/security.** UK asylum accommodation: three contracts (Clearsprings,
  **Serco**, Mears) re-valued from £4.5bn to **£15.3bn** (NAO, May 2025, T1);
  the Brook House Inquiry (statutory, 2023) on G4S-run detention — credible
  findings of mistreatment (gov.uk primary, T1).
- **Logistics.** LOGCAP V (2019): four IDIQs (KBR, Vectrus, Fluor,
  PAE-Parsons), **$82bn ceiling over ten years** (Army primary, T1).
- **Cyber.** NSO Group as the documented case: Pegasus classified as a
  defence export, saleable only under Israeli MoD (DECA) licence — the
  licence as the leash; the foreign-policy-leverage reading rests on NYT
  investigative reporting, not government records [CONTESTED — flag].
- **AI-mediated decision support.** Maven (above); the **CDAO frontier-AI
  awards of 14 July 2025** — OTA agreements up to $200M each to Anthropic,
  Google, OpenAI, and xAI for "agentic AI workflows" (~$800M total),
  deliberately outside the FAR (CDAO release via multiple outlets; cite the
  primary release before print).

## 3. The Williams counter-read

**[CONSENSUS, bundled.]** Williams (2008, bundled) is the pessimist limb:
state weakening in certain zones produces not durable disorder but "new dark
ages"; his "alternatively governed" spaces (integrated in ch05's memo) show
the contractor economy is, in those zones, *the operative form of
governance*, not a symptom. Chronology caveat carried from the corpus index:
Williams (2008) precedes McFate (2011/2014); the book stages the contrast
itself. Cerny 2005 (bundled) supplies the property-rights and grey-zones
characteristics this chapter instantiates.

## 4. The mechanism of retention — indenture to clearance

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT, instruments documented.]** The medieval indenture was a
technically-suspect but universally-practised legal fiction binding private
force to a magnate under the crown's nominal authority. The modern
instruments are documented and legal: the **FAR contract** (48 CFR ch. 1) as
the uniform legal form; the **security clearance** as the personal bond —
**4,243,937 people eligible** as of Oct 2019, contractors over a million of
them and ~45% of in-access Top Secret (ODNI, T1); **ITAR** (22 CFR 120–130)
controlling not just goods but "defense services" by *persons* — the licence
travels with the knowledge-holder. The 1504 parallel is exact in structure:
retaining only under royal licence. *Discipline:* the regulation supports the
mechanism, not the metaphor — the "modern indenture" is the book's framing
and is marked as such.

## 5. The mechanism of finance — the disanalogy, now in the filings

**[CONSENSUS, T1 — the chapter's core.]** Livery and maintenance ran on
rents, plunder, and central remittance in variable mix; the modern contractor
economy runs almost wholly on central remittance, and the firms say so as a
formal risk factor: Booz Allen "accounting for **98%** of our revenue…";
CACI "**95.7%**… of our total revenues"; Leidos "approximately **87%**"
(SEC 10-Ks, verbatim, verified). The modern condition is therefore *less*
neomedieval than the fifteenth century in the decisive respect: **the
contractors have not developed independent fiscal bases.** The state that
pays can, in principle, re-domesticate — which is Chapter 11's settlement
move, and the reason Prigozhin's arc (Chapter 4) ended as it did.

## 6. The pending question — the anomaly

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT; figures flagged.]** One class of actor inverts the
pattern. The frontier AI labs' revenue is overwhelmingly *commercial*:
OpenAI at a reported ~$21–25bn annualised (2025–26, The Information/Reuters
— **estimates**); Anthropic at a reported ~$30–47bn run-rate (2026, company
claims/Bloomberg — **unaudited**); against which the largest US private
security contractor (Constellis, ~$1.4bn — low-confidence estimate) is a
rounding error, and the state's own flagship AI contract ($200M ceiling) is
marginal. The labs are Booz Allen inverted: the state is a customer, not the
paymaster. Integration is nonetheless proceeding — the dated 2024–25 sequence
(policy change, Anduril, Palantir/AWS, Claude Gov, CDAO awards, GSA) belongs
to Chapter 4's Sforza-from-above analysis — and the **2026
Anthropic–Pentagon blacklist** is the live test: a contractor-adjacent actor
that set conditions, was struck with the state's coercive instruments, and
survived on its commercial base (raising $65bn during the blacklist).
*Cautions:* estimates flagged; cash-burn noted (the fiscal base rests on
capital-raising); 2026 litigation ongoing — date-stamp and hedge. The chapter
closes on the question the outline sets: what does the condition look like
when a contractor *does* develop independent finance? Chapter 4 answers with
Sforza.
