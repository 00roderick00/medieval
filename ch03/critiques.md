# Critiques — Ch. 3: Bastard Feudalism, Inc.

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 surfaced in
research. Each steelmanned before the answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at
the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The monopoly is not eroded; PMSCs are payroll (named)

**Steelman.** The strategic-studies revisionist's case: the state's monopoly
on legitimate violence is nowhere near as eroded as the neomedieval frame
claims. The chapter's own best evidence proves it — Booz Allen discloses 98%
government revenue, CACI 95.7%, Leidos 87%; LOGCAP exists at the Army's
pleasure; the CENTCOM census counts contractors *because the DoD pays and
directs them*. A workforce the state hires, clears, licenses (ITAR), audits
(GAO), and can defund at will is not a rival to the monopoly of violence; it
is the monopoly of violence, outsourced. Calling this "bastard feudalism" is
a metaphor at war with the chapter's own balance sheet: the fifteenth-century
magnate had rents and could defy the crown; Constellis cannot survive a lost
recompete. The neomedieval reading mistakes a procurement preference for a
constitutional condition.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the dependence — it is not a concession but
the chapter's own central finding, documented from the filings precisely so
the concession is exact. The chapter's claim is narrower and survives: (a)
the *form* of state capacity has changed — functions that were sovereign are
now performed by private organisations under legal instruments (contract,
clearance, licence) that structurally resemble the retention instruments of
the fifteenth century, and the *taboo* against private force (McFate's
evidence, bundled) has gone; (b) the dependence itself is the *disanalogy the
book needs*, stated as such in the outline and preserved per §6 — modern
contractors lack independent fiscal bases, which is exactly why the coming
re-domestication (Ch. 11) is feasible and why Prigozhin failed (Ch. 4); and
(c) the one actor class that *does* have an independent fiscal base — the
frontier lab — behaves differently, and the 2026 Anthropic–Pentagon episode
is the natural experiment: dependence explains contractor docility, and its
absence explains why a lab could refuse terms and survive. The objection
correctly describes the PMSCs; it thereby sharpens, rather than blunts, the
anomaly the chapter closes on.

## Objection 2 — The composite thesis is unsourced synthesis

**Steelman.** The chapter's organising claim — weak centre → magnates take
over state functions, financed by rents/remittance/plunder — is quoted from
no historian. The memo admits it: NOT FOUND as a single formulation. The
chapter is dressing its own generalisation in the authority of Tilly and
Kaminsky, who said adjacent but different things (Tilly: war-making *builds*
states; Kaminsky: the feud was a legal order, not a symptom of collapse).
Kaminsky in particular *contradicts* the "when the public sword rusts"
framing — he insists the feud was not disorder-from-weakness but a
constitutive right of the nobility, present in strong polities too.

**The chapter's answer.** Mark the synthesis as the book's own — (c), never
(a), per §4 — and absorb Kaminsky's correction rather than resisting it: the
chapter does not need "private force = decay"; it needs "private force as a
*legally instituted* layer of governance that central authority later
licensed, restricted, and absorbed" — which is precisely Kaminsky's picture,
plus the statutes of livery (1390–1504) and the Ewiger Landfriede (1495) as
the absorption record. The revised formulation is *stronger*: bastard
feudalism and the Fehde were not pathologies but institutions, and the
consolidating state's achievement (Chs. 10–11) was to convert their legal
instruments into licences it alone could grant. Tilly supplies the
protection-market frame; the composite remains the book's, said plainly.

## Objection 3 — The famous numbers are rotten

**Steelman.** The chapter's field runs on numbers that will not bear weight:
the "70% of the intelligence budget" figure is a leaked 2007 slide ODNI
itself disavowed; the clearance report is FY2019 (six-plus years stale); the
Constellis revenue is a data-scraper's guess; the lab revenues are leaks from
The Information; even the CRS table has an internal arithmetic error. A
chapter about contracting that cannot pin its own contract figures invites
the ch06 verdict: discredit the details, discredit the mechanism.

**The chapter's answer.** Agreed — and the memo already performs the triage
the objection demands. The load-bearing claims rest only on verbatim primary
text (10-K risk factors; GAO/CRS totals; ODNI's published FY2019 report; the
NAO valuation; the Army's LOGCAP announcement). The disputed 70% appears
*only as* a disputed leak, with the official 27% (FY2007) carrying the
intelligence-share point; Constellis is flagged low-confidence and used only
for order-of-magnitude contrast; lab revenues are flagged estimates with
outlet and date; the CRS table inconsistency is noted (use the text figures).
Where a figure cannot be pinned (Army Vantage; Serco/G4S mixes), the memo
asserts nothing. Omission over invention (§4) is the chapter's answer in
practice.

## Objection 4 — The AI-lab anomaly is a category error, not an anomaly

**Steelman.** The chapter's closing move smuggles a software company into a
chapter about force. The labs sell chatbots and enterprise licences; they
field no armed personnel, hold no contracts to apply violence, and their
"independent fiscal base" is a valuation bubble burning billions a quarter.
Calling Anthropic a "proto-condottiere" because it is rich is like calling
Standard Oil a condottiere in 1910. The genuinely armed contractors are
poor and dependent; the rich actor is unarmed. The anomaly dissolves on
inspection.

**The chapter's answer.** The chapter's claim is not that the labs are armed
but that they sit *inside the decisive-force supply chain* — which the
state's own conduct now documents: CDAO's $200M frontier-AI agreements for
"warfighting and enterprise" workflows, Maven's expansion, Claude Gov in
classified environments, the Anduril partnership on counter-UAS. The
fifteenth-century analogue of decisive capability was the gun-founder as
much as the man-at-arms (Ch. 6's institutional lesson); the modern analogue
includes the model-builder. What makes the labs the *case to watch* (the
outline's phrasing, kept deliberately modest) is the conjunction the PMSCs
lack: presence in the force supply chain *plus* a revenue base the state
does not control. The cash-burn point is conceded and flagged — the base is
capital-markets-dependent, which is itself a leash (Ch. 4 will weigh it).
And the 2026 blacklist episode is cited not as proof of sovereignty but as
the first observed *test* of the conjunction — hedged, date-stamped,
litigation ongoing. The chapter predicts nothing from it; it watches.

## Objection 5 — Pan-European sample, English conclusions

**Steelman.** The comparative register is decorative: the chapter's
mechanisms (indenture→clearance; remittance-finance) are derived almost
wholly from the English case and the modern US case, with France, Burgundy,
and Germany as colour. Kaminsky's Germany, in particular, does not fit the
retention-instrument frame at all — the Fehde was not retained service but a
right of lordship. The "common structural feature" is common only because it
is described at a level of abstraction where everything is common.

**The chapter's answer.** Partly conceded: the retention-instrument mechanism
is Anglo-modern in its sharpest form, and the draft should say so rather than
force the Fehde into it. The comparative claim is placed one level up, where
it genuinely holds and is documented per polity: *private force existed as a
legal institution that central authority could not yet monopolise* — the
indenture (England), the free company under contract or truce (France), the
ceremonial clientage of a near-sovereign duke (Burgundy), the declared feud
(Germany). What is common is not the instrument but the licensing problem —
and the differing instruments produce the differing absorptions of Chapter
11 (statute, ordonnance company, dynastic incorporation, imperial peace).
Difference is data, not noise; the disanalogies are stated per §6, and Italy
— where the instrument (the condotta) and the fiscal base diverge most —
gets its own chapter precisely because it does not fit this one.
