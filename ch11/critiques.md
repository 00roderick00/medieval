# Critiques — Ch. 11: The New Monarchies

Objections 1–2 are the outline's named stress tests; Objections 3–5
surfaced in research — two of them from the chapter's own 2025–26
verification results, which cut against easy versions of the thesis and
are engaged rather than smoothed. Each steelmanned before the answer (§6).
Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The great-power-frame complaint (named)

**Steelman.** Everything in §7 reads more naturally as interstate
competition than as internal consolidation. The Pillar Two carve-out is
the hegemon muscling a tax cartel; the DST retreats are tariff coercion;
the chip controls, WAICO, and the AI-stack export EOs are bloc-vs-bloc
instruments; even the "canonical assertion" section is dominated by the
EU regulating *American* firms and America retaliating. Rename the
chapter's evidence honestly and it is a Westphalian great-power story —
the Tudor frame is a costume.

**The chapter's answer — the internal/external test, now with better
evidence.** The outline's test stands: if bloc politics is great-power
balancing, the decisive actions should run *between* blocs against peer
states; if it is Tudor consolidation, they should run *inside* blocs
against domestic great subjects — with the external contest downstream.
The verified record shows both layers, in the predicted relation. Inside:
the US disciplines its own platforms through its own courts (Google
remedies; the contempt line against Apple; the Anthropic directive) and
its own subunits (EO 14365 preempting state AI law); China disciplined
its platforms first (2020–23) and only then built WAICO; the EU's fines
fall on gatekeepers *operating in its market* under laws of general
application. External: the carve-outs and tariff threats are blocs
refusing to let *another bloc's* canon bind their own subjects — which is
exactly what parallel settlements predict and what a single global
consolidation would forbid. The Pillar Two carve-out is the sharpest
case: the US did not break the minimum tax; it exempted *its own
parented groups* while the rest of the system binds everyone else's —
consolidation stopping at the bloc boundary, not failing inside it. The
distinction remains testable, and Appendix C's C11-1 scorecard codes
actions by target (own-subject vs peer-state) precisely so the two
readings can be scored.

## Objection 2 — The libertarian/decentralist complaint (named)

**Steelman.** The countervailing technologies are winning where it
matters. Open-weight frontier-class models keep appearing; crypto
survived its criminal-law decade and got a US statutory franchise (the
GENIUS Act is legalisation, not nationalisation); encrypted messaging is
default; the AI Act's bite keeps slipping (2026 → 2027/28); Art. 92 has
never been used; the RAISE Act is disclosure, not control. The tools of
exit are compounding faster than the tools of legibility.

**The chapter's answer.** Held to the outline's line, with the new
evidence sorted honestly. The countervailing forces are real and the
book's frame *locates* rather than denies them: they thrive in the
interstices (ch08's grey zones; ch12's interstitial spaces) and at the
low end of the stack (ch12's longbow layer). But the decisive-layer
evidence runs the other way: the GENIUS Act is precisely the print-move
— the state licensing the presses (reserve requirements, charters,
dollar denomination) rather than banning the pamphlets; stablecoin
legalisation extends dollar legibility into the grey zone. Open-weight
models matter, and the book's falsification indicator (C2-3/C3-2) tracks
exactly the case where they would bite — decisive capability escaping
absorption. As of this memo the observed motion is the opposite: the one
lab with an independent fiscal base spent early 2026 being blacklisted
by presidential directive and restored by court order — contested
absorption, not exit. The delay objection is conceded as to tempo (see
Objection 4) — tempo, not direction.

## Objection 3 — The hegemon's carve-out breaks the fiscal move

**Steelman.** §7's own headline: from 1 January 2026, US-parented groups
are formally outside the IIR/UTPR. The "permanent central taxation
reaching platform economies" move now exempts the polity that hosts most
of the platforms. Add Canada's DST rescission under tariff threat and
the June 2026 100%-tariff campaign, and the honest summary is that the
fiscal move failed at its decisive test: the richest subjects of the most
important bloc bought themselves out — through their state.

**The chapter's answer.** Distinguish the two claims the objection runs
together. The book's claim is *bloc-internal* fiscal consolidation — the
taille, not a universal papal tithe; the failed claim is *cross-bloc*
harmonisation, which the book never predicted and the medieval original
never achieved either (no fifteenth-century settlement taxed another
kingdom's magnates). Inside the US bloc the fiscal instruments proceed:
GILTI/CAMT (the side-by-side's own premise is that US law already taxes
the base), FATCA's one-way reach, the GENIUS Act's reserve-reporting
rails, IRS platform-economy reporting. Inside the EU, the directive
binds; CORE would tax large firms directly into the Union budget. What
the carve-out shows is the settlement's geometry — parallel fiscs, each
sovereign over its own subjects, contesting the overlap — and the memo
prints it as such rather than as harmony. Falsifiability sharpened: if
bloc-internal effective rates and reporting reach were now to *fall*
(not merely cross-bloc treaties), the fiscal move fails on the book's
own terms; C11-2 tracks that split explicitly.

## Objection 4 — Assertion without capacity: the canon is slipping its dates

**Steelman.** The EU delayed its high-risk regime by 16–24 months within
a year of asserting it; its gigafactories are unsited; its digital euro
is a 2029 promise pending 2026 legislation; Art. 92 sleeps. The US
asserts against its states while its own courts stall its platform
cases. If ch08 taught that the Empire's 1495 assertion-without-substrate
took a century to become real, the honest reading of 2026 is that the
canonical move is Reichskammergericht redux — paper supremacy.

**The chapter's answer.** Partly conceded — and the concession is the
book's own apparatus doing its work. The Depreter refinement (ch10
Revisions 8) says apparatus without a continuous political centre does
not consolidate; ch08's Empire case says assertion without fiscal
substrate enforces slowly. Both cut hardest at the EU, and the chapter
says so — that is ch12's EU assessment (canonically strong, structurally
unstable), not an embarrassment to hide. What the objection misses is
the differential: the delays cluster in the bloc *without* the fiscal-
military substrate, while the blocs with it convert assertion into
practice fast (China's labeling rules effective within six months; the
US turning procurement, clearances and export law — instruments it
already possessed — on the platforms within months of deciding to).
Assertion-to-enforcement lag is itself an Appendix C variable now
(C11-1 codes dates asserted vs dates enforced); the 1495 comparison is
a prediction about *which* polities' canons will bite, not a refutation.

## Objection 5 — The state keeps losing in its own courts

**Steelman.** Subordination's 2025–26 scorecard: the FTC *lost* to Meta
outright; Google kept Chrome and its liability is on appeal; Apple beat
the designation challenge only procedurally and its contempt sanctions
were partly reversed; X is appealing its DSA fine; and the presidential
directive against Anthropic — the clearest coercive act against a
platform in the record — was stayed within a month and reversed within
five weeks. The great subjects are winning the litigation the
"discipline-through-process" story depends on.

**The chapter's answer.** The pattern is the settlement, not its
failure — and the fifteenth century is the proof of concept. Henry VII's
instruments were *legal* instruments; Bergavenny's £70,650 was assessed
by statute, commuted by indenture, and cancelled by the next king — the
crown ruling through law was constrained by the law it ruled through,
and the constraint made the discipline durable rather than episodic
(the magnates kept litigating; the monopoly of legitimate adjudication
was the prize, and the crown held it). The modern record reads the same
way: every one of the objection's cases was decided *in the state's own
courts under the state's own law* — including the state's losses. That
is hierarchical plurality (ch08 §6) operating at the summit: the
platforms' victories are appeals to the sovereign's justice, not exits
from it — Meta wins by persuading an Article III judge, not by
excommunicating him; Anthropic's restoration was an *injunction*, the
public sword staying the public hand. What would actually falsify the
subordination move is adjudication migrating *out* of state courts —
platforms resolving their disputes with states in fora states do not
control — and the observed motion (ch08: Achmea, the ECT exit, DSA Art.
21's non-binding cap) runs the opposite way. C11-3 logs the win/loss
series so the sceptical reading stays scoreable; the book's claim
survives its members losing cases, but not the court losing the cases.
