## 1. Cerny 2005 — `Cerny_2005_Terrorism_and_New_Security_Dilemma_NWCR.pdf`

**Full citation.** Philip G. Cerny, "Terrorism and the New Security Dilemma," *Naval War College Review* 58, no. 1 (Winter 2005): 11–33. Newport, RI: U.S. Naval War College Press. (© 2004 Philip G. Cerny; author then Professor of Global Political Economy, Rutgers–Newark.)

**Tier — T2.** A substantive theoretical article by a leading IPE scholar and named foundation of the book's positioning; functions as peer-level journal literature despite the professional-military venue.

**Summary.** Cerny argues that post-9/11 American policy misreads terrorism by folding it into a state-centric realist frame prescribing unilateral military hegemony. Terrorism is instead one symptom of a deeper structural transformation he calls neomedievalism: overlapping, competing power structures above, below, and cutting across states, dissolving the state's monopoly on security as a public good. This produces a "New Security Dilemma," in which balance-of-power no longer stabilises the system and hegemonic responses generate backlashes that widen a self-reinforcing security deficit. He reframes the present through neomedieval features: competing institutions with overlapping jurisdictions; fluid boundaries lacking exogenous territorialising pressure; uneven consolidation of spaces and inequalities; fragmented identities; and expanding "zones grises" beyond the law. Crucially, no exogenous threat now forces consolidation, so the condition settles not into chaos but into "durable disorder"—a heterarchical order demanding sociopolitical, not military, remedies.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch1** (six-characteristics diagnostic checklist, war-college-tested version) · **Ch3** (property-rights & grey-zones characteristics) · **Ch5** (hollow sovereignty as a Cerny characteristic) · **Ch8** (legal grey zones, the sixth characteristic) · Introduction (durable disorder) · Ch12 & Appendix C (durable disorder in the interstitial spaces).

**Caveats.** Freely-available companion to the paywalled 1998 *Civil Wars* original; the framework predates 9/11. **Enumerates five characteristics, not six** — the outline's six-fold checklist derives from the 1998 piece (retrieval list). "Durable disorder" is borrowed from Alain Minc (1993); neomedievalism from Bull (1977). Cerny names *exogenous competitive pressure* as the consolidation driver now absent — the variable the book inverts. Mid-2004 empirics are atmospheric, now superseded.

## 2. Kobrin 1998/1999 — `Kobrin_1998-1999_Neomedievalism_Postmodern_Digital_World_Economy.pdf`

**Full citation.** Stephen J. Kobrin, "Back to the Future: Neomedievalism and the Postmodern Digital World Economy." Author manuscript (Wharton School, Univ. of Pennsylvania), forthcoming as ch. 6 in Aseem Prakash & Jeffrey A. Hart (eds.), *Globalization and Governance* (London: Routledge, 1999); a slightly revised version of the article in *Journal of International Affairs* 51, no. 2 (Spring 1998): 361–386.

**Tier — T2.** Peer-reviewed *JIA* article by a named Wharton scholar, subsequently an academic Routledge chapter; a foundational, frequently-cited statement of the digital-economy strand of neomedievalism and a named downstream source in the doctrine.

**Summary.** Kobrin argues we are living through a systemic transition from the modern to a "postmodern" world order comparable to the medieval-to-modern shift of the sixteenth–seventeenth centuries. Two technologically driven forces—the escalating cost and complexity of technology, which outgrows national markets, and the rise of a digital, electronically networked world economy—are dissolving the territorial basis of politics and economics. Because markets are increasingly constructed in cyberspace, which is neither physical nor geographic, geographically rooted jurisdiction loses meaning: one cannot say where a transaction occurs, whose law applies, or who may tax it. Deploying the neomedieval metaphor as inter-temporal comparison (not literal return), Kobrin traces the erosion across space and borders, ambiguous authority, multiple loyalties, transnational elites, blurred public/private property, and the pull toward supranational centralization. Territorial sovereignty, he concludes, may be an ephemeral modern anomaly, not a transhistorical principle of political organization.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch2** (Kobrin's prophecy re-read — central) · **Ch4** (transnational commercial sovereignty as a Kobrin-anticipated pattern) · Ch11 (predictive translation of the platform universalisms) · Ch12 (the two open gaps: platforms as universalisms) · Introduction & Appendix A/D (literature roll-call).

**Caveats.** This is the author's pre-publication manuscript; manuscript page numbers (2–35) match neither the *JIA* nor the Routledge edition — cite published pagination. The manuscript is unproofed (title misspells "Neomedivalism"; garbled bibliography spellings) — verify names against published versions. Kobrin himself points toward re-*centralization* (supranational authority with taxation/enforcement powers), which cuts toward the book's re-consolidation thesis though he frames the terminus as supranational rather than intra-bloc.

## 3. McFate 2011 — `McFate_2011_Durable_Disorder_LSE_thesis.pdf`

**Full citation.** Sean McFate, *Durable Disorder: The Return of Private Armies and the Emergence of Neomedievalism*, PhD thesis, Department of International Relations, London School of Economics and Political Science, submitted 1 August 2011 (LSE Research Online). The full academic-apparatus version (482 pp., ~800 footnotes, Liberia participant-observer case study) underlying *The Modern Mercenary: Private Armies and What They Mean for World Order* (Oxford UP, 2014).

**Tier — T2.** A completed LSE PhD thesis with full academic apparatus, meeting the doctrine's definition of T2 and directly downstream of Bull, Friedrichs, Cerny, and Kobrin.

**Summary.** Since the Cold War's end, private military companies—"conflict entrepreneurs that kill or train others to kill"—have proliferated. McFate's puzzle: the primary customer is not weak states seeking to consolidate force but the strongest state, the United States, which already holds the world's greatest monopoly of violence. He argues this reappearance is a harbinger of "globalised neomedievalism." The eroding taboo against mercenarism signals a return to the pre-Westphalian norm, when states enjoyed no monopoly of force and authority was polycentric—diluted among state and non-state actors. Neomedievalism is non-state-centric and multipolar, with overlapping authorities and allegiances at once local and global. It implies neither atavism nor anarchy: states persist but matter less, and the system settles into a "durable disorder" that contains rather than solves problems. The central danger is the commodification of conflict—selling war to anyone who can pay—making the twenty-first century resemble the twelfth more than the twentieth.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch3** (Bastard Feudalism, Inc. — the modern PMSC parallel, central) · **Ch4** (the condottieri; modern parallels; the Prigozhin question) · Ch11 (domestication of the market for force) · Ch12 · Introduction/Positioning (downstream of McFate).

**Caveats.** Cite the thesis for full apparatus and the Liberia annexes (DynCorp IDIQ contract; Comprehensive Peace Agreement); the core argument is identical to *The Modern Mercenary* (2014). "Durable disorder" is not McFate's coinage (credits Minc and Cerny). Insider positionality: McFate is a former US Army paratrooper and DynCorp contractor, and the Liberia chapter is participant-observation—privileged testimony, not neutral observation. Forward-looking passages (the *Yojimbo* two-clients proxy-war scenario) are explicitly conjectural. Key load-bearing point for the book: the modern market is a **monopsony** (the US as consumer-in-chief), and modern PMCs *require* state-provided stability (banking, contract enforcement)—the precise disanalogy with the fiscally autonomous condottiere that Part III turns on. He does not use "bastard feudalism"; that framing is the book's.

## 4. Williams 2008 — `Williams_2008_New_Middle_Ages_to_New_Dark_Age_SSI.pdf`

**Full citation.** Phil Williams, *From the New Middle Ages to a New Dark Age: The Decline of the State and U.S. Strategy* (Carlisle, PA: Strategic Studies Institute, U.S. Army War College Press, June 2008). Monograph #645. ISBN 1-58487-358-2. Public-domain U.S. Government work.

**Tier — T3.** Serious government/think-tank strategic analysis: a single-authored, internally-reviewed SSI policy monograph rather than blind-peer-reviewed journal literature or a canonical scholarly monograph, so it sits in T3 despite Williams's T2-caliber credentials—its structural claims should be corroborated, but it is authoritative as the pessimist strategic reading and cites the T2 neomedieval literature it builds on.

**Summary.** Williams accepts the neomedievalist diagnosis—fragmented authority, overlapping jurisdictions, no-go zones, contested property, competing loyalties—but rejects its complacency. Where Cerny's neomedievalism treats "durable disorder" as a stable, self-managing equilibrium, Williams argues state decline, both absolute and relative, is accelerating and could shift abruptly and non-linearly from durable disorder into chaos: a "New Dark Age." Failed states are not aberrations but symptoms of the Westphalian system's structural erosion, obscured by policymakers' "stateocentrism." His evidence is the interstitial zone: feral cities and slums, the drug-run favelas of Rio and São Paulo, Sadr City governed by the Mahdi Army, clan dynamics in Somalia, Iraq and Afghanistan, porous borders and transnational criminal networks. These spaces are not ungoverned but alternatively governed by militias, traffickers and warlords acting as surrogate authorities. Intersecting shocks—transnational crime, terrorism, pandemics, WMD proliferation, climate stress—could tip disorder into chaos, demanding a holistic, "transagency" U.S. strategy.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch3** (the Williams counter-read — the pessimist's version) · Ch5 (hollow sovereignty as territorial failure: Somalia) · Ch11–Ch12 (the interstitial neomedieval spaces).

**Caveats.** June 2008; visibly of its moment (Iraq surge, March 2008 Basra fighting) and predates the events that would test it (Sept. 2008 crisis, Arab Spring, ISIS's territorial proto-state). **Chronology vs. McFate:** Williams is a *predecessor* (2008), not an interlocutor—he cannot be read as replying to McFate (2014); the book must stage the McFate contrast itself. The "New Dark Age" is offered as a hedged worst-case scenario, not a prediction. Quantified projections are dated forecasts—check against outcomes.

## 5. Wæver 1996 — `Waever_1996_European_Security_Identities_JCMS.pdf`

**Full citation.** Ole Wæver, "European Security Identities," *Journal of Common Market Studies* 34, no. 1 (March 1996): 103–132. Oxford: Blackwell. (Author then at the Centre for Peace and Conflict Research, Copenhagen.)

**Tier — T2.** A peer-reviewed article in a leading journal by a founder of the Copenhagen School, foundational for securitization theory and named in the book's positioning discipline.

**Summary.** Wæver reads post–Cold War European order through the Copenhagen School's securitization framework: security is a speech act by which an actor declares a referent object existentially threatened and thereby claims extraordinary measures. Distinguishing referent object from actor, he shows survival means different things to different units—sovereignty for the state, identity for society/nation, sustainability for the environment. Post-bipolar Europe hosts overlapping political subjectivities: state and nation decouple in the west and recouple in the east. Sovereignty becomes contested—a "new middle ages" of overlapping authorities—organised as concentric circles around a "westernistic" centre, Europe being systemically post-sovereign while units retain formal sovereignty. Most strikingly, "Europe" itself becomes a security referent: integration is securitised against the existential threat of fragmentation (Europe's own violent past), binding security, integration and identity into one self-referential project distinguished from its Other "in time, rather than space."

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch11** (the EU regulatory church / the new monarchies — EU as neomedieval polity) · Introduction (Copenhagen School engagement) · Ch1 (the diagnostic literature) · Ch12 (the EU as anomalous case) · Appendix A/D.

**Caveats.** A contemporaneous complement to—not one of—the two target Wæver chapters that remain paywalled: "Europe, State and Nation in the New Middle Ages" (1996) and "Imperial Metaphors" (1997), both on the retrieval list. It shares their vocabulary (explicit "new middle ages"; concentric circles; the "three empires" line) and is the accessible, page-verifiable statement of the securitization-of-Europe argument.

## 6. Kaplan 1994 — `Kaplan_1994_Coming_Anarchy_Atlantic.pdf`

**Full citation.** Robert D. Kaplan, "The Coming Anarchy," *The Atlantic Monthly* 273, no. 2 (February 1994): 44–76. (This bundled copy is a web-rendered reprint; PDF pagination differs from the journal's pp. 44–76.)

**Tier — T3.** Reputable long-form magazine journalism—an influential *Atlantic* essay synthesising named scholars and firsthand reportage; period-defining commentary, not peer-reviewed scholarship or a primary source.

**Summary.** Opening in Sierra Leone and travelling across West Africa, Kaplan reads the region as a "premonition of the future": a place where crime, disease, overpopulation, deforestation, refugee flows and tribalism are dissolving the social fabric and hollowing out states whose borders, he insists, are cartographic lies. West Africa is his "prism" and bellwether; its criminal anarchy foreshadows the twenty-first-century order. Braiding Homer-Dixon on environmental scarcity, Huntington on civilisational clash, and van Creveld on the transformation of war, he sketches a "bifurcated world"—a comfortable "stretch limo" of post-historical elites outside which re-primitivised sub-national wars blur crime and war. The nation-state, a Western invention, is eroding; the future is a "Last Map," an ever-mutating hologram of city-states, shanty-states, mafias and moving power-centres "as in the Middle Ages." The mode is atmospheric, impressionistic and travelogue-driven rather than analytically rigorous.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Introduction** (the declinism-cycle inoculation: "Kaplan announced it in 1994") · **Ch1** (engaged critically as the atmospheric predecessor) · Appendix D.

**Caveats.** Kaplan does **not** use "neomedievalism" or any rigorous medievalist framework; his medieval references are evocative gestures, not a mechanism. Position him as influence/atmosphere—the mood-setting predecessor systematised by Bull's heirs—never as analytical scaffolding. His causal engine is Malthusian scarcity, a clean point of divergence from the book's force-cost mechanism. His method is self-consciously impressionistic, the non-rigorous mode the book critiques.

## 8. Slaughter 2004, Ch. 5 — `Slaughter_2004_New_World_Order_Ch5.pdf`

**Full citation.** Anne-Marie Slaughter, "An Effective World Order," ch. 5 in *A New World Order* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2004), pp. 210–282. (Bundled as a pre-publication typescript; text is that of the published chapter.)

**Tier — T2.** A canonical statement of the transgovernmental-networks / disaggregated-sovereignty thesis from a leading international-law scholar (then Dean of Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School), densely footnoted; the book must engage it.

**Summary.** Chapter 5 asks how a disaggregated, networked order—of regulators, judges, and legislators rather than unitary states—actually governs, dividing the answer into what networks do now and what they could do. Presently they generate convergence and "informed divergence" of national rules through regulatory export, credible information, and non-binding "soft law" (World Bank guidelines, codes of best practice); they strengthen compliance through vertical enforcement and capacity-building in weak states; and they enhance cooperation through "regulation by information" (EU information agencies, NAFTA's environmental commission, the UN Global Compact learning forum). Marrying soft power—persuasion, socialisation, reputation—to the hard power officials already wield at home, networks could become self-conscious governance mechanisms: enforcing "network norms" through professional socialisation and selective membership, generating reasoned consensus through deliberation, and harnessing "positive conflict" as an engine of trust. What such an order still lacks, she concludes, is norms.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch1** (Slaughter's networked-government alternative — "important and wrong") · **Ch2** (the alternative reading being rejected) · Ch12 (networked government as interregnum, not architecture) · Introduction/Positioning (against the Silicon Valley optimist frame).

**Caveats.** Slaughter presents government networks as the *architecture of a new order* co-existing with traditional institutions—asserting exactly the stability the book denies. For the book, this is the connective tissue of an interregnum mistaken for load-bearing architecture. Pre-publication typescript pagination (210–333) approximates the published edition.

## 9. Slaughter 2004, Ch. 6 — `Slaughter_2004_New_World_Order_Ch6.pdf`

**Full citation.** Anne-Marie Slaughter, "A Just World Order," ch. 6 in *A New World Order* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2004), pp. 283–333. (Bundled as a pre-publication typescript; text is that of the published chapter.)

**Tier — T2.** As Ch. 5: canonical Princeton UP scholarship, the normative half of the disaggregated-sovereignty argument.

**Summary.** Chapter 6 concedes that a feasible, effective networked order is not necessarily a desirable one, and confronts the critiques. Government networks are charged with technocracy (unelected regulators and judges escaping politics, as with the Basel capital-adequacy accords), with distorting national political processes through closed-door harmonisation, with feeding unrepresentative input into global and judicial decision-making, and with replicating power asymmetries by privileging wealthy, expert states. Slaughter answers with reforms: treating every official as performing a "dual function," domestic and transgovernmental; making networks visible through a virtual public sphere; building legislative networks to counterbalance regulators; and mobilising transnational policy networks around a governmental "spine." She then proposes five governing norms—global deliberative equality, legitimate difference ("different" is not "wrong"), positive comity, Madisonian checks and balances, and subsidiarity—to constrain networked power without erecting a centralised world government, preserving pluralism and local autonomy within a global order.

**Chapters (per outline).** **Ch1** (Slaughter's networked-government alternative) · **Ch2** (the alternative reading being rejected) · Ch12 (the two open gaps) · Introduction/Positioning.

**Caveats.** Ch6 concedes the "ineradicability of power"—weak states subject to the "soft power of the strongest members"—yet bets inclusion and counter-networks diffuse power rather than concentrate it. This is the pivotal wrong turn on the book's terms: Slaughter sees networks dispersing power where the artillery-state mechanism predicts the underlying stacks concentrating it and driving re-consolidation. Subsidiarity deliberately keeps power dispersed downward—offered as sufficient connective tissue.
