# Neomedievalism — Reading Access Dossier

A location guide to the working bibliography. Each entry gives the best legitimate access path I could find: open-access PDFs where they exist, publisher/DOI pages where they don't, and Internet Archive borrowable copies for the monographs. Starred items (★) mark the spine reading.

---

## I. Foundation

### ★ Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society: A Study of Order in World Politics* (Columbia UP, 1977; 4th ed. 2012)

- Full text (1977 first edition), Internet Archive — borrowable: archive.org/details/anarchicalsociet00bull
- Full text (later printing), Internet Archive — borrowable: archive.org/details/anarchicalsociet0000bull
- Full-text PDF (3rd edition, Palgrave), National Academic Digital Library of Ethiopia: ndl.ethernet.edu.et — direct PDF · landing page
- Chapter 10 excerpt (Olivia Lau course archive): olivialau.org/ir/archive/bul2.pdf (this is the "New Mediaevalism" section you want; pair with bul1.pdf for the opening chapters)
- **Reading target:** Chapter 10, "The Decline of the States System?" — the "A New Mediaevalism" section and the five sub-trends (regional integration; state disintegration; private international violence; transnational organizations; technological unification), plus the book's concluding chapter for Bull's own skepticism.

### Martin Wight, *Systems of States* (Leicester UP, 1977)

- Internet Archive — borrowable: archive.org/details/systemsofstates0000wigh
- Open Library record: openlibrary.org/books/OL4605215M
- No fully open PDF; the Archive borrow is the working option.
- **Reading target:** "De Systematibus Civitatum," pp. 21–45.

## II. Theoretical Reconstruction

### ★ Jörg Friedrichs, "The Meaning of New Medievalism," *European Journal of International Relations* 7:4 (2001), 475–502

- DOI / publisher page (paywalled at Sage): doi.org/10.1177/1354066101007004004
- Book-chapter version (more polished), full text in the ethernet.edu.et repository: *European Approaches to International Relations Theory* (Routledge, 2007), Chapter 7 — PDF of full book (the neomedievalism chapter runs roughly pp. 127–145)
- If you can only read one, the 2007 chapter is the tighter version.

### ★ Philip G. Cerny, "Neomedievalism, Civil War and the New Security Dilemma: Globalisation as Durable Disorder," *Civil Wars* 1:1 (1998), 36–64

- DOI / publisher (paywalled): tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/13698249808402366
- Semantic Scholar record with abstract and citation graph: semanticscholar.org/paper/…Cerny/fbb9014fcd009aab6620731a05e2106696df58e4
- Companion/successor article by Cerny with much of the same argument, freely available: "Terrorism and the New Security Dilemma," *Naval War College Review* 58:1 (2005) — full PDF at USNWC Digital Commons. This is the closest freely-available Cerny statement of the six-characteristics framework if you can't get institutional access to the 1998 original.
- **Reading target in the 1998 piece:** the six characteristics of a neomedieval world (around p. 42): multiple competing institutions; absence of exogenous territorializing pressures; uneven consolidation of new spaces, cleavages, and inequalities; fragmented loyalties and identities; entrenchment of property rights; spread of legal grey zones and the black economy.

### ★ Stephen J. Kobrin — the "platforms" line

Two versions of the same argument. The 1998/1999 chapter version is freely available in a near-final author manuscript:

- "Neomedievalism and the Postmodern Digital World Economy" — author manuscript (Wharton faculty repository, forthcoming in Prakash & Hart, *Globalization and Governance*, Routledge, 1999; explicitly noted on the title page as "a slightly revised version" of the *Journal of International Affairs* Spring 1998 article, pp. 361–386): faculty.wharton.upenn.edu/…hartrev2.pdf — this is your working full text for the 1998 JIA argument.
- "Back to the Future: Neomedievalism and the Postmodern Digital Economy," *Journal of International Affairs* 51:2 (Spring 1998), 361–386 — journal issue forward at CIAO: ciaotest.cc.columbia.edu/olj/jia/jia_spr98fwd.html; Semantic Scholar record: semanticscholar.org/…Kobrin/0c12a3ff8fca85a22c707dee0f9ea8577da0ecf2.
- "The Architecture of Globalization: State Sovereignty in a Networked Global Economy," in Dunning (ed.), *Governments, Globalization, and International Business* (Oxford UP, 1999), 146–172 — publisher page: OUP Academic. The Wharton page also hosts a related Kobrin manuscript on multinationals and sovereignty (Oxford-rev2-print.pdf) but this is a different chapter (for the Rugman/Brewer Oxford Handbook of International Business), not "Architecture of Globalization." No freely available PDF of the Dunning chapter found; use OUP or a library.

## III. Security, Private Force, and the Military Dimension

### ★ Sean McFate, *The Modern Mercenary: Private Armies and What They Mean for World Order* (Oxford UP, 2014)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full text: archive.org/details/modernmercenaryp0000mcfa
- Publisher / Google Books preview: books.google.com/books?id=T0gnDAAAQBAJ
- **Reading target:** the neomedievalism/"durable disorder" chapters (the NLA catalog confirms the chapter titles: "Neomedievalism," "Neomedieval warfare," "Military enterprisers in Liberia," "Mercenaries in Somalia: a neomedieval tale," "Medieval modernity" — NLA catalog record).

### Sean McFate, *Durable Disorder: The Return of Private Armies and the Emergence of Neomedievalism* — PhD thesis, LSE, 2011

- Full-text PDF, LSE Theses Online — freely downloadable (~10 MB): etheses.lse.ac.uk/3118
- This is the academic-apparatus version underlying *The Modern Mercenary*. Long, but genuinely free.

### Phil Williams, *From the New Middle Ages to a New Dark Age: The Decline of the State and U.S. Strategy* (SSI / US Army War College, 2008)

- Full-text PDF, freely available from the Army War College Press: press.armywarcollege.edu/…article=1644
- Landing page (monograph #645): press.armywarcollege.edu/monographs/645
- **Reading target:** the core argument at pp. 23–27 and the policy conclusions.

## IV. European / Regional Application

### Jan Zielonka, *Europe as Empire: The Nature of the Enlarged European Union* (Oxford UP, 2006)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full text: archive.org/details/europeasempirena0000ziel
- Publisher (OUP) chapter-level access: OUP Academic — Chapter 2, "European Power Politics" (the ch. 1/2 pair is what you want)
- Google Books preview: books.google.com/books?id=thJREAAAQBAJ
- Supplement: Zielonka, "The Remaking of the EU's Borders and the Images of European Architecture," *Journal of European Integration* 39:5 (2017), 641–656 — publisher access via Taylor & Francis (paywalled; check institutional).

### Ole Wæver — two pieces

- "Europe, State and Nation in the New Middle Ages," in de Wilde & Wiberg (eds.), *Organized Anarchy in Europe* (Tauris, 1996), 107–128 — no open PDF located. Best route: library access via the edited volume.
- "Imperial Metaphors: Emerging European Analogies to Pre-Nation-State Imperial Systems" (this is the essay referenced as "After Neo-Medievalism: Imperial Metaphors for European Security") — appears in Tunander, Baev & Einagel (eds.), *Geopolitics in Post-Wall Europe: Security, Territory and Identity* (Sage/PRIO, 1997) and in the Burgess volume; PRIO listing: prio.org/publications/718. No open full text located; library access via either edited volume.
- Bonus, freely available Wæver from the same period on the same themes: "European Security Identities," *JCMS* 34:1 (1996) — full PDF at gla.ac.uk/…Waever_for_Galbreath.pdf. Useful complement even though it isn't one of the two target chapters.

### N. J. Rengger, "European Communities in a Neo-Medieval Global Polity: The Dilemmas of Fairyland?" in Kelstrup & Williams (eds.), *International Relations Theory and the Politics of European Integration* (Routledge, 2000), 51–71

- Publisher page (table of contents visible): routledge.com/…9780415214179
- Google Books preview: books.google.com/books?id=oVilsKZIZrsC
- No open PDF located; use the preview or library access.

## V. Popular / Strategic Synthesis

### Parag Khanna, "The Next Big Thing: Neomedievalism," *Foreign Policy* (May/June 2009)

- Full text, freely available: foreignpolicy.com/2009/09/17/the-next-big-thing-neomedievalism
- Short (three pages). Read it whole.
- Follow-on book: *Connectography* (2016) — Internet Archive borrow: search "connectography" at archive.org; publisher page at Penguin Random House.

### Robert D. Kaplan, "The Coming Anarchy," *The Atlantic* (February 1994)

- Full text at The Atlantic's archive, freely available: theatlantic.com/past/politics/foreign/anarchy.htm
- Read for atmosphere and influence; Kaplan doesn't use the term rigorously.

### Anne-Marie Slaughter, *A New World Order* (Princeton UP, 2004)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full text: archive.org/details/newworldorder0000slau
- Author-hosted chapter PDFs (Chapters 5 & 6): iilj.org — Chapter 5 · Chapter 6
- Author's antecedent essay: "The Real New World Order," Slaughter Princeton site — slaughter.scholar.princeton.edu/document/224
- **Reading target:** the Introduction — the optimist's counter-case.

## VI. Medieval History (the substrate)

### Joseph Strayer, *On the Medieval Origins of the Modern State* (Princeton UP, 1970)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full text: archive.org/details/onmedievalorigin0000stra
- Google Books preview: books.google.com.pr/books?id=8SaHb2UaLEEC
- Under 120 pages; read cover to cover.

### Perry Anderson, *Lineages of the Absolutist State* (NLB, 1974)

- Full-text PDF, University of Lisbon (ISEG) course repository: fenix.iseg.ulisboa.pt/…Perry Anderson_Lineages of the Absolutist State.pdf
- Companion volume *Passages from Antiquity to Feudalism* (NLB, 1974) — Verso publisher page, Internet Archive borrow available.

### Walter Ullmann, *The Growth of Papal Government in the Middle Ages* (Methuen, 3rd ed. 1970)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full texts: archive.org/details/growthofpapalgov0000walt · archive.org/details/growthofpapalgov0000ullm_m4j6
- Full text (older edition) on Archive with djvu OCR: archive.org/stream/in.ernet.dli.2015.129662/…
- **Reading target:** introduction and conclusion.

### G. R. Elton, *The Tudor Revolution in Government* (Cambridge UP, 1953)

- Internet Archive — borrowable full text: archive.org/details/tudorrevolutioni0000elto
- Cambridge published excerpt PDF (front matter/opening): assets.cambridge.org/…9780521092357_excerpt.pdf

## Two gaps worth restating (from your brief)

1. **No book-length serious treatment of platforms as the new universal Church/Empire.** Kobrin sketched it in 1998; the FAANG-era literature never caught up.
2. **No serious treatment of the re-consolidation question — "Who is the Tudor state?"** The literature is entirely about the dispersal phase. The pendulum's return swing is unclaimed territory.
