## 1. Kobrin's prophecy re-read

**[CONSENSUS, bundled]** Kobrin (bundled; see corpus/bundled/index.md) saw in
1998 that a digital, networked economy dissolves the *territorial* basis of
jurisdiction: in cyberspace one cannot say where a transaction occurs, whose
law applies, or who may tax it, so singular territorial authority gives way to
"overlapping, heteronomous" authority — the medieval condition returning. What
Kobrin *could not see*, and what the literature never developed, is **which
institutions would fill the void** — the specific universalist actors with
specific canonical claims. **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** That is the gap Chapter 2
fills: not a "market" (Friedrichs's second universalism, Chapter 1) but a set
of *institutional churches*. Usefully, the corpus indexing found Kobrin himself
pointing toward *re-centralisation* (a supranational authority with taxation
and enforcement powers) — which the book redirects from the supranational to
the intra-bloc terminus (Part III). This chapter cites Kobrin freely (in-repo,
§4).

## 2. The medieval templates

The templates are the settlements to one recurring structural problem: two
universal authorities claiming jurisdiction over the same subjects (bishops who
were both spiritual officers and temporal vassals).

- **The Investiture Contest.** The question — who *invests* an officeholder
  with legitimacy — is the chapter's core structural template. Gregory VII's
  *Dictatus Papae* (1075) asserted a monarchic papal supremacy (may depose
  emperors; "judged by no one"); Henry IV's penance at Canossa (1077); and the
  **Concordat of Worms (1122)**, which *partitioned the symbols* — the emperor
  renounced investiture with ring and crozier, retained the sceptre (the
  *regalia*) — but **did not resolve which universal authority was ultimately
  superior**. [CONSENSUS] (encyclopedia.com reprinting the *New Catholic
  Encyclopedia*, open, with an MGH/Barraclough bibliography). The interpretive
  framing — Worms as an unresolved structural stalemate, the *Dictatus* as
  monarchic sovereignty — is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Ullmann, *The Growth of
  Papal Government in the Middle Ages*]**, the chapter's scholarly spine.
- **The Avignon papacy (1309–1377).** A universal authority perceived as
  *captured* by a particular polity (France); Petrarch's "Babylonian
  Captivity." **[CONTESTED]** — modern scholarship complicates the puppet
  reading; the draft presents dependence as contemporary perception, not fact
  (New World Encyclopedia, open; **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]** for a scholarly
  Avignon study).
- **Conciliarism and the Great Schism (1378–1417).** The intra-Church attempt
  to constrain universal authority through a representative institution (a
  general council above the pope). The schism's two obediences, the Council of
  Pisa's third claimant (1409), and the Council of Constance's resolution
  (Martin V, 11 November 1417). [CONSENSUS] (Catholic Encyclopedia, open). The
  conciliarist decree *Haec Sancta* (1415) and conciliar theory as
  constitutional precedent need **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Tierney,
  *Foundations of the Conciliar Theory*; or Ullmann]**.

## 3. The three modern churches, functionally described

Documented from primary sources per the brief. Each is described *functionally*
— canon law (rule-making), sanction regime, jurisdictional claim.

- **The hyperscaler church.** Canon law: platform content policies as
  rule-making instruments — Meta's Community Standards, and the **Meta
  Oversight Board** (its March-2024 Charter, a constituting instrument;
  individual content decisions **binding** on Meta, policy recommendations
  non-binding — a quasi-judicial appellate body). [PRIMARY: Meta Transparency
  Center; Oversight Board Charter.] Sanction regime: **deplatforming as
  excommunication** — Twitter's permanent suspension of @realDonaldTrump (8 Jan
  2021); the Oversight Board's 5 May 2021 ruling upholding the restriction but
  rejecting Meta's "indeterminate and standardless" indefinite penalty; Meta's
  two-year suspension (June 2021). [PRIMARY, all self-published.] *Caveat §0.3:*
  the "interdict/penance" (demonetisation/demotion) sanctions are metaphor
  without a documented instrument.
- **The Chinese state-platform church.** Universal authority *fused* with the
  state. The post-2020 crackdown as intra-church discipline ("Xi's Gregorian
  moment"): the Ant IPO suspension (3 Nov 2020); the Alibaba antitrust fine
  (**RMB 18.228bn**, 4% of 2019 China sales, 10 Apr 2021); the DiDi
  cybersecurity review, app removal (July 2021), NYSE delisting (June 2022) and
  **RMB 8.026bn** fine (21 July 2022); the gaming (30 Aug 2021) and edtech
  "Double Reduction" (24 July 2021) crackdowns; all under the "Common
  Prosperity" doctrine (Xi, 17 Aug 2021, *Qiushi*, primary). Extraterritorial
  apparatus: **PIPL Art. 3** (effective 1 Nov 2021) reaches processing outside
  China aimed at persons *in* China (NPC official translation). [PRIMARY for
  PIPL/*Qiushi*; high-quality secondary for the enforcement events.]
- **The EU regulatory church.** Not a platform but a canonical authority
  asserting jurisdiction over the other two, via market-access conditionality
  (the "Brussels effect," Bradford). Doctrinal apparatus, all EUR-Lex primary:
  GDPR (Reg 2016/679, applicable 25 May 2018, Art. 3 extraterritorial); DMA
  (Reg 2022/1925, gatekeepers designated 6 Sep 2023); DSA (Reg 2022/2065,
  applicable 17 Feb 2024); AI Act (Reg 2024/1689, in force 1 Aug 2024, reaches
  systems whose output is used in the Union). Sanctions: the first DMA
  decisions — Apple €500M, Meta €200M (23 Apr 2025); the first DSA fine — X
  €120M (5 Dec 2025). [PRIMARY: EUR-Lex + Commission press releases.]

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The point is not that these firms are private companies
(they are) but that their *functional role* is that of a universalism —
canonical rule-making, sanction, extraterritorial reach, doctrinal dispute —
"in the same way the medieval Church was a set of institutions long before it
was a coherent legal person" (the outline's answer to the category-error
objection).

## 4. The Great Schism as the structural analogue — the central move

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The chapter's central move (per the outline): there is no
single new Church. There are three rival churches — a US-led hyperscaler bloc,
a Chinese state-platform hybrid, and an EU regulatory church — and the present
is the analogue not of the papal monarchy but of the **Great Schism**: three
universalisms, mutually incompatible, each backed by a civilizational bloc.
Three-body sovereignty is the modal condition. This is well-founded (§0.2) and
does the essential Part III set-up: consolidation happens *inside* each bloc,
not globally. *Discipline:* the parallel is licensed as a shared structural
configuration (three universal claimants + territorial backers), not as
resemblance; keep it structural.

## 5. The Investiture Contest, running now

The specific disputes, each a fight over *who invests an actor with
legitimacy/permission* — primary-sourced:

- **App Store — who invests app publishers with legitimacy.** *Epic Games v.
  Apple* (N.D. Cal. 4:20-cv-05640): 10 Sep 2021 ruling (Apple won 9/10; the
  anti-steering rules held to violate California's UCL); Apple's 27%-commission
  workaround (2024); the 30 Apr 2025 contempt order (wilful violation, criminal
  referral); the Ninth Circuit's ~Dec 2025 affirmance-but-narrowing; SCOTUS
  grant (2026, pending). Parallel EU-DMA track: iOS 17.4 permitting alternative
  marketplaces/sideloading (Mar 2024). [PRIMARY: Apple Newsroom/developer docs;
  **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL/verify]** the 2021 opinion and clean Ninth Circuit
  text — court PDFs did not parse; pull from CourtListener/PACER.]
- **The chip war — who invests AI development with permission.** BIS advanced-
  computing export controls: 7 Oct 2022 (chips + SME thresholds + US-persons
  restriction + Foreign Direct Product Rule expansion); 17 Oct 2023 (the
  "performance-density threshold" catching the A800/H800 workarounds, geographic
  expansion, Entity List additions); 2 Dec 2024 (HBM controls, 140 Entity List
  additions). [PRIMARY: BIS press releases; Federal Register.] *Caveat §0.4:*
  the A100/H100-specific targeting is press-attributed; anchor to ECCN 3A090.
  Note the regime is not static (2025–26 H20/H200 shifts).
- **EU vs US hyperscalers — who has jurisdiction over data.** Schrems II (CJEU
  C-311/18, 16 July 2020, invalidating Privacy Shield); Meta's **€1.2bn** GDPR
  fine (Irish DPC, 22 May 2023, record); the EU-US Data Privacy Framework
  adequacy decision (10 July 2023, resting on EO 14086). [PRIMARY: Irish DPC;
  Commission.]
- **Frontier labs — who may release or withhold a model.** OpenAI's GPT-2
  *staged release* (Feb 2019; full 1.5B weights withheld until ~Nov 2019;
  arXiv 1908.09203, primary) — the origin case of a lab asserting independent
  release authority (contested at the time as a "PR stunt"); the White House
  voluntary AI commitments (21 July 2023, seven labs; +8 on 12 Sep 2023),
  self-adopted release-gating. [PRIMARY: arXiv report; White House archive.]

## 6. What the medieval settlement looked like — the foreshadow

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The Investiture Contest was settled at Worms by
compromise and never fully resolved; the *decisive* settlement was Henry
VIII's, which did not partition the symbols or adjudicate rival claimants but
**absorbed** the rival universalism — the Act in Restraint of Appeals (1533),
declaring "this realm of England is an empire" subject to no external
jurisdiction, and the Act of Supremacy (1534), making the crown "the only
Supreme Head… of the Church of England." [PRIMARY: UK Parliament; Univ. of
Oregon text of the Act.] The chapter foreshadows Part III: the coming state
will not *break* the platforms; it will *nationalise their functions* — the
Henrician, not the Worms, settlement. (Developed in Chapter 11.)
