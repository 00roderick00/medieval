# Critiques — Ch. 2: The New Church

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 were surfaced in
research. Each is steelmanned before the chapter answers it (§6). Because this
is the book's most analogy-dense chapter, several objections turn on the
mechanism-not-analogy discipline (§2). Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The category error: private firms, not churches (named)

**Steelman.** Calling platforms "churches" is a category mistake. Google and
Meta are private corporations, incorporated under national law, taxed by
states, subject to courts, and dissolvable in bankruptcy. The medieval Church
claimed *divinely-ordained* universal authority, ran its own courts and canon
law over a millennium, and answered to no temporal sovereign. A firm whose
terms of service a national court can strike down is not a universal authority;
it is a licensee. The "canon law" of community guidelines is a contract of
adhesion, not a jurisdiction. The metaphor flatters the platforms and inflates
the argument.

**The chapter's answer.** The claim is functional, not ontological. The
question is not whether the platforms are private firms (they are) but whether
their *functional role* is that of an institutional universalism — and the
evidence is that it is: canonical rule-making (Meta's Community Standards; the
Oversight Board's binding case decisions), a sanction regime (deplatforming a
sitting US president across every major platform in 48 hours, January 2021),
extraterritorial reach (GDPR Art. 3; PIPL Art. 3 reaching outside China), and
doctrinal dispute (open-weights vs closed). The medieval Church, too, "was a
set of institutions long before it was a coherent legal person," and it too
operated inside and against temporal powers that could and did seize its lands.
The test the chapter proposes is behavioural: does the actor make binding rules
over a transnational population, sanction the excluded, and claim jurisdiction
beyond any single sovereign's writ? Where the answer is yes, "universalism" is
the accurate functional description, and "private firm" is the incomplete one.

**Residual discipline.** The answer holds only if the draft keeps the parallel
*structural* (a universal authority over the same subjects) and does not slide
into resemblance ("platforms feel medieval"). Any such slide is flagged
[ANALOGY-ONLY] and cut or rewritten (§2). The strongest form of the objection —
that a court *can* strike a platform rule — is not a refutation but the
chapter's own foreshadow: the Investiture Contest was precisely a fight a
temporal power could sometimes win, and the Henrician settlement is where the
state wins decisively (§6). The objection describes the contest; it does not
end it.

## Objection 2 — The Great Schism analogy proves too much

**Steelman.** If the present is "the Great Schism," the analogy predicts the
wrong ending. The Schism was *resolved* — by a council (Constance) that
reasserted a single universal Church, not by three durable blocs. If the
chapter's own best template ends in reunification under conciliar authority,
it undercuts the book's Part III claim that consolidation happens *inside*
three separate blocs. The analogy, taken seriously, points to a single global
settlement (a "Council of Constance" for the platforms — a global governance
regime), which is Slaughter's networked order, not the book's.

**The chapter's answer.** The chapter uses the Schism for its *structural
configuration* (three universalisms, each with a territorial backer), not as a
prophecy of its resolution — and it should say so explicitly to disarm this.
The book's claim is not that the three churches reunify but that each is
*absorbed by its own bloc's state* — the Henrician settlement (§6), not the
conciliar one. Constance is the counter-model the book rejects: a single
supranational settlement is exactly what the book argues will *not* happen,
because the military and legibility stacks concentrate *within* blocs, not
across them (the mechanism, Parts II–III). The draft must not let the Schism
analogy carry its own resolution; it borrows the three-body problem and
supplies a different, mechanism-driven answer. [This is the load-bearing
discipline: the analogy sets the problem; the mechanism sets the outcome.]

## Objection 3 — Three blocs is too neat; the field is messier

**Steelman.** "US hyperscaler / Chinese state-platform / EU regulatory" is a
tidy triad that ignores the actual topology: India (India Stack, a
state-controlled identity/payments layer with none of the three's
characteristics), Russia's sovereign-internet, Gulf sovereign-AI plays, and the
fact that the "EU church" has no platforms of its own to govern — it regulates
*other blocs'* platforms. A three-body model imposed for its resemblance to the
Schism's three popes is numerology, not analysis.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede that three is the *modal*, not exhaustive,
structure, and name the others (India especially, developed in Chs. 11–12) as
candidate fourth churches or as clients. The triad earns its place not because
the Schism had three popes but because three actors currently exhibit the full
functional profile — canon law + sanction + extraterritorial claim — at
continental scale: the US hyperscalers, the Chinese state-platform fusion, and
the EU's canonical apparatus. The EU's peculiarity (canonical authority without
platforms of its own) is not an embarrassment to the model but a feature the
chapter foregrounds — it is the purest "church" of the three, an authority that
is *only* canonical, which is exactly why Chapter 12 flags it as structurally
unstable. The draft should present three as observed, not derived from the
analogy, and mark the number as contingent on the indicators.

## Objection 4 — The sanction metaphor overreaches the evidence

**Steelman.** The chapter wants "excommunication / interdict / penance" =
"deplatforming / demonetisation / algorithmic demotion." But only the first
pair is documented. There is no named, transparent platform instrument called
"demonetisation-as-interdict" or "demotion-as-penance"; down-ranking is opaque,
denied, and unproven. Building a canon-law taxonomy on one documented sanction
and two rhetorical ones is exactly the loose-detail failure that discredits a
causal argument (the ch06 lesson).

**The chapter's answer.** Conceded, and corrected in the memo (§0.3). The draft
leads with the documented sanction — the January 2021 deplatforming and the
Oversight Board process, which are primary-sourced and genuinely striking (a
transnational authority expelling a head of state from communion) — and treats
demonetisation and demotion as *illustrative extensions*, clearly marked as
less-evidenced, not as documented instruments of a canon-law regime. The
taxonomy is suggestive, not load-bearing; the argument rests on the one
sanction that is fully evidenced. The ch06 discipline applies: do not assert an
instrument that cannot be sourced.

## Objection 5 — Ullmann is doing unseen work

**Steelman.** The chapter's interpretive spine — the *Dictatus Papae* as
monarchic sovereignty, Worms as an unresolved stalemate, the Church as
institutional universalism — is credited to Ullmann, who is not in the repo and
was not consulted. The medieval facts are sourced to encyclopedias; the
*interpretations* that make the analogy work are, at present, unsourced
assertions dressed as consensus.

**The chapter's answer.** Correct, and marked: every interpretive claim resting
on Ullmann is flagged [UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL] and will not be stated as
consensus until the work is in hand. The *facts* (Dictatus 1075, Canossa 1077,
Worms 1122, Pisa 1409, Constance 1417, Supremacy 1534) are open-sourced and
solid; the *framing* (these as a recurring structural problem with informative
settlements) is the book's own argument and must be presented as such — (c) not
(a), per §4 — not smuggled in under Ullmann's authority before Ullmann is read.
Until then the draft argues the structural parallel on the open facts and
reserves the scholarly interpretive claims for the retrieval pass.
