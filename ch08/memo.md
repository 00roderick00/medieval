# Research Memo — Ch. 8: Private Justice, Private War

**Chapter weight:** ~11,000 words (Part II). **Memo target:** 4–6k words.
**Function:** the plural legal environment as the operative law of the
dispersal period — and the assertion of royal justice as monopolist shown to
be a *consequence* of the fiscal-military consolidation, not its
precondition. The chapter carries Cerny's sixth characteristic (legal grey
zones and the black economy) from diagnosis (Part I) into mechanism (Part II)
and foreshadows ch. 11's canonical assertion.

**Comparative register (per outline structural note):** primary polity
England (the Pastons); comparators the Empire (*Fehde*/*Landfrieden*) and
Italy (vendetta and the *pax*). Disanalogies flagged in §5 and §6.

Every factual claim is tagged by tier (CLAUDE.md §5) and classified
**[CONSENSUS]**, **[CONTESTED]** (sides named), or **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]**
(§4). Claims resting on gated works are marked **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]**
and carried to `sources.md`.

## 0. Provenance summary — read first

Four findings from the research bear on how this chapter must be drafted.

1. **Cerny 1998 is now IN REPO** (`corpus/retrieved/NeomedievalismCivilWars.pdf`;
   *Civil Wars* 1:1, pp. 36–64) — and it resolves the "six characteristics"
   provenance question that has trailed the project since ch01. The article
   contains **two enumerations**: an inline list of *seven* attributes
   (pp. 45–46) and a run of *six* characteristic **section headings**
   (I: multiple competing institutions, p. 49; II: lack of exogenous
   territorialising pressures, p. 52; III: uneven consolidation of new
   spaces, cleavages, conflicts and inequalities, p. 54; IV: fragmented
   identities, p. 55; V: property rights, p. 56; VI: the spread of *zones
   grises*, p. 57). The outline's six-fold checklist follows the section
   headings, and **the sixth characteristic — this chapter's warrant — is
   section VI, "The Spread of 'Zones Grises'" (pp. 57–58)**, where Cerny
   also carries the black-economy material ("many traditionally mainstream
   social and economic activities will expand as much through grey zones as
   through legitimate means, much as the so-called 'black economy' has
   done," p. 58). The *zones grises* coinage is Alain Minc's (*Le nouveau
   Moyen Âge*, 1993; Cerny's n. 16, p. 61). The draft may now cite all of
   this at page level. A bonus for this chapter specifically: Cerny
   predicted in 1998 that the multilayering of institutions "will lead, at
   best, to the emergence of **quasi-public, quasi-private dispute
   settlement regimes seeking to arbitrate competing claims for rights and
   privileges in this patchwork system**" (p. 52) — the chapter's modern
   half, foreseen inside its own theoretical source. (ch01's post-retrieval
   upgrade pass should absorb the same finding; noted in
   retrieval-master.md.)

2. **The brief's *signorie* claim does not survive verification in its
   stated form.** "Italian vendetta and the signorie's street-level
   tolerance (Muir)" implies a general rule — princely regimes tolerating
   street vendetta while suppressing magnate feud — that no open scholarly
   source states. What the open scholarship supports: communes legislated
   against *magnate* violence (Florence's Ordinances of Justice, 1293)
   while notarised peace contracts absorbed ordinary enmities; Zorzi (open
   access, T2) treats vendetta as a *socially regulated practice within*
   communal legal culture, not simply suppressed crime; and Muir shows the
   Venetian regime in Friuli co-existing with and factionally exploiting
   vendetta. The draft should build the Italian section on
   Zorzi/Muir/Jansen and either drop the tolerated/suppressed asymmetry or
   hold it **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Muir; Dean, *Crime and Justice in
   Late Medieval Italy*]**. Flagged for Roderick (§8: conflict between
   brief wording and source consensus, not silently resolved).

3. **The *lex mercatoria* is contested historiography — and the revisionist
   side strengthens the chapter.** The traditional account (a transnational
   customary merchant law administered in piepowder and fair courts —
   Berman, Benson, Trakman, Milgrom/North/Weingast) is under open,
   quotable attack: Kadens, "The Myth of the Customary Law Merchant"
   (*Texas L. Rev.* 2012, open): "**The law merchant myth is false on many
   levels**… the most widespread aspects of commercial law arose from
   contract and statute rather than custom"; Sachs (open) shows the St
   Ives fair-court records leaving merchants "substantially subject to
   local control." The chapter should take the revisionist side *and use
   it*: the sharpest modern advocates of private ordering rest their case
   on a romanticised medieval private legal order that the medievalists
   have dismantled — which is this chapter's anti-romanticisation argument
   applied to the *modern* material. Detail in §4. (Baker's 1979 CLJ
   statement of the sceptical position: gated, retrieval.)

4. **Smaller corrections to carry into the draft.** (a) The 1487 act (3
   Hen. VII c. 1) did **not** create Star Chamber; the "pro camera
   stellata" tag is a sixteenth-century annotation and the court evolved
   from the council's judicial business (open-access chapter in Kesselring
   & Mears, *Star Chamber Matters*, T2). (b) The much-quoted "90% of
   cross-border contracts contain arbitration clauses" is not a
   contract-drafting statistic: the QMUL/White & Case survey figure is a
   *respondent-preference* number (90% of surveyed practitioners preferred
   arbitration in 2021; 87% in 2025). (c) Apple's App Store transparency
   report exists under the 2021 *Cameron v. Apple* small-developer
   settlement, not the Epic judgment. (d) Muir's verified superlative for
   Udine 1511 is "the most extensive popular **revolt** in Renaissance
   Italy," not "largest massacre." (e) Sanctuary: the decisive statute is
   32 Hen. VIII c. 12 (1540); crime sanctuary was abolished under 21 Jac.
   I c. 28 (session of 1623–24).

## 1. The English feud tradition: the Pastons

**The corpus itself is open T1.** Gairdner's six-volume edition of the
Paston Letters (1904) is public domain (archive.org; Project Gutenberg).
The Davis edition (OUP 1971–76) remains gated. All quotations below were
verified against the open Gairdner text.

**Gresham, 1448–49. [CONSENSUS on the fact-pattern; T1 for the detail.]**
Lord Moleyns seized the Paston manor of Gresham in 1448 (instigated by
John Heydon of Baconsthorpe). John Paston re-entered on 6 October 1448 and
held the mansion until, per his own petition, on 28 January 1449 Moleyns
"sent to the seid mansion a riotous peple, to the nombre of a thowsand
persones… with curesse, brigaunders, jakks… gonnes… long cromes to drawe
doun howsis… with which thei myned down the walles… and so came in to the
seid mansion, the wiff of your besecher at that tyme beyng ther in, and
xij. persones with her; the which persones thei dreve oute" (T1, Gairdner
vol. II). **Source-criticism to state in the draft:** the "thousand
persones" is the Pastons' partisan claim in a petition, not an independent
count; tertiary sites even disagree on the date (the petition's own words
carry 28 January).

**Why the law failed him — the thesis in one sentence, from 1449. [T1]**
Paston's petition: "your seid besecher is not abille to sue the commone
lawe in redressyng of this heynos wrong, **for the gret myght and alyaunce
of the seid Lord**." Recovery followed not a verdict but a political
event: the fall and killing of the Duke of Suffolk (1450), which stripped
Moleyns of protection.

**Royal justice as a factional resource. [T1 — the cleanest primary
evidence located this pass.]** John Osbern to John Paston, May 1451
(Gairdner vol. II, no. 193): the sheriff of Norfolk "wold do for yow that
he may, excepte for the aquitell of the Lord Molyns men, in so meche as
**the Kyng hath wrete to hym for to shewe favour to the Lord Moleyns and
hese men**… Also he seythe that the Lord Molyns hath sent hym a letter,
and my Lord of Norffolk anoder, for to shew favour in these indytements,
he darnot abide the joporte of that, that he shuld offende the Kinges
commaundment." The crown's writ, the magnate's letter, and the sheriff's
fear operate as one system. The statutory vocabulary of the abuse appears
in Paston's own bill to the Chancellor (c. 1450): an oyer and determiner
sought into "trespaces, extorcions, riottes, forcible entrees,
**mayntenaunces, champerties, embraceries**, offenses, and mesprisions"
(T1). Cross-ref ch03 §4 (the retention instruments): maintenance and
embracery are the *judicial* face of the same affinity system.

**Fastolf to Caister, 1459–1469. [CONSENSUS core; figures
source-criticised.]** Sir John Fastolf died 5 November 1459 leaving a
contested nuncupative will (Paston claiming the Norfolk/Suffolk lands for
4,000 marks plus a college at Caister); the excluded executors fought it
for years. In August–September 1469 the Duke of Norfolk besieged Caister
Castle: c. five weeks (21 August to 26/27 September), defended by John
Paston the younger and about thirty men, artillery used on both sides, at
least one defender killed. The "3,000 besiegers" is John Paston's own
figure; historians suggest nearer a tenth of it (T3) — attribute, never
assert. **DeVries (T2, IN REPO) supplies the artillery dimension and its
significance:** among his catalogue of English private gunpowder war —
the 1443 Norwich riots, Mowbray's guns against Wingfield's house at
Letheringham, the 1455 siege of Powderham, **Nibley Green 1469 (the
Berkeley–Talbot feud "ended… with gunpowder weaponry bombardments between
the two factions")**, the 1471 siege of Hornby Castle taken with "the
cannon *Mile End*" — sits Caister: Norfolk's guns against Sir John
Paston's castle, with the note that "John Paston was forced to surrender
his own gunpowder weapons to the duke of Norfolk once his castle had
fallen" (DeVries 1998, p. 144 and n. 94, citing Gairdner). Private war in
England was conducted with artillery *by subjects against subjects*
"without ever involving the king" (p. 144). Cross-ref ch10: this is the
same evidence DeVries uses for England's failed royal monopoly of the
gun, and ch05's hollow-sovereignty diagnosis seen from below.

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The Pastons are the paradigm case not because their
troubles were exceptional but because their coping was normal: a
propertied family navigating king's bench, chancery, church courts (the
Fastolf will), county sessions, magnate good-lordship and, at Caister,
siege warfare, as *alternative instruments in a single repertoire*. Where
the brief says "access to royal justice was itself a factional resource,"
the Osbern letter lets the draft show it in the sheriff's own words.

## 2. The Empire: *Fehde* and *Landfrieden*

**The feud as legal institution. [CONSENSUS on the rule; T1 for the
text.]** The Golden Bull of 1356, ch. 17, invalidated challenges issued
without notice: a feud is lawful only if "the challenge, **three natural
days before, shall have been intimated personally to the challenged man
himself, or publicly in the place where he has been accustomed to
reside**" (T1, Avalon Project translation). The *Absage* (formal
defiance) made the difference between lawful feud and mere robbery — the
plural legal environment regulating, not forbidding, private war.

**The historiographical stakes. [CONTESTED — sides named.]** Otto Brunner
(*Land und Herrschaft*, 1939; Eng. *Land and Lordship*, 1992) argued the
feud was a *Rechtsinstitut* — a coherent legal order rooted in noble
lordship, not anarchy. Gadi Algazi's counter (*Herrengewalt und Gewalt
der Herren*, 1996): Brunner read his sources through a fascist-era lens,
and the feud was lordly violence whose costs fell on peasants. Oexle and
others hold a contextualising middle. Hillay Zmora's two monographs
(1997, 2011) reframe the Franconian evidence: feuds arose from "an
interplay between two fundamental processes: **princely state-building
and social stratification among the nobility**," conducted "not against
strangers but with neighbours, relatives and their feudal lords"
(publisher/review wording; books gated). **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
Zmora both titles; Algazi; Brunner for direct quotation.]** The draft
should stage this debate briefly and honestly: whichever side one takes,
nobody's feud was the war of all against all — it was *jurisdiction*,
claimed and exercised privately. That serves the chapter better than the
romantic-anarchy picture, and it parallels §4's law-merchant correction.

**The public-sword assertion, 1495 — and its limits. [CONSENSUS]** The
Diet of Worms under Maximilian I enacted the *Ewiger Landfriede*,
"noteworthy for its **definitive abolition of the right of feud**"
(GHDI/Brady translation, T2), paired with the creation of the
*Reichskammergericht* — a standing supreme court detached from the
emperor's person: prohibition and institutional substitute in the same
diet. And yet feuding persisted well into the sixteenth century — the
career of Götz von Berlichingen (c. 1480–1562) is the standard
demonstration: the 1512 attack on Nuremberg merchants, imperial ban,
release for 14,000 gulden (1514); second ban (1518); imprisonment and the
2,000-gulden oath (1519–22); by his own memoir (written c. 1560s,
published 1731; open in the 1859 German edition) some fifteen feuds in
his own name. **[T3/T1]** The Empire is the chapter's control case: where
the fiscal-military consolidation was weakest, the paper abolition of
private war ran a century ahead of the enforceable fact — which is the
close's argument (§6) stated negatively.

## 3. Italy: vendetta and the *pax*

**Udine, 1511. [CONSENSUS core; superlative corrected.]** On Giovedì
Grasso (27 February) 1511 the Zambarlani faction under Antonio Savorgnan
— pro-Venetian, with peasant support — massacred and burned out the
Strumieri nobility (Della Torre, Colloredo) in Udine, the violence
spreading to castle-sackings across Friuli; Muir's verified superlative
is "the most extensive popular **revolt** in Renaissance Italy" (JHU/H-Net
wording; the book is gated). The sequel is the chapter's point: Savorgnan
was himself murdered by Strumieri avengers outside a church at Villach,
27 May 1512 — vendetta answered through vendetta, across a border, under
Venetian and imperial jurisdictions at once. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
Muir, *Mad Blood Stirring*, for narrative detail and exact wording.]**

**The scholarly texture. [T2]** Dean ("Marriage and Mutilation," *Past &
Present* 157, 1997) shows Bolognese and Florentine vendetta narratives
more law-entangled and restrained than the literary image; Zorzi (open
access) treats vendetta as a *practice regulated within* communal legal
culture — timing, targets and settlements all rule-bound. Jansen
(*Peace and Penance in Late Medieval Italy*, 2018, gated) anchors the
institutional counterpart: the notarised *instrumentum pacis*, with **526
peace agreements in the Florentine notarial records, 1257–1343** — private
dispute settlement as a mass documentary practice, penitential in origin,
contractual in form. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Jansen for the dataset's
use at page level.]**

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT — reformulated per §0.2]** The Italian case shows the
plural legal environment at its most institutionalised: vendetta as
regulated self-help, the *pax* as its notarised settlement instrument,
communal statute as one layer among several. The draft should *not*
assert the tolerated-below/suppressed-above rule the brief sketches;
pending Muir/Dean retrieval it should say what the sources say — that
communes and later princely regimes governed *through* the management of
enmity as much as through its abolition.

## 4. The plural foundation: sanctuary, clergy, law merchant, admiralty

**Sanctuary. [CONSENSUS]** Any felon could take refuge in any church for
up to forty days, then abjure the realm before the coroner "clothed in
sackcloth"; chartered sanctuaries (Westminster, Durham, Beverley, St
Martin le Grand — at least twenty-two houses) offered permanent refuge.
Scale is now quantified in open scholarship: McSheffrey's project counts
**1,800+ sanctuary seekers, 1394–1557**; the Durham register alone,
1464–1524, records "petitions from 283 men who admitted to involvement in
195 homicides" (T2). The Tudor erosion: a cluster of statutes 1529–36,
then 32 Hen. VIII c. 12 (1540) abolishing most sanctuaries, stripping the
privilege from murder, rape, burglary, highway robbery and arson, and
designating eight sanctuary towns; final abolition for crime under 21
Jac. I c. 28 (1623–24). **[T2/T3; the "1536" discrete act NOT FOUND
openly — retrieval note in sources.md.]**

**Benefit of clergy. [CONSENSUS]** The neck-verse (Psalm 51, *Miserere
mei Deus*); the literate walked. Tudor restriction: 4 Hen. VII c. 13
(1489) — one use for laymen, branded on the thumb; 4 Hen. VIII c. 2
(1512) removed it for murder and robbery — the statute that detonated the
Standish affair (1515) and intersected the Hunne case (Richard Hunne
found hanged in the Lollards' Tower, December 1514): a
state–church jurisdictional collision over who may judge whom,
prefiguring ch11's canonical assertion. Abolition: 1827. **[T3; the
Standish/Hunne scholarly literature is gated — retrieval.]**

**The law merchant. [CONTESTED — the chapter's historiographical set
piece; sides named.]** Traditional account: a uniform, transnational,
customary *lex mercatoria*, administered by merchants in piepowder and
fair courts, the medieval ancestor of modern arbitration (Berman, *Law
and Revolution*; Benson; Trakman; Milgrom/North/Weingast on the Champagne
fairs' "private judges"). Revisionist demolition, fully open: **Kadens**,
"The Myth of the Customary Law Merchant" (2012): "The law merchant myth
is false on many levels, but this Article takes aim at two of its
fundamental principles: **that uniform and universal customary merchant
law could have existed and that merchants needed it to exist**… the most
widespread aspects of commercial law arose from contract and statute
rather than custom." **Sachs** on the St Ives fair court (the fullest
surviving records): merchants "substantially subject to local control,"
custom varying town by town. Baker's earlier scepticism (law merchant as
expeditious *procedure* within ordinary courts, not a separate
substantive law) is the standard English statement — gated.
**[RETRIEVAL: Baker 1979; Berman for fair statement of the traditional
side in its own words.]** Why this matters to the chapter — see §5: the
modern arbitration order cites the myth as its pedigree.

**Admiralty. [CONSENSUS]** The English Admiralty court emerges after
Sluys (1340), consolidated c. 1360, civil-law in procedure; the common
lawyers caged it early — 13 Ric. II c. 5 (1389): the admirals "shall not
meddle from henceforth of anything done within the realm, but only of a
thing done upon the sea" (T1/T3). Jurisdictional boundary-drawing between
the king's own courts: plurality inside the crown itself.

**The frame. [T2, open]** Tom Johnson (*Law in Common*, OUP 2020, gated;
author's open statement): "Late-medieval England was a world of a
thousand law-courts… tribunals for business and trade ('piepowder'
courts…), for craft guilds, and for borough custom, as well as… church
courts… **This was a society of dense legal pluralism, with thousands of
overlapping and occasionally conflicting institutions.**" The draft can
rest the navigational claim — that a fifteenth-century merchant or
gentleman worked multiple jurisdictions as a matter of course — on
Johnson's statement plus the Paston corpus at T1. "Medieval regulatory
arbitrage" survives, but as *jurisdictional* plurality (forum-shopping
among real courts), not as a coherent private substantive law.

## 5. The modern parallel: the sixth characteristic in operation

**The theoretical warrant, now page-citable. [T2, IN REPO]** Cerny 1998,
section VI (pp. 57–58): in a neomedieval world there will be "increased
escape routes– and organisational opportunities – for those operating
more or less 'outside the law'. **Exit from political society is likely
to become a more viable option for a wider range of actors and
activities**"; grey zones span urban ghettoes, inaccessible enclaves, and
the point "where the resources and networks of the drugs trade… extend
into state bureaucracies and 'legitimate' private firms"; and "many
traditionally mainstream social and economic activities will expand as
much through grey zones as through legitimate means, much as the
so-called 'black economy' has done" (p. 58). Add the p. 52 prediction
quoted in §0.1: quasi-public, quasi-private dispute settlement regimes as
the *best case* of neomedieval institutional evolution. The chapter's
modern half is the audit of that prediction, twenty-eight years on.

**Arbitration as the operative forum for high-value commerce. [T1
figures, all dated.]** ICC (2024): 831 new arbitration cases; aggregate
in dispute in new cases US$103bn; **pending caseload US$354bn — the ICC's
own "all-time record"**. LCIA (2024): 362 referrals, "95% of cases…
international," parties from 101 jurisdictions. HKIAC (2024): 503 cases;
arbitration amount in dispute HK$106bn (~US$13.6bn). SIAC (2024): 625 new
cases, ~SGD 16.12bn (T3 pending the primary PDF). The New York Convention
stands at **172 contracting states** (UNCITRAL, checked July 2026) — the
enforcement lattice that makes the private award run. The famous "90%"
figure must be given honestly: QMUL/White & Case survey respondents
*preferring* arbitration for cross-border disputes — 90% (2021), 87%
(2025) — a preference statistic among the arbitration community, not a
count of contracts. **[Correction per §0.4.]**

**ISDS. [T1]** UNCTAD's Navigator: **1,440 known treaty-based
investor–state arbitrations as of 31 July 2025**; ICSID: 1,085 registered
cases cumulatively by 31 December 2025, with 63 new in calendar 2025, its
second-highest year. A parallel judiciary for capital against states,
built out of the same instruments (the *compromis*, the standing offer in
treaty) the medieval merchant would have recognised as franchise
jurisdiction.

**Platform adjudication at scale. [T1, from the institutions' own
reports.]** Meta's Oversight Board — charter September 2019, first
members May 2020, first decisions January 2021, financed through an
irrevocable trust ($130M initial, $150M added 2022): in 2023 it "received
398,597 appeals" and "issued 53 decisions"; in 2024, 558,235 appeals and
65 decisions, overturning Meta in ~90% of the cases it took. Content
decisions are binding on Meta by charter; policy recommendations are
advisory. Beneath the appellate spire, the mass jurisdiction: Meta
"removed millions of pieces of content every day" (December 2024, Meta's
own statement, which also concedes "one to two out of every 10 of these
actions may have been mistakes"). Apple's App Store Transparency Report
(calendar 2024, under the *Cameron v. Apple* settlement): **7,771,599
app submissions reviewed; 1,931,400 rejected; 82,509 apps removed; 26,224
removal appeals; 421 restorations; 128,961,839 customer accounts
terminated**. The scale of a jurisdiction; the process of a helpdesk.
Cross-ref ch02 (sanction regime as excommunication/interdict): this is
that canon law's *court* system, quantified. Amazon: after "more than
75,000 individual arbitration demands" over Alexa recordings, it deleted
the mandatory-arbitration clause from its Conditions of Use (May–July
2021) — mass arbitration as a weaponised private process forcing retreat
*to the public courts* (WSJ's Randazzo broke it; paywalled — retrieval).

**The crypto legal experiment. [T1 core]** Lessig's dictum ("This
regulator is code — the software and hardware that make cyberspace as it
is," 1999/2000) became the movement's constitutional theory; its first
hard case broke it. The DAO: ~3.6M ETH (~$50M then) drained June 2016;
the Ethereum Foundation's "Hard Fork Completed" (20 July 2016, block
1,920,000) unwound the theft by social consensus — code-is-law suspended
by its own community the first time the code produced an intolerable
result. Since then: Kleros (juried token-staked arbitration; ~1,000+
cases by late 2021, T2); Wyoming's DAO LLC statute (SF0038, effective 1
July 2021) — the chain seeking the state's wrapper; DIFC's Digital
Economy Court (Dubai, 2021–22) — an offshore *state* court competing for
the business. The experiment's arc bends toward re-incorporation.

**The offshore grey zone — under visible state assertion. [T1]** EU Tax
Observatory, *Global Tax Evasion Report 2024*: "Thanks to the automatic
exchange of bank information, **offshore tax evasion has declined by a
factor of about three in less than 10 years**. Before 2013, households
owned the equivalent of 10% of world GDP in financial wealth in tax
havens globally, the bulk of which was undeclared… Today there is still
the equivalent of 10% of world GDP in offshore household financial
wealth, but in our central scenario only about 25% of it evades
taxation"; ~$12.6tn was reported to foreign tax authorities under the CRS
in 2022. The stock persists; the *untaxed fraction* collapses — Cerny's
grey zone being made legible without being abolished. (Cross-ref ch01's
C1-3 indicator; the untaxed-fraction series is the discriminating
number — carried to appendix-c.)

**Pistor as bridge text. [T2 via open excerpts; book gated.]** *The Code
of Capital* (2019): capital is coded in the modules of private law —
"property and collateral law, corporate, trust, and bankruptcy as well as
contract law" — and global capitalism "is built around **two domestic
legal systems, the laws of England and those of New York State**" (p.
132), with the ISDA Master Agreement directing parties to choose exactly
those two. **[RETRIEVAL: the book, for the "empire of law" passage at
page level.]**

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT — the disanalogy, stated per §6 doctrine]** The modern
plural legal environment differs from the fifteenth century's in three
connected ways, and the draft must say so. It is (mostly) non-violent;
its fora are parasitic on state law rather than rivals to it (Pistor's
two anchor jurisdictions; the New York Convention's 172 states as the
award's enforcement machinery; the DAO's flight into a Wyoming LLC); and
its grey zones are being made legible in real time (the Tax Observatory
numbers). The medieval analogy is therefore strongest not at "private
justice = feud" but at the structural level Cerny specifies: plural,
overlapping, competing jurisdictions, with the enforcement business
migrating to fora the parties choose. The feud's true modern analogue is
not arbitration but the zones where enforcement still runs on violence —
the interstitial spaces of ch11–12.

## 6. The public sword: monopoly as consequence, not precondition

**The medieval close. [CONSENSUS, with the §0.4(a) correction]** The
sixteenth-century assertion of royal justice was built *after* and *on*
the fiscal-military consolidation:

- **England:** Star Chamber — evolved from the council's judicial
  business (the 1487 act did not create it; the "pro camera stellata" tag
  is a later annotation); Council of the North (origins in Richard of
  Gloucester's council of the 1470s–80s; permanent Tudor form 1537, after
  the Pilgrimage of Grace); Council of Wales and the Marches (1473,
  statutory footing 1543); Court of Requests (usage from Richard III;
  first reference to a "court of requests" 1485). Add the Tudor sanctuary
  and benefit-of-clergy statutes of §4.
- **France:** the provincial parlements as the 15th-century royal-justice
  build-out — Toulouse (definitively 1443), Grenoble (1453, by the
  dauphin Louis), Bordeaux (1462), **Dijon (1477 — planted in the
  conquered duchy the year Charles the Bold fell at Nancy; cross-ref
  ch10)**.
- **Spain:** the *audiencia*/chancillería of Valladolid (1371),
  reorganised by the Catholic Monarchs (1485), a second seat at Ciudad
  Real (1494) moved to Granada (1505); the Santa Hermandad instituted at
  the Cortes of Madrigal (April 1476) as royal constabulary.
- **The Empire (the control case):** the 1495 *Ewiger
  Landfriede* + *Reichskammergericht* — the assertion made *without* the
  fiscal-military substrate, and therefore a century of paper supremacy
  (§2).

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Sequence is the argument: the courts follow the
companies and the *taille*, not the reverse. England's assertion
completes only under the Tudors (ch11); France's tracks the ordonnance
state decade by decade; Spain's arrives with the Catholic Monarchs'
consolidation; the Empire asserts without the substrate and fails to
enforce. The plural legal environment was not argued out of existence; it
was outspent.
