# Critiques — Ch. 8: Private Justice, Private War

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test (the romanticisation
complaint, inverted per the brief). Objections 2–5 surfaced in research.
Each steelmanned before the answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The romanticisation complaint (named; inverted)

**Steelman.** Chapters like this one invariably end up romanticising the
plural legal environment: the plucky Pastons navigating their options, the
feud as a colourful *Rechtsinstitut*, the law merchant as bottom-up order,
vendetta as social regulation — a medieval libertarian idyll against which
the Leviathan's monopoly looks like loss. And the modern half compounds
it: describing arbitration, platform courts and crypto experiments in the
same register invites the reading that private ordering is a natural and
adequate substitute for public justice. The historian's complaint and the
political complaint converge: the actual operation of feud and vendetta
was violent, arbitrary and grim, and any chapter that files them under
"legal pluralism" has prettified them.

**The chapter's answer — the inversion, run all the way through.** The
chapter *agrees*, and builds on the agreement. Its thesis is that the
plural environment was grim precisely because enforcement tracked private
power: Margaret Paston was carried out of Gresham by armed men and her
husband could not "sue the commone lawe… for the gret myght and alyaunce
of the seid Lord" — the sheriff himself, in a private letter, pleads the
king's interference (memo §1, both T1). The erosion of that environment
was a genuine achievement of the consolidating state, and the chapter
says so flatly. The inversion then does the forward work: if the plural
environment was grim, its return should be examined in the same light,
not celebrated as innovation. The chapter's three-part disanalogy (memo
§5) is the discipline that keeps the inversion honest: the modern plural
environment is mostly non-violent, state-parasitic, and increasingly
legible — so the indictment is not "arbitration = feud" but that forum
migration re-prices justice by private power, which is exactly what the
fifteenth century teaches. Romanticism is refused in both directions: no
idyll of the feud, and no idyll of the monopolist state either — the
public sword of §6 arrives as the instrument of the fiscal-military
consolidation, not as a moral hero.

## Objection 2 — The chapter's own medievalism is a myth (the Kadens problem)

**Steelman.** The chapter mocks modern private ordering for citing a
mythical medieval law merchant — while itself running a chapter-length
medieval analogy for modern private justice. If Kadens and Sachs are
right that there was no transnational customary merchant law, then
"medieval regulatory arbitrage" is equally a modern projection, and the
chapter saws off the branch it sits on. One cannot deploy the
myth-busting literature against ISDS apologists and then keep the myth's
furniture — piepowder courts, forum-shopping merchants — as one's own
analogical stock.

**The chapter's answer.** The claims are different in kind, and the
chapter must keep them visibly distinct. What Kadens demolishes is a
*substantive* claim: a uniform, universal, custom-generated commercial
law independent of polities. What the chapter asserts is a
*jurisdictional* claim: that many real courts — royal, ecclesiastical,
manorial, borough, staple, admiralty — operated concurrently, and that
litigants moved among them. That claim does not rest on the myth; it
rests on the institutional record and is stated in exactly those terms by
the current specialist literature (Johnson: "a world of a thousand
law-courts… dense legal pluralism," memo §4) and demonstrated at T1 by
the Paston corpus. Kadens herself does not deny the courts existed; she
denies their law was a coherent private *lex*. The chapter's modern
parallel is likewise jurisdictional (forum migration to arbitration,
platform process, offshore courts), not a claim that a new universal
private law has formed — indeed Pistor's point, which the chapter
adopts, is that the "private" order runs on two *state* legal systems.
The draft states this distinction once, explicitly, where the Kadens
material is introduced (memo §4 caution).

## Objection 3 — Nothing here is neomedieval: the state never left

**Steelman.** Every modern institution the chapter cites is a creature of
state law. Arbitration awards run only through the New York Convention —
a treaty among 172 *states* — and are set aside by state courts; ISDS
exists by treaty and is being dismantled by treaty (Achmea, the ECT
exit); the Oversight Board is corporate governance, DSA Article 21 bodies
are state-certified and expressly non-binding; the DAO fled into a
Wyoming LLC; even the offshore system is being drained by an
OECD-coordinated information-exchange regime — the Tax Observatory's own
headline is that evasion fell by a factor of three. Cerny's sixth
characteristic, applied to this material, is a category error: this is
not a plural legal environment; it is a single state-law environment with
subcontractors. The chapter's premise dissolves on its own evidence.

**The chapter's answer.** The objection restates the chapter's close as
if it were a refutation of its opening. Two replies. First, the medieval
plural environment was *also* formally under a single supreme
jurisdiction — the king's peace covered England in 1450 as completely on
parchment as state law covers commerce now; the Empire "abolished" the
feud in 1495 and Götz feuded for another generation. Pluralism is a
claim about where the enforcement business actually goes, not about
formal supremacy — and the caseload numbers (ICC's record $354bn
pending; 1,440 ISDS cases; half a million platform appeals against
sixty-five decisions) show where it goes. Second, the state's current
reach is not a rebuttal of the chapter but its final section: the
re-assertion (Achmea → ECT exit, DSA 20–21, DMA fines, MiCA, Binance) is
*dated, recent, and incomplete* — it is the beginning of the settlement
the book predicts, arriving in the sequence the book predicts
(consolidation first, judicial monopoly after). What would actually
falsify the chapter is the objection being true *throughout the period*:
if the enforcement business had never migrated, there would be nothing
to re-assert over. The 10%-of-world-GDP offshore stock, still standing
while its untaxed fraction collapses, is the two halves of the chapter
in one statistic.

## Objection 4 — Platform "adjudication" is customer service, not jurisdiction

**Steelman.** Calling App Review or content moderation "private justice"
inflates a helpdesk into a court. There is no lis inter partes, no
independent adjudicator, no law but a changeable policy document, and no
finality — Apple restored 421 of 26,224 appealed removals, which is not a
justice system's reversal rate but a vendor's discretion. The Oversight
Board hears 0.01% of appeals and can be defunded by its founder. A
jurisdiction whose "judgments" are terms-of-service enforcement is a
metaphor, and the chapter's own ch02 already spent the canon-law
metaphor; running it again here is double-counting.

**The chapter's answer.** The chapter's claim is functional, and the
numbers are the argument, not the embarrassment. For the merchant on
Amazon, the developer on iOS, the publisher on YouTube, the *operative*
determination of property and livelihood — can I trade, is my stock
frozen, is my channel demonetised — is made by these processes and
almost never reviewed elsewhere: 7.77 million submissions and 128.9
million account terminations against 421 restorations is precisely the
point, the scale of a jurisdiction with the process of a helpdesk. That
is what "legal grey zone" means in Cerny's sixth characteristic: rule
and sanction without the rule-of-law apparatus. The Board's 0.01% is
cited *for* the argument (an appellate spire on a mass jurisdiction, of
which it overturns ~90% of what it examines — evidence of the error rate
below); the DSA's compulsory internal complaints and certified-but-
non-binding external bodies are the state conceding both that the
adjudication is real and that it must be caged. And the distinction from
ch02 is kept: ch02 established the *doctrinal* apparatus (canon law,
excommunication); this chapter counts the *enforcement business* — the
same division the book uses between the medieval Church's law and its
courts. The disanalogy (contract not sacrament; exit exists in principle)
is stated per §6; passages leaning on the robes rather than the function
are [ANALOGY-ONLY].

## Objection 5 — Partisan sources, English weighting

**Steelman.** The chapter's most vivid evidence is special pleading
preserved by accident: the Pastons' own petitions (a thousand attackers!
three thousand besiegers!), a sheriff's reported excuses, Götz's
self-serving memoir written for posterity, Muir's massacre known chiefly
through one historian's gated monograph. The comparative register leans
on England because England kept the paper; the Empire and Italy sections
ride on translated set pieces. A chapter about the unreliability of
partisan justice is built on partisan documents.

**The chapter's answer.** Conceded as to the documents; managed as to the
method — and the concession is itself material. The memo applies source
criticism in-line: the "thousand persones" and "3,000" are flagged as the
Pastons' own numbers and are to be attributed, never asserted; the
sheriff's words arrive through Osbern's report and are cited as such;
Götz's fifteen feuds are counted from his own memoir with its date and
publication history given. Partisan provenance is not a defect here but
part of the phenomenon: in a plural legal environment, the petition *is*
the legal instrument — pleading to chancery in exactly the register the
chapter quotes was how the system was worked. On the weighting: the
Empire and Italy carry the two structural points England cannot (the
feud as codified legal institution with statutory notice periods; the
notarised pax as mass private settlement), each on open T1/T2 anchors
(Golden Bull ch. 17; GHDI 1495; Zorzi; the Jansen dataset via reviews),
with the gated monographs on the retrieval list rather than silently
paraphrased. That is the Batch-1/2 discipline (the ch09 "Kantorowicz at
second hand" answer) applied again: bounded, declared exposure, primary
evidence first-hand where it is load-bearing.
