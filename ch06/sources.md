## T1 — Primary sources

- **J. Stevenson (ed.), *Letters and Papers Illustrative of the Wars of the
  English in France during the Reign of Henry the Sixth*** (Rolls Series,
  1861–64), p. 619 et seq. — the contemporary English document listing **one
  hundred strongpoints taken by the French in 1450**. *Cited here via Rogers
  (fn 108), NOT consulted directly.* The Rolls Series volumes are open-access
  (archive.org); a direct pass would let the list be cited first-hand and the
  named places (Château Gaillard, St-Sauveur-le-Vicomte, Cherbourg,
  Roche-Guyon, Rouen) verified. → carried to RETRIEVAL for direct consultation.
- **Jean Chartier, *Chronique de Charles VII, roi de France*** — praises the
  Bureau brothers' "approaches, fosses, entrenchments and mines" before the
  besieged towns; primary attestation of the siege-engineering competence
  behind the campaign's speed. Open-access (Gallica: gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/
  bpt6k277120; Internet Archive: archive.org/details/chroniquedecharl01char),
  Middle French, **not yet parsed line-by-line** — flagged for a French-reading
  pass before quotation.
- **[*Le Recouvrement de Normandie*, attrib. Berry Herald (Gilles le
  Bouvier)]** — the specialist chronicle of the 1449–50 reconquest; academic
  discussion open at books.openedition.org/puc/11912. Primary source for the
  campaign narrative; not yet consulted.

## T2 — Canonical scholarship (open-access, consulted)

- **Clifford J. Rogers, "The Military Revolutions of the Hundred Years' War,"
  *Journal of Military History* 57 (1993).** Open access at De Re Militari
  (deremilitari.org/2014/06/the-military-revolutions-of-the-hundred-years-war/).
  **The load-bearing source of this chapter.** Supports: (a) the "one hundred
  strongpoints in 1450" figure and the "most surrendered rather than resist"
  caveat (fn 108, Stevenson); (b) the temporal-compression data — Bayeux
  (16-day siege, wall "pierced and brought down"), Blaye 1451 (5 days), Dax
  and Acx; (c) the artillery-revolution dating (1420s–1440s, gunpowder
  reversing the defensive dominance in siege warfare); (d) **the fiscal
  mechanism in a sceptic's own words** — "Artillery was expensive… As early as
  1442, the French government was spending more than twice as much on its
  artillery train as… traditional war materiel… central governments of large
  states could afford… their subjects and smaller neighbors… could not";
  (e) the punctuated-equilibrium sequence (Infantry → Artillery → Artillery-
  Fortress → administrative revolutions), which is the substance of the named
  objection engaged in critiques.md. *Note:* Rogers is simultaneously the
  chapter's objector (infantry-revolution priority) and its best witness.
- **Encyclopædia Britannica** — "Battle of Formigny"; "Battle of Castillon";
  "military technology / the castle"; "Battle of France (World War II)".
  Encyclopedic (T2-ish). **Fetch-blocked (HTTP 403); key wording captured via
  search snippets only.** Every Britannica-sourced line in the memo is marked
  "confirm wording in browser" and must not be quoted verbatim until confirmed.
  Supports: Formigny as "the first occasion of the French use of field
  artillery" (a "first" claim — corroborate before print); castle design
  baseline; the "just over six weeks" fall-of-France framing.
- **US Holocaust Memorial Museum, Holocaust Encyclopedia, "German Invasion of
  Western Europe, May 1940."** encyclopedia.ushmm.org. Authoritative. Supports
  the 1940 timeline and the "just over six weeks" figure (10 May → 22 June).
- **UK Parliament, House of Commons Library research briefing CBP-9299
  (Afghanistan, 2021).** Open PDF (researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk).
  Authoritative. Supports the August 2021 Kabul timeline and the intelligence-
  assessment-vs-actual-pace point.

## T3 — Serious trade / synthesis / reputable reference (consulted)

- **Bret Devereaux, "Collections: Fortification, Part IV — French Guns and
  Italian Lines," ACOUP (acoup.blog, 2021).** Specialist academic historian's
  blog; reliable for the *mechanism* (tall-thin stone walls shattering under
  battering). **Caveat:** the vivid "few Hours vs the like Number of Days"
  quantification is Guicciardini on **Charles VIII's 1494** campaign, not
  1449–53 — mechanism only, never a Bureau-era datum.
- **English Heritage** — castle design and medieval siege practice (blockade/
  starvation as the type-case). Supporting colour for the fortification
  baseline.
- **britishbattles.com** — "Battle of Formigny"; "Battle of Castillon".
  Detailed popular history. Supports narrative detail (Talbot's death sequence;
  the entrenched Castillon park; "probably around 200" guns — the low end of
  the contested count). Not for load-bearing figures.
- **longbow-archers.com** — "Battle of Formigny". Unusually precise and
  *sceptical* on the guns' decisiveness ("decisive only in that their noise
  located the English position"). Used to keep the Formigny claim calibrated.
- **Encyclopedia.com / Gale, "The Invention of Gunpowder and Its Introduction
  to Europe."** Supports corned powder dating ("shortly after 1400") as a
  general development.
- **medievalhistory.info, "How France won the Hundred Years' War."** Popular,
  coherent, **uncited** — the Rouen 1418 (5 months) vs 1449 (under a month)
  contrast is taken from here and flagged for verification against Curry/
  Allmand.
- **History.com, "Kabul falls to Taliban"** — supporting timeline for August
  2021.
- **Washington Post, "The shocking speed of the Taliban's advance" (16 Aug
  2021)** — reputable, **paywalled**; supporting colour only.

## T4 — Unverified web / tertiary (pointers only; never sole support)

- **Wikipedia:** "Jean Bureau"; "Gaspard Bureau"; "Battle of Formigny";
  "Battle of Castillon"; "Normandy campaign of 1449–1450"; "Siege of Falaise
  (1450)"; "Siege of Cherbourg (1450)"; "Siege of Bordeaux (1453)"; "Round
  shot"; "Trunnion"; "Compagnie d'ordonnance". Used only as pointers and for
  campaign *sequence*; every date leans on Nicolle (2012) or Wagner (2006) and
  must be reconfirmed against a T2 work before print. The "up to 300 guns"
  Castillon figure and the Bureau device-attributions live here and are
  explicitly **not** trusted as load-bearing.
- **Warfare History Network, "The Guns of Formigny"; historybynicklin.
  wordpress.com, "Jean Bureau".** Popular; source of the "sixty sieges"
  formulations (in "a year and six days" / "sixteen months") that the memo
  rejects, and of the Bureau-at-Formigny attribution. Pointers only.
- **David Nicolle, *The Fall of English France 1449–53* (Osprey, 2012)** —
  the source underlying most Wikipedia campaign dates. Osprey is popular-
  military reference (T4/T3 boundary); useful for the timeline but **should be
  upgraded to Contamine/Curry** for any printed date. → RETRIEVAL (optional).

## RETRIEVAL LIST (gated — do NOT cite until the PDF is in the repo)

Per CLAUDE.md §4: each entry names the specific claim that depends on it. The
memo marks every such claim **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]**; none may be asserted
as consulted until retrieved. Access routes for the canonical works are in
`/reading-list.md` (Cambridge alumni / Internet Archive).

1. **Philippe Contamine, *Guerre, état et société à la fin du Moyen Âge***
   (the outline's "definitive source"). NEEDED FOR: the Bureau brothers'
   exact offices and the 27 Dec 1444 succession; the Jean/Gaspard division of
   labour; the standardised-calibre attribution; gunners' pay and the
   artillery arm inside the 1445 *compagnies d'ordonnance*; a defensible gun
   count for Castillon and the Normandy train; any firm figure replacing
   "sixty places." Currently the largest single dependency.
2. **Anne Curry, "Was there a Military Revolution in Lancastrian Normandy?"**
   (Univ. of Southampton eprints 153723; anti-bot-blocked). NEEDED FOR: the
   authoritative count of places taken in 1449–50 and the scholarly
   confirmation/rebuttal of the "sixty"/"one hundred" figures; the revisionist
   counter-weight to Rogers (does the campaign constitute a "military
   revolution"?), which the draft must engage (critiques.md).
3. **Kelly DeVries, *Guns and Men in Medieval Europe* (and DeVries & Smith,
   *Medieval Military Technology*).** NEEDED FOR: the chronology of when siege
   guns overtook developed stone walls (the c. 1400 tipping point); iron-vs-
   stone shot in the Bureau era; the Castillon/Formigny gun details; whether
   the two Formigny culverins are securely attributable to the Bureaux.
4. **Bert S. Hall, *Weapons and Warfare in Renaissance Europe*.** NEEDED FOR:
   corned powder, trunnions, and carriage development chronology and their
   correct attribution (general vs Bureau-specific); the ballistics of the
   transition.
5. **Malcolm Vale, *War and Chivalry*.** NEEDED FOR: the cultural-military
   context of the late-Valois reconquest; the obsolescence of the chivalric
   order that Castillon dramatises (Talbot's charge).
6. **Anne Curry, *The Hundred Years War*; Christopher Allmand, *The Hundred
   Years War: England and France at War c.1300–c.1450*.** NEEDED FOR: the
   campaign narrative and dates (Rouen 1418 vs 1449 contrast; Harfleur
   Dec-1449-vs-Jan-1450 variant; the 1452–53 Gascon campaign and Bordeaux);
   the standard framing of Castillon as the war's end.
7. **A. J. Pollard, *John Talbot and the War in France, 1427–1453*.** NEEDED
   FOR: Talbot's firm birth year/age and the death narrative at Castillon
   (the horse felled by a gun; the coup de grâce), which opens the book's
   Introduction and must be told within what the biography supports.
8. **Clifford Rogers (ed.), *The Military Revolution Debate*; Geoffrey Parker,
   *The Military Revolution*; Andrew Ayton & J. L. Price (eds.), *The Medieval
   Military Revolution*.** NEEDED FOR: the full scholarly debate framing
   (Roberts–Parker–Rogers) behind the objection in critiques.md. (Rogers's
   *1993 article* is open and already cited; the *edited volume* is gated.)
9. *(Optional)* **J. Stevenson (ed.), *Letters and Papers…* (Rolls Series)** —
   open on archive.org; retrieve to cite the "one hundred strongpoints" list
   first-hand rather than via Rogers.
