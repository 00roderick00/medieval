# Brief — Ch. 6: 1450 — The Year the Walls Fell (~11,000 words)
PURPOSE: The causal turn; the chapter that makes the book falsifiable.
Fortification vs. artillery baseline c. 1400; the Bureau brothers as
institutionalisers of the financed, standardised royal siege train — NOT
inventors (corned powder, trunnions, iron shot, calibres are general
developments they organised and financed at scale; the salaried artilleurs
as a paid technical service); Formigny (field artillery's first effect in
open battle — provocative, drawing the English out of position; Richemont's
cavalry decisive, not the guns); the Normandy campaign 1449–50 (one hundred
strongpoints taken in 1450 per Rogers/Stevenson, most surrendered without a
siege; temporal compression IS the evidence, via the train's reputational
effect); Castillon in full. Modern rhymes handled with care: France 1940 and Kabul 2021 as
temporal-compression parallels only, explicitly not artillery revolutions.
Close by setting up ch. 10: magnates could not afford the train.
RESEARCH FOCUS: Contamine (definitive — retrieval), Vale, Rogers (ed.),
Parker, DeVries, Ayton & Price, Curry, Allmand (retrieval). Verify every
engagement detail; no colour without source.
COMPARATORS: France, England; Burgundy foreshadowed.
OBJECTION (critiques.md): Rogers' infantry-revolution priority — engaged as
INTERNAL to the mechanism, not against it: Rogers himself supplies the
artillery→fiscal-centralisation concession, so the debate is over ranking
revolutions, not over whether artillery set the fiscal threshold magnates
could not cross.
