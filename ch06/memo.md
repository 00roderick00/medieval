# Research Memo — Ch. 6: 1450, The Year the Walls Fell

**Chapter weight:** ~11,000 words (Part II). **Memo target:** 4–6k words.
**Function:** the book's causal turn — the chapter that makes the whole
argument falsifiable. If the mid-fifteenth-century artillery revolution did
not in fact change the economics of sovereignty, the central mechanism fails.

This memo assembles the sourced material for a draft, following the brief's
seven-part structure. Every factual claim is tagged by tier (per CLAUDE.md
§5) and classified as **[CONSENSUS]**, **[CONTESTED]** (sides named), or
**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** (per §4). Claims resting on gated works or on training
knowledge I could not verify in an open source are marked **[UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL]** and carried to `sources.md`.

## 0. Provenance summary — read first

Three findings from the research bear directly on how this chapter must be
drafted, and one of them touches the book's falsifiability head-on.

1. **The "sixty fortified places in fifteen months" figure does not survive
   verification.** It appears in no open scholarly source in that form. The
   number and the window both drift between popular retellings ("sixty" in "a
   year and six days" or "sixteen months"); "fifteen months" matches nothing.
   The rigorous open-access figure is different: Clifford Rogers, citing a
   contemporary English document, gives **one hundred strongpoints taken by
   the French in 1450** — with the load-bearing caveat that *most were never
   besieged; they surrendered rather than resist*. **The draft must drop
   "sixty in fifteen months" and use Rogers's sourced "one hundred
   strongpoints in 1450."** Detail in §4. This is the single most important
   correction in the memo, and the brief flagged the figure for exactly this
   scrutiny.

2. **The Bureau brothers did not invent the technologies the brief lists.**
   Corned powder, trunnions, mobile carriages, iron shot and standardised
   calibres are general European developments of c. 1400–1470. Open sources
   credit the Bureaux with *the institution* — a systematic, standardised,
   well-supplied royal artillery train and a salaried technical service — not
   with any single device. The draft must attribute the *organisation*, not
   the *inventions*, or it will overclaim in exactly the way a specialist
   reviewer will catch. Detail in §2. (This is a conflict between the brief's
   wording and the source consensus; per CLAUDE.md §8 it is flagged here, not
   resolved silently. The brief is tasking, not doctrine, so the correction
   is offered for Roderick's decision.)

3. **Formigny's guns were provocative, not decisive.** The open sources
   support the *factual* claim — two French culverins fired beyond bowshot
   and drew the English out of their prepared position — but the weight of
   the better sources treats Richemont's flanking cavalry as decisive and the
   guns as contributory. The brief's "the culebrines that broke the English
   line" runs ahead of the consensus. Detail in §3.

Two smaller corrections: "serpentine" powder is the *old dry-mixed* powder,
the opposite of *corned*, not a synonym for it; and iron shot did **not**
displace stone in the Bureau era — their guns still fired stone. Both are in
§2.

None of these corrections weakens the chapter. As §4 and §7 argue, Rogers's
"most surrendered rather than resist" actually *strengthens* the book's
mechanism: the credible threat of a siege train nobody else could afford
produced capitulation without a shot — which is the fiscal-military advantage
operating, not merely masonry falling faster.

## 1. The technological baseline

**The fortification.** The developed high-medieval castle was optimised
against escalade and the trebuchet: tall, relatively thin stone walls, high
towers, battlements, arrow slits. Height was the virtue and, it turned out,
the fatal constraint — a tall wall had to be thin, because a tall *and* thick
wall was ruinously expensive in stone and labour. **[CONSENSUS]** (Britannica,
"military technology / the castle," T2 — fetch-blocked, confirm wording in
browser; English Heritage on castle design, T3.) The standard way to take
such a place was not to breach it but to invest it: "medieval sieges were
settled more often by treachery, starvation, or disease than by breached
walls," with blockade over months the type-case (Kenilworth, 1266, roughly
six months). **[CONSENSUS]** (English Heritage, T3.)

The mechanism by which artillery reversed this is well set out in open
sources. "'Tall and thin,' while good against ladders, was a huge liability
against cannon": European walls with a rubble-and-mortar core *shattered*
under sustained battering where a rammed-earth core would have absorbed the
shock (Bret Devereaux, "Fortification, Part IV," ACOUP, T3 — a specialist
historian's blog, reliable for the mechanism, not for Bureau-era specifics).
**Caveat to carry:** Devereaux's vivid contemporary quantification — guns
doing "as much Execution… in a few Hours as… in the like Number of Days" — is
Guicciardini on **Charles VIII's 1494 Italian campaign**, forty years after
our decade. Use it for the *mechanism*, never as a 1449–53 datum. **[CONTESTED
provenance — do not misdate.]**

**The artillery c. 1400.** Early bombards threw large stone balls with great
striking power but fired at short range with reduced charges to avoid
bursting (Warfare History Network, "Medieval Siege Artillery," T4). The claim
that early guns were effective against light masonry and timber but slow
against first-rate stone walls is intuitive and partly supported, but I could
not find a crisp open-access statement fixing the *tipping point* at which
siege guns became reliably faster than starvation against developed stone.
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: DeVries, *Guns and Men in Medieval Europe*; or Bert
Hall, *Weapons and Warfare in Renaissance Europe*, for the chronology of when
guns overtook walls.]** The draft should state the baseline qualitatively and
let Rogers's 1420s–1440s dating (below) carry the "when."

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The gap between an impregnable-by-starvation wall and a
gun that could open it in days is the material foundation of the whole
chapter: it is what made fortified magnate power viable in the dispersal
phase, and its closing is what made that power obsolete.

## 2. The Bureau brothers

**Who they were. [CONSENSUS, but tertiary-sourced — flag.]** Jean Bureau
(b. c. 1390, Semoine, Champagne; d. 1463) and his brother Gaspard (d. 1469)
were Charles VII's artillery chiefs. Jean was master of the French artillery
by 1439 and simultaneously a senior crown financial officer (Treasurer of
France, 1443; later mayor of Bordeaux); Gaspard is given as grand-master of
the king's artillery from 27 December 1444. (Wikipedia, "Jean Bureau" /
"Gaspard Bureau," T4.) **These dates and titles rest only on tertiary
sources.** **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Contamine, *Guerre, état et société*,
for the offices, the 1444 succession, and the division of labour between Jean
as commander and Gaspard as the technical man.]**

**What they actually contributed — the central correction.** The brief lists
"standardized calibers, corned powder, iron shot, trunnions and improved
carriages, the specialized artilleurs as a paid technical service" as "the
specific innovations they introduced." The open research does not support
device-level attribution:

- **Corned powder** (wet-milled into stable grains that burn uniformly)
  dates to "shortly after 1400" and is a **general** European development
  (Encyclopedia.com / Gale, T3). *Correction:* "serpentine" powder is the
  *older, dry-mixed* type — the opposite of corned, not a synonym.
- **Iron shot** appears in the first half of the fifteenth century but does
  **not** displace stone until the mid-sixteenth; the Bureaux's guns fired
  mostly **stone**. The tertiary claim that the brothers switched "from
  wrought to cast iron" is ambiguous (barrels, not shot) and unsourced.
  **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: DeVries.]**
- **Trunnions and improved carriages** are a French–Burgundian development,
  essentially fixed by c. 1465; open sources (citing Duffy, *Siege Warfare*,
  1979; McNeill, *Pursuit of Power*, 1982) do **not** attribute the trunnion
  to the Bureaux by name (Wikipedia "Trunnion," T4).
- **Standardised calibres** are attributed to the Bureaux only by Wikipedia
  (T4), with no scholarly citation located. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
  Contamine / DeVries.]**
- **The salaried technical service** is the best-anchored and the most
  defensibly attributable point. Charles VII's ordonnance of 28 April 1445
  created the *compagnies d'ordonnance*, the first permanent salaried royal
  standing army in France; the artillery arm sat within this establishment
  (Wikipedia "Compagnie d'ordonnance," T4). **[CONSENSUS on the 1445 reform;
  the fine detail of gunners' pay and organisation is UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
  Contamine.]** (Chapter 10 owns the *compagnies* and the *taille* in full;
  here they are named only as the fiscal-administrative substrate.)

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT, corrected]** The Bureaux's contribution was
organisational, not inventive: they built the first *institution* that could
take a set of loose, generally-available technologies and field them as a
standardised, permanently-financed, technically-staffed royal siege train.
That is precisely the point the book needs — the decisive variable is not a
gadget but the *fiscal-administrative capacity to operate the gadget at
scale* — and it is stronger, not weaker, for not resting on a myth of
lone-genius invention. The draft should make the institution the protagonist.

## 3. Formigny, 15 April 1450

**[CONSENSUS]** Date (15 April 1450) and location (Formigny, on the
Carentan–Bayeux road in Normandy) are confirmed across all sources
(Britannica, T2, fetch-blocked; Wikipedia, T4; britishbattles.com, T3). An
English relief force under Sir Thomas Kyriell, landed at Cherbourg in March
1450 and grown to roughly 4,000 (about 2,900 archers), was met by French
forces under the Count of Clermont (~3,000) and, arriving from Brittany, the
Constable Arthur de Richemont (~2,000). Numbers are the conventional figures
repeated across popular accounts; **for defended strengths, [UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL: DeVries or Curry].**

**The artillery point — factual core sound, decisiveness [CONTESTED].** The
open sources agree on the narrative: two French culverins (light,
wheeled, probably breech-loading field guns) fired on the English line from
beyond effective bowshot (~400 yards), and the English broke their prepared
position to charge and seize the guns — which they did — before being caught
by Clermont's renewed assault and Richemont's cavalry on the flank. Where the
sources **differ** is on how much the guns *decided*:

- Britannica calls Formigny "the first occasion of the French use of field
  artillery" (T2, via snippet — confirm in browser before using as a "first"
  claim).
- britishbattles.com puts real weight on the "galling fire" of the guns (T3).
- longbow-archers.com is the most precise and most sceptical: the guns were
  "decisive only in that their noise located the English position… and
  therefore made a concentrated attack on two fronts possible," the victory
  coming from "time-honoured hand to hand combat" (T3/T4).
- Wikipedia splits it: the Breton cavalry's arrival was "arguably more
  decisive," "although the French guns also played a role" — and the guns'
  *noise* alerted Richemont that a battle was under way (T4).

The attribution of the two guns to the Bureaux *at Formigny specifically*
rests on popular sources (Warfare History Network; the Nicklin blog, T4) and
is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: DeVries / Contamine].**

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT — calibrate]** Formigny should be drafted as the first
demonstration that field artillery could *matter in open battle*, not as the
battle field artillery *won*. The honest and in fact more interesting reading
is available: the guns did not break the English line by execution; they
broke English *discipline*, drawing a hitherto-unbeaten archer army out of
the position that had won Crécy, Poitiers and Agincourt — and the guns' noise
is what brought the decisive cavalry to the field. That is a subtler and
better-sourced claim than "the culebrines broke the line," and it keeps the
chapter honest before the specialist. **The brief's phrasing should be
softened accordingly.** [Flag for Roderick.]

**Aftermath [CONSENSUS]:** the last English field army in Normandy was
destroyed (French sources claim ~3,744–3,774 English dead, buried in mass
graves, ~900–1,400 captured incl. Kyriell; casualty figures are French-source
claims — treat as such). Caen fell ~1 July 1450; Cherbourg, the last English
hold, by 12 August 1450.

## 4. The Normandy campaign, 1449–1450 — and the figure

**The verification result. [CONTESTED / corrected]** The brief's "sixty
fortified places in fifteen months" matches no open source in that form:

- The **rigorous, open-access** figure is Clifford Rogers's: "A contemporary
  English document lists **one hundred strongpoints taken by the French in
  1450**, including Château Gaillard, St-Sauveur-le-Vicomte, Cherbourg,
  Roche-Guyon, and Rouen, all of which had earlier required long sieges to
  capture" (Rogers, "The Military Revolutions of the Hundred Years' War,"
  *Journal of Military History* 57 [1993]; open access at De Re Militari, T2;
  his fn 108 cites J. Stevenson, ed., *Letters and Papers Illustrative of the
  Wars of the English in France…*, Rolls Series, 1861–64, p. 619 et seq. —
  a **T1** contemporary document, cited here *via Rogers*, not consulted
  directly).
- "Sixty" appears only in uncited popular sources, and even they disagree on
  the window — "a year and six days" (Nicklin blog, T4) or "sixteen months"
  (Warfare History Network, T4). "Fifteen months" appears to be a further
  mutation. The actual campaign span (war resumed 31 July 1449 → Cherbourg 12
  August 1450) is ~12½ months.

**Recommendation:** the draft uses Rogers's "**one hundred strongpoints in
1450**," sourced to the Stevenson list, and drops "sixty in fifteen months"
entirely. If Roderick wants the "sixty" formulation retained for cadence, it
must be explicitly marked as an unverified traditional figure pending
Contamine/Curry. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Contamine, *Guerre, état et
société*; and especially Anne Curry, "Was there a Military Revolution in
Lancastrian Normandy?" (Soton eprints 153723, anti-bot-blocked), the paper
that would confirm, source, or debunk any count.]**

**The load-bearing caveat — and why it helps the book. [CONSENSUS, Rogers]**
Rogers immediately qualifies: "Most of the one hundred strongpoints referred
to were actually never besieged; they surrendered rather than make a hopeless
attempt to resist." Naively this complicates the "speed of sieges =
battering" evidence. Read correctly, it *is* the book's mechanism: a siege
train that no baron and no town could hope to withstand produced surrender on
sight. The fiscal-military advantage did not have to be exercised to be
decisive; its mere credibility collapsed the defensive order. **[BOOK'S
ARGUMENT]** The draft should lead with this, not bury it — capitulation-
without-siege is the purest evidence that the *economics* of decisive force,
not any single engagement, had changed.

**Temporal compression, where guns were used [CONSENSUS, Rogers]:** after a
sixteen-day siege in 1450 "almost the entire wall of Bayeux was 'pierced and
brought down'"; at Blaye in 1451 "it took only five days before 'the town
walls were completely thrown down in many places'"; Dax and Acx had walls "so
battered… that by diverse breaches they could be taken by assault" (Rogers,
T2, quoting contemporary documents). A popular secondary source adds the
contrast that Rouen, which withstood Henry V for five months in 1418, fell in
under a month in 1449 (medievalhistory.info, T3 — coherent but uncited; the
Rouen 1418 vs 1449 contrast is worth verifying against Curry/Allmand before
print). **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL for the 1418-vs-1449 Rouen contrast.]**

**Campaign timeline [CONSENSUS on the sequence; dates tertiary — reconfirm
against Nicolle/Curry]:** Pont-Audemer (12 Aug 1449), Lisieux (16 Aug 1449),
Rouen (capitulated 29 Oct 1449), Harfleur (Dec 1449 / ~1 Jan 1450 — variant
dating, flag), Honfleur (18 Feb 1450), Fresnoy (Jan 1450), Formigny (15 Apr
1450), Bayeux (~16-day siege, surrendered mid-May 1450), Caen (~1 July 1450,
after ~3-week bombardment), Falaise (6–21 July 1450), Cherbourg (besieged 6
July, fell 12 Aug 1450). (Wikipedia campaign and siege articles, all leaning
on David Nicolle, *The Fall of English France 1449–53*, Osprey 2012, T4;
Falaise and Cherbourg dates from dedicated Wikipedia siege articles.) The
much-repeated detail that Jean Bureau sited guns on the tidal beach at
Cherbourg, protecting them with hides against the tide, appears in the
Wikipedia campaign article but *not* in the dedicated siege stub — **thinly
sourced, flag.**

**Contemporary attestation [T1, open but not yet parsed]:** Jean Chartier's
*Chronique de Charles VII* praises the "approaches, fosses, entrenchments and
mines which the [Bureau brothers] had made before all the towns and castles
which were besieged" (surfaced in search; primary text open-access at Gallica
and Internet Archive, in Middle French — flagged for a French-reading pass,
not yet consulted line-by-line). The *Recouvrement de Normandie* (Berry
Herald) is the specialist chronicle (OpenEdition). These are the primary
sources for the compression claim and should be cited directly once read.

## 5. Castillon, 17 July 1453

**[CONSENSUS]** 17 July 1453, near Castillon (now Castillon-la-Bataille), on
the Dordogne about thirty miles east of Bordeaux. Context: Bordeaux, chafing
under French rule, invited the English back; Talbot entered Bordeaux on 17
October 1452; Charles VII's 1453 campaign advanced on Castillon.
(britishbattles.com, T3; Britannica, T2, via snippet.)

**Talbot's death [CONSENSUS on the core, anecdotal on the detail].** John
Talbot, 1st Earl of Shrewsbury (b. c. 1387, so ~66), "the last great English
captain" — "Old Talbot," to the French *le roi Talbot*, the most feared
English commander of the war's final phase — was killed when his horse was
brought down by an artillery round and he was killed on the ground. The
precise coup de grâce (a French archer; or an axeman named in one account)
**varies by source and is anecdotal** (britishbattles.com, T3; Wikipedia, T4).
His son, Lord Lisle, died with him. **The horse-felled-by-a-gun, killed-on-
the-ground sequence is well attested; the exact killer is not.** For a
load-bearing death narrative or a firm age, **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: A. J.
Pollard, *John Talbot and the War in France*].** (This scene opens the book's
Introduction; the draft and the intro must tell it identically and within
what the sources support.)

**The artillery park [CONSENSUS on setup; gun count CONTESTED].** Jean Bureau
laid out an entrenched artillery park along the Lidoire, ditched and
palisaded on three sides with the river on the fourth, the guns sited to
enfilade an attacker. Believing the French were retreating, the Anglo-Gascon
army assaulted the camp frontally and was cut down by massed artillery fire —
commonly called the first major battle *decided* by field artillery.
The **number of guns is not settled**: britishbattles.com gives "probably
around 200"; the widely-repeated "**up to 300** guns of all sizes" traces to
Wikipedia citing Wagner, *Encyclopedia of the Hundred Years War* (2006), p. 79
— a popular reference (T4). **The draft should say "perhaps two to three
hundred guns," not assert 300 as fact.** **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: DeVries or
Contamine for a defensible count and for what it counts — field guns, all
pieces, or the whole park.]**

**Significance [CONSENSUS].** The Anglo-Gascon army was effectively destroyed;
French losses were light. Bordeaux surrendered and French troops entered on
19 October 1453 — the conventional end of the Hundred Years' War, leaving
Calais as England's last continental holding. (Wikipedia "Siege of Bordeaux
(1453)," T4; date consistent across sources.)

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Castillon is the clean case Formigny is not: here the
guns did not merely provoke, they executed. A fortified gun park, unaffordable
to any subject, annihilated a charging feudal-chivalric army led by the
age's most celebrated captain. The book's claim — that the price of decisive
force had moved, and moved in favour of whoever could pay for a siege train —
is carried by Castillon more safely than by any other single engagement.

## 6. The modern rhyme within the rhyme

The brief uses two modern collapses as **temporal-compression parallels only,
explicitly not artillery revolutions** — the disanalogy must be stated in the
draft, per CLAUDE.md §6. Both are contemporary and solidly sourced.

- **The fall of France, May–June 1940.** German offensive 10 May 1940; Paris
  fell 14 June; armistice 22 June — "in just over six weeks" (US Holocaust
  Memorial Museum, Holocaust Encyclopedia, T2/T3; Britannica "Battle of
  France," T2). 10 May → 22 June is 43 days; the "six weeks" framing is
  accurate. **[CONSENSUS]**
- **The fall of Kabul, August 2021.** First provincial capital (Zaranj) fell
  6 August; Kabul fell 15 August 2021 as the government collapsed with little
  resistance. US intelligence had assessed on 10 August that Kabul might hold
  30–90 days (earlier June assessments: 6–12 months); it fell within days
  (History.com, T3; UK Parliament research briefing CBP-9299, T2/T3, open;
  Washington Post timeline, T3, paywalled). **[CONSENSUS]**

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT, disanalogy stated]** Neither 1940 nor 2021 is an artillery
revolution; Blitzkrieg was an organisational-doctrinal shift and the Taliban
advance a political collapse. What they share with Normandy 1450 is only the
*tempo* of legitimacy failure: an order that looked stable weeks before
dissolved faster than the affected polity could process. The parallel is to
the *rate* of collapse, not its cause — and the draft must say so, or invite
exactly the over-claim the book forbids. This is a **[BRIDGE]** passage:
the analogy joint is Roderick's to set.

## 7. Setting up Chapter 10 — and the mechanism, confirmed by the objector

The chapter closes where the brief directs: the Bureau train was not the
weapon of great magnates, because great magnates could not afford it. It
required the fiscal capacity of the crown — the *taille* levied on the whole
country — and the administrative capacity of a salaried technical service.
Both had existed before; artillery made them *decisive*.

The strongest single piece of evidence for this is that it comes from
**Rogers — the very historian whose "infantry revolution" is the chapter's
named objection** (critiques.md). Rogers states plainly: "Artillery was
expensive. As early as 1442, the French government was spending more than
twice as much on its artillery train as it spent on more traditional war
materiel… The central governments of large states could afford to acquire and
maintain large siege trains: their subjects and smaller neighbors, in general,
could not." He then describes the self-reinforcing cycle the book calls the
mechanism: central governments acquired artillery, suppressed regional powers,
raised tax revenue, and expanded their artillery and armies further (Rogers,
De Re Militari, T2). **[CONSENSUS — and it is the book's mechanism in a
sceptic's own words.]**

This is the memo's most valuable result. The book's causal chain — weapon
capital-intensity → permanent finance → administrative capacity → suppression
of intermediate authorities — is not the book's idiosyncratic reading of the
1450s. It is Rogers's, offered while arguing that the infantry revolution
mattered *more*. The disagreement (see critiques.md) is over *ranking*
revolutions, not over whether artillery drove fiscal centralisation. The draft
should hold this in reserve and deploy it in the objection-handling: the
chapter does not need to defeat Rogers on the infantry revolution; it needs
only his own concession that the artillery revolution set the fiscal
threshold above what any subject could cross.

**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** That is the sentence Chapter 10 will pick up: the
artillery revolution did not merely win battles; it priced sovereignty, and
only those who could meet the price survived as sovereigns.
