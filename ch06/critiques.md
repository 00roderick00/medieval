# Critiques — Ch. 6: 1450, The Year the Walls Fell

Each objection is steelmanned at full strength before the chapter answers it
(CLAUDE.md §6). Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–6
were surfaced during the research for this memo. The Perplexity second-opinion
run on the eventual draft is to be logged at the foot.

The governing discipline: the chapter's falsifiability is a feature, not a
liability. The book *wants* the mechanism testable, so these objections are
engaged on the merits, never deflected.

---

## Objection 1 — Rogers's infantry-revolution priority (the named objection)

**Steelman.** The "military revolution" is contested, and the most rigorous
reconstruction — Clifford Rogers's — does not put artillery first. Rogers
argues for a *sequence* of punctuated revolutions across the Hundred Years'
War, and the one that broke the old order was the **Infantry Revolution of the
fourteenth century**: at Courtrai (1302), Bannockburn (1314), Morgarten
(1315), and above all in the English longbow victories at Crécy (1346) and
Poitiers (1356), common pikemen, halberdiers and archers "overturned the
centuries-long dominance of aristocratic shock cavalry." That is the deeper
rupture — it dethroned the mounted noble, the load-bearing figure of the
feudal-military order — and it happened a *century* before the Bureau guns.
The artillery revolution of the 1420s–1440s, on this account, is merely the
second beat in a four-part sequence (Infantry → Artillery → Artillery-Fortress
/ *trace italienne* → Roberts's administrative revolution); to isolate 1450 as
"the year the walls fell" and hang the whole book's causal claim on it is to
mistake one movement of a symphony for the score. Worse for the book: the
infantry revolution had *already* shown that cheap, disciplined foot could
beat expensive cavalry — that is a *de*-concentrating shock, capability moving
*down* the wealth gradient, the opposite of the book's thesis. If the story of
late-medieval war is that cheap infantry humbled costly chivalry, why should
we read the period as one of capital-*intensification* of decisive force?

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the infantry revolution entirely; it is
necessary to the argument, not fatal to it. Two moves.

First, *necessary but not sufficient for consolidation.* The infantry
revolution changed who won battles; it did not change who could afford to
project decisive force, because disciplined infantry could be raised by great
magnates, free cities and leagues as readily as by kings — the Swiss cantons,
the English indentured retinue, the Italian *condottieri* companies all fielded
it (this is Chapters 3 and 4's material). A capability any subject can raise
does not concentrate sovereignty; it disperses it, which is exactly the
neomedieval dispersal Part I diagnoses. The infantry revolution is therefore
part of the *problem* the artillery revolution resolves, not a rival
explanation of the resolution.

Second — and decisively — *the objector concedes the mechanism.* Rogers
himself supplies the fiscal argument the book needs: "Artillery was expensive.
As early as 1442, the French government was spending more than twice as much
on its artillery train as… traditional war materiel… The central governments
of large states could afford to acquire and maintain large siege trains: their
subjects and smaller neighbors, in general, could not." And he names the
self-reinforcing cycle — acquire artillery, suppress regional powers, raise
tax revenue, expand the train — that the book calls its mechanism. The
disagreement with Rogers is therefore *not* about whether the artillery
revolution drove fiscal-administrative consolidation; Rogers affirms that. It
is only about *ranking* the revolutions against each other. The chapter does
not need to win that ranking contest. It needs the narrower, Rogers-endorsed
claim: that the artillery revolution was the step which put the price of
decisive force above what any subject could pay. The book can grant Rogers the
infantry revolution as the more consequential *tactically* while holding that
the artillery revolution was the more consequential *constitutionally* — and
cite Rogers's own section on cost to prove it.

**Framing [load-bearing, post-review 2026-07-22].** The draft must present this
debate as *internal to the mechanism, not against it*. Rogers is not an
opponent of the book's causal chain; he is its witness — he supplies the
artillery → permanent-finance → centralisation sequence himself. The "military
revolution debate" the chapter engages is therefore a family quarrel among
scholars who all accept that the capital-intensity of decisive force drove
fiscal-administrative consolidation; they disagree only on which revolution to
crown. The chapter should stage it that way — not "here is a scholar who denies
our thesis, whom we refute," but "here is the scholar who proves our mechanism,
arguing about a ranking we need not settle." Drawn this way, the strongest
available objection becomes the chapter's strongest citation, and the
falsifiability the chapter stakes itself on is shown to survive contact with
its most rigorous critic.

*Residual exposure:* this answer stands or falls on the "infantry could be
raised by subjects, artillery could not" distinction. If a reviewer shows that
first-rate siege trains were in fact within reach of great magnates in the
1450s, the distinction collapses. The Burgundian counter-case (Charles the
Bold's superb artillery) is the obvious pressure point — handled in Chapter 10,
where the answer is that Burgundy's train did not save it and, more to the
point, that Burgundy was itself a would-be consolidating state, not a mere
subject. The draft must not let the reader reach the Burgundy objection before
Chapter 10 arms them against it; a one-line forward-pointer is warranted.

## Objection 2 — "They surrendered; the guns did not batter them down"

**Steelman.** The chapter's strongest evidence — the speed of the 1449–50
reconquest — dissolves on inspection. Rogers's own source says "most of the
one hundred strongpoints… were actually never besieged; they surrendered
rather than make a hopeless attempt to resist." If the fortresses fell by
capitulation, not by battering, then the proximate cause of the collapse was
*morale and political calculation* after Formigny and the fall of Rouen, not
artillery. The English position in Normandy was under-garrisoned, unpaid, and
politically abandoned by a bankrupt Lancastrian government; it would have
crumbled in 1449–50 with or without the Bureau train. The "walls fell"
narrative is thus a technological gloss on what was really a fiscal-political
implosion on the *English* side.

**The chapter's answer.** This objection, taken seriously, does not weaken the
book's claim — it states it more purely. The book's thesis is not "guns knock
down more walls per week"; it is that *the economics of decisive force had
moved*, such that whoever could field a siege train nobody else could match
did not have to use it to win. Capitulation-on-sight is the mechanism at its
most complete: the credible, unmatchable threat collapsed the defensive order
without a shot. That garrisons surrendered *because resistance was hopeless*
is the point — resistance was hopeless *because* of the train. The draft
should therefore lead with the surrenders, not the batterings, and frame the
handful of actual sieges (Bayeux's sixteen days; Blaye's five in 1451) as the
demonstrations that made the surrenders rational. The English fiscal-political
implosion is real and belongs in the chapter — but it is Chapter 5's hollow
sovereignty meeting Chapter 6's artillery, the two blades of the same shears,
not a rival cause. *The one thing the draft must not do is claim the walls
were physically battered down across Normandy; that is false and the objection
is right to reject it.*

## Objection 3 — Formigny proves the opposite of what the chapter wants

**Steelman.** If Formigny (15 April 1450) is offered as artillery's coming-of-
age in open battle, it proves the reverse. Two culverins did not break the
English line; the English *captured* them. The better sources (Wikipedia,
longbow-archers.com) judge the Breton cavalry's flank arrival decisive and the
guns merely contributory — indeed the guns' chief effect was that their
*noise* told Richemont where the battle was. A battle in which the enemy
overruns your guns and wins by cavalry and hand-to-hand is a strange exhibit
for the decisiveness of field artillery.

**The chapter's answer.** Accept the correction and draft Formigny accordingly
— it is more useful honestly told. The guns' effect was not execution but
*provocation*: they drew a hitherto-unbeaten English archer army out of the
prepared defensive position that had won Crécy, Poitiers and Agincourt, and
into the open where it could be destroyed. That is a real and novel tactical
effect of field artillery, and it is enough. Formigny is the chapter's *minor*
key; **Castillon** is the major one, and there the sources are unambiguous —
the fortified gun park executed a frontal assault and annihilated the
Anglo-Gascon army. The draft should weight the two accordingly and must not
assert "the culebrines broke the English line" (the brief's phrasing), which
the sources will not bear. Over-claiming Formigny would hand a specialist the
easiest possible rebuttal on the very chapter that carries the book's
falsifiability.

## Objection 4 — The Curry revisionist objection (gradualism)

**Steelman.** Anne Curry, the leading modern authority on Lancastrian
Normandy, has questioned whether the 1449–50 reconquest constitutes a
"military revolution" at all. The gradualist case: French success owed as much
to numbers, money, unified command, English exhaustion and the collapse of the
1444 truce as to any technological leap; artillery was one factor among many
in a conventional reconquest, not a revolution. The punctuationist framing
(Rogers's "revolutions") may impose a sharpness the evidence does not support.
The book's whole architecture depends on 1450 being a genuine *rupture*; if the
best Normandy specialist reads it as evolution, the "hinge" cracks.

**The chapter's answer.** [PARTIALLY DEFERRED — depends on retrieving Curry;
see sources.md RETRIEVAL #2. The following is the intended line, to be
confirmed against her actual argument, not asserted over it.] The book does
not need the *maximal* revolution thesis. It needs only that (a) the price of
decisive siege capability fell sharply within a datable window, and (b) that
price sat above what subjects could pay — both of which Rogers documents from
contemporary fiscal records (the 1442 artillery-spend figure) and which do not
require Curry's assent. If Curry shows the *tactical* revolution was less
abrupt than Rogers claims, the book can absorb that: its claim is
*constitutional*, about who could afford decisive force, not about the tempo
of tactical change. The honest move is to cite Curry as the serious gradualist
counter-weight, concede the mixed causation of the reconquest, and rest the
mechanism on the fiscal asymmetry rather than on a knock-out technological
"revolution." **This objection cannot be fully answered until Curry's paper is
retrieved and read; the draft must not caricature her.**

## Objection 5 — Anachronism and over-attribution discredit the chapter

**Steelman.** A chapter that makes the Bureau brothers the inventors of corned
powder, iron shot, trunnions, standardised calibres and mobile carriages will
be dismantled by any historian of technology: these are general European
developments, not French inventions of the 1440s. Calling serpentine and
corned powder the same thing, or putting iron shot in guns that fired stone,
or asserting "three hundred guns" at Castillon as fact, are precisely the
errors that let a reviewer discredit the whole causal argument by discrediting
its details. A book that stakes its falsifiability on this chapter cannot
afford loose specifics.

**The chapter's answer.** Conceded in full, and already corrected in the memo.
The draft attributes to the Bureaux the *institution* — the standardised,
permanently-financed, technically-staffed royal siege train — not the
device-inventions, which it treats as generally-available technologies the
Bureaux were the first to *organise and finance at scale*. This is not a
retreat; it is the book's actual thesis (the decisive variable is
fiscal-administrative capacity, not any gadget), and it is more defensible for
being accurate. The draft says "perhaps two to three hundred guns" at
Castillon, keeps corned and serpentine powder distinct, and keeps stone in the
guns. The provenance discipline is the chapter's armour, not its constraint.

## Objection 6 — The modern parallels smuggle in the analogy the book forbids

**Steelman.** The book's own rule is "mechanism, not analogy." Yet the chapter
reaches for 1940 France and 2021 Kabul — collapses driven by armoured doctrine
and political disintegration, nothing to do with the capital-intensity of
siege artillery. Invoking them lets the reader feel a resemblance the book has
sworn off, and invites the charge that "the year the walls fell" is
atmospheric history dressed as causal argument.

**The chapter's answer.** The parallels are admitted for one property only —
the *tempo* of legitimacy collapse, an order dissolving faster than the polity
could process — and explicitly *not* as instances of the artillery mechanism.
The draft states the disanalogy in the text (Blitzkrieg and the Taliban
advance are not artillery revolutions), per CLAUDE.md §6, and flags the passage
[BRIDGE] for Roderick, whose call the analogy joint is. If the disanalogy
cannot be stated cleanly, the parallels should be cut rather than allowed to do
covert work. They earn their place only as a controlled illustration of
temporal compression, never as evidence.
