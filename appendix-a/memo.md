## II. Theoretical Reconstruction

**Philip G. Cerny, "Terrorism and the New Security Dilemma," *Naval War College Review* 58:1 (2005), 11–33.** *[T2]*
The freely-available companion to Cerny's paywalled 1998 *Civil Wars* article, and the book's working text for the neomedieval **diagnostic checklist**. Contributes the "durable disorder" concept — a heterarchical order that is a mid-point, not a terminus — and the enumerated characteristics of a neomedieval world (competing institutions with overlapping jurisdictions; fluid boundaries; uneven consolidation; fragmented identities; expanding legal "grey zones"). Most valuable to the mechanism argument: Cerny names *exogenous competitive pressure* as the consolidation driver now absent, the very variable the book re-introduces (weapon capital-intensity) to predict re-consolidation. *Caveat:* the 2005 piece enumerates **five** characteristics; the outline's **six**-fold checklist derives from the 1998 original (retrieval list). — Engaged in **Ch1** (checklist), **Ch3** (property-rights & grey-zones), **Ch5** (hollow sovereignty), **Ch8** (grey zones), Introduction, Ch12 & Appendix C.

**Stephen J. Kobrin, "Back to the Future: Neomedievalism and the Postmodern Digital World Economy," *Journal of International Affairs* 51:2 (1998), 361–386** (bundled as the 1999 Routledge author manuscript). *[T2]*
The origin of the book's **platforms line**. Kobrin argues a digital, networked world economy dissolves the territorial basis of jurisdiction: in cyberspace one cannot say where a transaction occurs, whose law applies, or who may tax it — so singular territorial authority gives way to overlapping, heteronomous authorities. Treats NGOs, the WTO, and standard-setting bodies as genuine political authorities, not mere market actors — the seed of the "platforms as institutional universalisms" move the book completes. Notably, Kobrin himself anticipates *re-centralization* (a supranational authority with taxation and enforcement powers), which the book redirects from the supranational to the intra-bloc terminus. *Caveat:* cite published *JIA*/Routledge pagination, not the manuscript's. — Engaged in **Ch2** (central), **Ch4**, Ch11, Ch12, Appendix D.

## III. Security, Private Force, and the Military Dimension

**Sean McFate, *Durable Disorder: The Return of Private Armies and the Emergence of Neomedievalism*, PhD thesis, LSE, 2011** (the apparatus behind *The Modern Mercenary*, OUP 2014). *[T2]*
The book's evidence for the **return of the market for force**. McFate's puzzle — that the chief customer for private armies is the strongest state, not the weakest — grounds his claim that the eroding mercenary taboo restores "the final piece of Bull's neomedievalism," private international violence. Supplies the condottieri lineage (*condottieri* = "contractors") and, crucially, the **disanalogy the book's Part III turns on**: the modern market is a *monopsony* (the US as consumer-in-chief), and modern PMCs depend on state-provided stability — banking, contract enforcement — where the medieval condottiere built independent fiscal bases. He does not use "bastard feudalism"; that framing is the book's. — Engaged in **Ch3** (central), **Ch4** (condottieri; Prigozhin), Ch11, Ch12.

**Phil Williams, *From the New Middle Ages to a New Dark Age: The Decline of the State and U.S. Strategy* (SSI / U.S. Army War College, 2008).** *[T3]*
The **pessimist counter-read**. Williams accepts the neomedieval diagnosis but rejects its complacency: "durable disorder" is not a stable equilibrium but a way-station that can tip, abruptly and non-linearly, into a "New Dark Age." Contributes the key qualifier for the interstitial spaces — that "ungoverned" zones (favelas, Sadr City, Somalia) are *alternatively* governed by militias and traffickers acting as surrogate authorities — and the "stateocentrism" blind spot. *Caveat:* as government/think-tank analysis its structural claims want corroboration (T3); and chronologically Williams (2008) *precedes* McFate (2014), so the book must stage the McFate–Williams contrast itself rather than present Williams as replying. — Engaged in **Ch3** (counter-read), Ch5, Ch11–Ch12.

## IV. European / Regional Application

**Ole Wæver, "European Security Identities," *Journal of Common Market Studies* 34:1 (1996), 103–132.** *[T2]*
The accessible, page-verifiable statement of the Copenhagen School reading of European order, standing in for the two paywalled target Wæver chapters (retrieval list). Contributes the securitization lens — security as a speech act with distinct referent objects (state = sovereignty, nation = identity, Europe = integration) — and, directly useful to the EU-as-neomedieval argument, Wæver's own invocation of a "new middle ages" of contested sovereignty organised as **concentric circles** around a "westernistic" centre, with Europe "systemically post-sovereign while the units retain their sovereignty." Grounds the book's treatment of the EU as the most advanced neomedieval polity in a securitization-theorist's own terms, not the book's imposition. — Engaged in **Ch11** (EU regulatory church), Introduction, Ch1, Ch12, Appendix D.

## V. Popular / Strategic Synthesis

**Anne-Marie Slaughter, *A New World Order* (Princeton UP, 2004), ch. 5, "An Effective World Order," and ch. 6, "A Just World Order."** *[T2]*
The **optimist's read of the dispersal phase**, engaged as "important and wrong." Slaughter's disaggregated-sovereignty thesis holds that the state is not disappearing but disaggregating into transnational networks of courts, regulators, and legislators; ch. 5 argues these networks can govern durably through soft power and "regulation by information," and ch. 6 supplies five norms to make such governance just. The book treats this as the connective tissue of an interregnum mistaken for load-bearing architecture: where Slaughter sees networks *dispersing* power, the artillery-state mechanism predicts the underlying stacks *concentrating* it and driving re-consolidation. Her conceded "ineradicability of power" is the pivotal wrong turn. — Engaged in **Ch1** & **Ch2** (the alternative reading rejected), Ch12, Introduction/Positioning.

**Robert D. Kaplan, "The Coming Anarchy," *The Atlantic Monthly* 273:2 (Feb. 1994), 44–76.** *[T3]*
The **atmospheric predecessor**, engaged critically. Kaplan reads West Africa as a bellwether of a "coming anarchy" — states hollowed out by scarcity, disease, tribalism, crime — and gestures at a pre-Westphalian "Last Map" of moving power-centres "as in the Middle Ages." Contributes the register the later theorists systematise, and a clean point of divergence: Kaplan's causal engine is Malthusian scarcity, not the book's force capital-intensity. Positioned as an instance of the recurring **declinism-cycle** (every generation announcing the end of the state) that the introduction inoculates against. *Caveat:* Kaplan uses no rigorous neomedieval framework — influence and atmosphere only, never analytical scaffolding. — Engaged in **Introduction** (declinism-cycle), **Ch1**, Appendix D.

**Parag Khanna, "The Next Big Thing: Neomedievalism," *Foreign Policy* no. 172 (May/June 2009).** *[T3]* ⚠️ **retrieval list**
The **popular-synthesis marker** of the neomedieval diagnosis — a mass-audience packaging of the Bull/Kobrin/Friedrichs thesis (overlapping authorities: corporations, city-states, NGOs, PMCs, would-be empires), in an admonitory "new Dark Age" register. Framed as *resemblance*, so any use flags **[ANALOGY-ONLY]** per doctrine §2. **The bundled PDF is a defective web capture (article body absent); it cannot carry a page-level citation and is on the retrieval list for a clean full text.** The annotation above is provisional pending that copy. — Engaged in Introduction, Ch1, Appendix D.

## Pending integration (on the retrieval lists; not yet in the repo)

The spine of the neomedievalism literature and its critics are gated or in-print monographs, to be integrated with page-level citations once their PDFs appear per CLAUDE.md §4:

- **Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society* (1977), ch. 10** — the founding "new mediaevalism" hypothesis and the five sub-trends; the book's starting point and Bull's own skepticism.
- **Jörg Friedrichs, "The Meaning of New Medievalism," *EJIR* 7:4 (2001)** (or the polished 2007 chapter) — the Church/Empire duality that the book institutionalises; and Friedrichs 2004, "The Neomedieval Renaissance," the fullest literature review.
- **Philip G. Cerny, "Neomedievalism, Civil War and the New Security Dilemma," *Civil Wars* 1:1 (1998)** — the **six**-characteristics original behind the bundled 2005 companion.
- **Jan Zielonka, *Europe as Empire* (2006)** — the EU as neomedieval empire, paired with Wæver.
- **Bruce Holsinger, *Neomedievalism, Neoconservatism, and the War on Terror* (2007)** and **N. J. Rengger, "The Dilemmas of Fairyland?" (2000)** — the critics engaged seriously (the fairyland objection), answered architecturally via the mechanism.
- **Martin Wight, Strayer, Anderson, Ullmann, Elton** and the wider medieval historiography — the empirical substrate for Parts II–III.
