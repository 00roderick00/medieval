# Critiques — Ch. 4: The Condottieri

Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 surfaced in
research. Each steelmanned before the answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at
the foot.

---

## Objection 1 — The Hansa-flattening complaint (named)

**Steelman.** The medievalist's case: calling the Hansa a "proto-platform"
flattens its actual institutional character to the point of caricature. The
research itself proves it: the League had no permanent administration, no
treasury, no standing force, no legal personality, not even a definitive
membership list; its "law" was a patchwork of Lübeck-law appeals, consensus
recesses non-binding on absentees, and Kontor house-rules; its "navy" was an
ad hoc levy plagued by free-riding. Amazon has a board, a balance sheet, ten
thousand lawyers and a single controlling will. To press the parallel is to
mistake a loose merchants' confederation for a corporation — precisely the
anachronism (Rengger's "fairyland") the book promises to avoid. And the one
spectacular counter-exhibit — Stralsund's succession clause — turns out on
inspection to be a consent right glossed as a "veto" by encyclopedias.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the institutional differences in full —
the outline already does ("the analogy is imperfect… a *structural* parallel
— a transnational commercial actor with legal, coercive, and jurisdictional
capacity — rather than a functional one"), and the memo's calibrations
(consent not veto; no single Hanserecht) are exactly the discipline the
objection demands. The parallel is then stated at the level where it is
true and useful: a non-territorial commercial actor operating rule-making,
sanction (the Bruges blockade), and extraterritorial enclaves (the Kontors)
*across* the sovereignty of others — the structural problem a territorial
sovereign faces when a commercial actor's scale exceeds its writ. The point
is not that Amazon is the Hansa; it is that the *state's problem* is the
same shape. And the differences are made to work: the Hansa's lack of
centre, treasury and personhood is why it could only be strangled slowly,
enclave by enclave (Peterhof 1494; Steelyard 1597/98), whereas the
platforms' centralisation and balance sheets make them *nationalisable* —
the difference is load-bearing for Chapter 11, not an embarrassment to
Chapter 4.

## Objection 2 — Sforza proves the opposite: sovereignty was never granted

**Steelman.** The chapter offers Sforza as the proof that a contractor
could become sovereign. But the research shows the transition was never
completed on the plane that mattered: Frederick III refused investiture,
regarded him as a usurper, and no Sforza held imperial title until 1494/95.
Sforza held Milan by acclamation and force — a condottiere squatting in a
duchy, not a sovereign among sovereigns. If the deepest historical case of
mercenary-to-sovereign never achieved recognised sovereignty, the chapter's
category dissolves: the market for force produced, at its absolute maximum,
a tolerated usurper.

**The chapter's answer.** Use the fact instead of fearing it — it sharpens
the chapter twice over. First, within the period: Sforza *ruled* — coined,
taxed, warred, married into the system (his recognition came de facto
through the Peace of Lodi order) — demonstrating that in a dispersed order,
*functional* sovereignty could be seized while *formal* investiture lagged
half a century. That is the neomedieval condition in one biography:
authority operative, legitimacy contested, the universal authority (the
Empire) reduced to withholding a ribbon. Second, for the modern parallel:
it calibrates exactly what Sforza-from-above confers and withholds — the
modern state gives the contractor office, rank and programme (Detachment
201's lieutenant-colonelcies) while withholding the thing Sforza also never
got: recognised standing independent of the grantor. The draft should state
this as the chapter's finding, not bury it: even the best case of
contractor-to-sovereign shows legitimacy following force and never fully
catching up.

## Objection 3 — Prigozhin refutes the checklist's usefulness

**Steelman.** The chapter's Sforza checklist (weak centre, rich city,
dynastic vacuum, reputation, marriage) explains Prigozhin's failure only in
hindsight. Any failed coup can be post-hoc scored against any checklist. The
mutiny's actual contingencies — a brokered deal, a turned-back column, a
bomb two months later — were personal and accidental, not structural; had
the column pressed on, the chapter would now read "the Sforza move from
below succeeded." An unfalsifiable template that absorbs every outcome is
astrology with footnotes.

**The chapter's answer.** The checklist is not a predictor of coup outcomes;
it is a specification of the *structural preconditions for converting
contract force into sovereign tenure* — and it generated a discriminating
observation before the outcome test: Prigozhin's fiscal base was state
catering and state military contracts (ch03's paymaster pattern), so even a
militarily successful march could not have yielded Sforza's result — there
was no independent revenue to sustain rule, no Milan to hold, and the
centre he challenged was not fiscally hollow. The claim is conditional and
falsifiable in form: contractors without independent fiscal bases do not
become sovereigns *even when they win battles* — the Swiss-pikes lesson of
ch10 in modern dress. The mutiny's contingencies are conceded (ISW's own
"specifics unclear" caveat is kept in the memo); what the chapter rests on
is not why the column turned back but why, structurally, its success could
not have been converted into tenure. And the aftermath is the checklist's
confirmation, not hindsight: the state absorbed Wagner's contracts and
theatres "without much trouble" (RUSI) — because the fiscal base was always
the state's.

## Objection 4 — Venice breaks the book's own causal arrow

**Steelman.** Chapter 4 presents Venice as the surveillance-state-before-
the-state: denunciation infrastructure, state manufacturing, funded debt,
fiscal legibility — all *before* gunpowder mattered. But the book's central
mechanism (ch06, ch10) claims the causal arrow runs weapon
capital-intensity → permanent finance → administrative capacity. Venice
built the finance (1262) and the administration (1310) centuries before the
artillery revolution, out of *commerce*, not cannon. If a republic could
build the fiscal-administrative stack without the military shock, the shock
is not necessary — and the book's mechanism is one path among several, not
the mechanism.

**The chapter's answer.** Concede the sequence — and note the book never
claims artillery is the *only* road to fiscal-administrative capacity; it
claims artillery *repriced decisive force* so that only polities with such
capacity could remain sovereign at scale (ch06 §7: both capacities "had
preceded the artillery revolution but had not been decisive; artillery made
them decisive"). Venice is in fact the mechanism's supporting case read
correctly: commercial pressure built Venice's stack early, and when the
military revolution arrived, Venice — already fiscally legible, already
running the Arsenal — was among the few that could pay the new price, which
is precisely why it survived as a great power into the sixteenth century
while its unconsolidated neighbours did not. What Venice refutes is
technological determinism (the strawman the book disavows), not the
repricing mechanism. The draft should make this explicit — it is the
chapter's contribution to keeping the book's causal claim honest, parallel
to the Swiss-pikes inoculation in ch10.

## Objection 5 — The modern section is building on last week's newspapers

**Steelman.** The Sforza-from-above section rests on events months old at
writing: Detachment 201 (June 2025), YFQ-44A's first flight (October 2025),
PFCS Forward (February 2026), The Economist's "neo-primes" (April 2026),
and — via ch03 — a blacklist still in litigation. Programmes get cancelled
(IVAS itself is already being replaced by SBMC Next), prototypes lose
competitions, consortia dissolve. A book aiming at Tooze-register
durability is anchoring its Part I close in reporting that may be obsolete
before copy-edit — the precise failure mode of strategy-shop writing.

**The chapter's answer.** Structure the section so the *pattern* is
load-bearing and the *instances* are dated exhibits. The pattern —
integration of the new contractors through procurement, accreditation and
rank rather than ownership — is documented across multiple independent
instruments and years (SOCOM 2022; CCA 2024; TITAN 2024; IVAS novation
2025; Detachment 201 2025; IL6 2026), from primary or institutional
sources (army.mil, DOT&E, investor filings), so no single cancellation
breaks it; the memo's own correction discipline (NGC2 attribution; the
absent golden share stated as absence) shows the exhibits are handled as
evidence, not colour. Every instance is date-stamped in the draft; the
developing items (the blacklist) are marked developing; and the section's
conclusion is framed as the trajectory-to-watch that Part III's indicators
formalise (C3-2), not as a settled outcome. This is what the doctrine's
calibrated-confidence rule is for: the chapter observes a pattern with
named, dated instances and stakes a conditional claim on its continuation.
