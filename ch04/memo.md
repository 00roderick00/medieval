## 1. Hawkwood and the compagnia di ventura

**[CONSENSUS, tertiary-sourced]** John Hawkwood (b. c. 1320–23, Essex; d.
Florence, 17 March 1394): Hundred Years' War veteran turned routier after
the Peace of Brétigny (1360) demobilisations; into Italy with the **White
Company** (1361; c. 3,500 cavalry, 2,000 infantry, organised in lances;
captain from 1363), which introduced dismounted men-at-arms with
**longbowmen** to Italian warfare. Employers in sequence: Pisa, the
Visconti (he married Bernabò's natural daughter Donnina), Gregory XI (the
Massacre of Cesena, 1377, on his company's hands — the draft should not
launder him), then Florence — de facto commander-in-chief **from 1377**
(the 1380 lifetime condotta is Caferro's: **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]**).
Castagnaro (**11 March 1387**) his tactical masterpiece. Florence voted him
a marble monument *while alive* (Aug 1393), gave him a state funeral, and
in 1436 commissioned **Uccello's fresco** in the Duomo. **[BOOK'S
ARGUMENT]** The fresco is the chapter's emblem: the mercenary as honoured
servant of the fiscal city-state — domestication commemorated in paint.
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Caferro, *John Hawkwood*; Mallett.]**

## 2. The system in operation

**[CONSENSUS on outline; anatomy gated per §0.2]** The condotta was the
written contract — duration, pay, force composition in lances — from which
the condottiere took his name; the buyers were the fiscally-capable states
(Milan, Venice, Florence, the Papacy, Naples). Across the fifteenth century
the system *stabilised*: longer contracts, permanent commands, integration
by marriage, land and honours (Sforza, Colleoni, Federico da Montefeltro as
the standard examples), and the **lanze spezzate** — men-at-arms hired as
individuals under state-appointed commanders, a step toward direct state
control. **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The Italian domestication ran through
*market competition among fiscally-capable buyers* — not, as in France,
through royal monopoly (ch10's compagnies). Venice supplies the
stick-and-carrot pair: **Carmagnola**, summoned on pretext, tried, and
beheaded between arrest and sentence (**5 May 1432**); **Colleoni**,
captain-general **for life (1455)**, estates, castle, and the Verrocchio
statue — execution for the suspect captain, tenure and bronze for the loyal
one. [CONSENSUS, tertiary; **UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Mallett & Hale** for
the institutional apparatus (provveditori oversight); 1454-vs-1455 variant
flagged.]

## 3. Sforza — the sovereign who came from nothing

**[CONSENSUS]** Francesco Sforza (1401–66), son of the condottiere Muzio
Attendolo (drowned 1424; company inherited); served Papacy, Milan, Venice,
Naples; married **Bianca Maria Visconti** (25 October 1441 — 24th in one
source, flag), illegitimate daughter of Duke Filippo Maria, with a dowry of
Cremona-area territories. Filippo Maria died **13 August 1447** without male
heir; the **Golden Ambrosian Republic** hired Sforza as captain-general; he
defected to Venice (Oct 1448), blockaded Milan into famine, and after the
rising of 25 February entered the city on **26 February 1450**, acclaimed
duke **25 March 1450**. **He never obtained imperial investiture** (§0.1).
**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The enabling conditions, exactly as the outline
specifies and now dated: fiscally weak central authority (the republic's
collapse), a fiscally strong city worth taking, dynastic vacuum, personal
military reputation, marriage strategy. Sforza is what a
contractor-to-sovereign transition *requires* — and the checklist is what
the modern cases fail.

## 5. The Hansa as proto-platform

**[CONSENSUS, calibrated per §0.3]** The League as a transnational
commercial actor with law, courts, coercion, and enclaves — and pointedly
*not* a state: no permanent administration, no treasury, no standing force,
never a legal person, membership fluid, decisions (Rezesse) not binding on
absent towns. Its apparatus: the **Hansetag** (first formal diet at Lübeck
— 1356 in one strong open source; 1358/"c. 1300" variants flagged; Dollinger
to adjudicate); layered law (Lübeck law's appellate hierarchy + diet
recesses + Kontor statutes — "Hanserecht" as a single code is a term to
avoid); the four **Kontors** — Novgorod's palisaded Peterhof (c. 1200; its
statute, the *Schra*, the oldest Kontor code; closed by Ivan III, 1494),
Bergen's Bryggen (stockfish; ended 1754), Bruges, and the London
**Steelyard** (walled enclave, own weigh-house and guildhall, privileges
from 1303's Carta Mercatoria to the 1503 Act; merchants expelled by
Elizabeth **1597**, the yard closed **1598**). Coercive capacity: the
**Confederation of Cologne (19 Nov 1367)** and the **Treaty of Stralsund
(24 May 1370)** — Scanian fortresses held 15 years, toll revenue, and a
**prior-consent right over the Danish succession** (not "veto"; §0.3).
**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The structural parallel to the hyperscaler platform
is exact where it matters — a non-territorial commercial actor operating
law, sanction, and enclave *across* the sovereignty of others — and the
disanalogies are the chapter's teaching: the Hansa had no centre, no
balance sheet, no personhood; the platforms have all three, which is why
they are *more* absorbable, not less (ch11's move). The Hansa's end is the
cautionary line: it "silently disintegrated" as territorial states
consolidated around it — Ivan III closed the Peterhof, Elizabeth closed
the Steelyard; the consolidating state ends the enclave. **[UNVERIFIED —
RETRIEVAL: Dollinger for the Hansetag date, the Stralsund wording, and
Bergen's social regime (celibacy/separateness NOT FOUND openly).]**

## 6. Venice — the surveillance-state-before-the-state

**[CONSENSUS, with two flags]** The republic's apparatus, verified: the
**Council of Ten** (founded 1310 after the Tiepolo conspiracy; permanent
c. 1335 per Treccani — the 1455 "formalisation of powers" in EN-Wikipedia
noted as a distinct event; State Inquisitors 1539; abolished 1797) with
secret funds, informer networks, and police powers; the **bocche di
leone** denunciation boxes, with the crucial rule that anonymous
denunciations were *destroyed* unless of special gravity — the state
demanding attributable information (the exact voting-threshold rule is NOT
FOUND openly; **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Lane; Chambers & Pullan]**); the
**Arsenal** (largest industrial complex in pre-industrial Europe, ~45 ha,
~16,000 workers at peak, standardised assembly-line galley production —
the "galley a day" as surge anecdote only, §0.3); and the **prestiti** —
forced loans on the wealthy, consolidated **1262** into a single fund at
5% (paid semi-annually to 1371), with an active secondary market: the
first funded sovereign debt, the state making its own citizens its
creditors. (Open sourcing for the debt is a finance-history blog citing
Homer & Sylla — **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Lane & Mueller; Pezzolo]** for
Monte Vecchio nomenclature, the estimo, and the series.) **[BOOK'S
ARGUMENT]** Venice unsettles the assumption that surveillance is a modern
or an autocratic phenomenon: a *republic*, operating at commercial scale
in a plural jurisdictional environment, built denunciation
infrastructure, state manufacturing, and fiscal legibility three centuries
before Louis XIV — surveillance as a *state-form* phenomenon, arrived at
wherever commerce, scale, and insecurity meet. The prestiti are the
legibility half: to tax and borrow, the state must *see* wealth — the
estimo is India Stack's ancestor. [Flag: that last bridge is
