## 1. Kantorowicz re-read

**[CONSENSUS, via open reviews and public-domain texts]** The doctrine at the
chapter's head is Tudor legal fiction reported by Plowden (§0.1): the body
natural, mortal and subject to "the Imbecility of Infancy or old Age," and
the body politic "that cannot be seen or handled," in which "what the King
does… cannot be invalidated or frustrated by any Disability in his natural
Body"; the marginal gloss — "The King is always adjudged in Law of full Age"
— and Blackstone's maxim ("The king never dies") carrying the doctrine into
the classical constitution (Avalon, open). Kantorowicz's genealogy — from
the Norman Anonymous's *gemina persona* (c. 1100) through the
thirteenth-century juristic turn (the *corpus mysticum* migrating from
church to polity), Bracton, to the Tudor maturity — is confirmed in outline
by the open reviews (Folz; Imbert) and open scholarship on the *corpus
mysticum* transfer (Ruiz 2024, open access); the chapter-level detail is
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Kantorowicz]**. Maitland's scepticism (the
"metaphysiological nonsense" of the two bodies; the demise of the crown
stopping "all the wheels of state") is open and public-domain — and the
chapter should keep him in view: the fiction was always strained, which is
why its fifteenth-century breakage is diagnostic. **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The
doctrine's specific vulnerability: it presumes the natural body *executes*.
Where demand outgrows execution, the fiction must either break (1450s) or be
re-founded on institutions (the Tudor settlement).

## 2. Henry VI's incapacity as a two-body crisis

**[CONSENSUS, T1 verbatim]** The facts are ch05's; the constitutional
language is this chapter's, and it is now open: the Windsor deputation
(25 March 1454) pressing the insensible king "by all the waies and meanes
that they cowede thynke… but they cowede gete noo answere ne signe," entered
on the roll "of record"; two days later the lords electing York "Protectorem
& Defensorem… quamdiu Regi placeret"; York's protestation locating authority
in the peerage only "by th'occasion of th'enfirmite of oure said Souveraine
Lord," in whom "resteth th'exercice of his auctoritee"; and the office
itself defined negatively — *not* tutor, lieutenant, governor, or regent,
"nor noo name that shall emporte auctorite of governaunce," but a "personell
duete of entendaunce to the actuell defence of this land" (§0.1). **[BOOK'S
ARGUMENT]** This is the two-body doctrine operating as a *constraint*: the
political nation could not construct an executive substitute without
implying the king's authority was transferable — so it deliberately built a
non-substitute, revocable at pleasure, prejudicing neither king nor prince.
The protectorate failed as an institution because it was *designed* to fail
as one. The Watts framing (Henry's kingship sustained as a fiction by the
lords) is **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Watts]**, with open proxies noted
(Hicks's TMR review naming the "Cambridge School" argument).

## 3. Charles VI as the deeper case

**[CONSENSUS]** Facts in ch05 (the 1392 onset; intermittency; the
Armagnac–Burgundian pathology). This chapter adds the *institutional
experiments*: the **ordinance of January 1393** naming Isabeau co-guardian
hedged by a council of twelve, and the **April 1403 ordinance** providing
majority rule in council during the king's "absences" — both verified from
an open CC-licensed De Gruyter chapter (Adams), footnoted to the printed
*Ordonnances des rois de France* (vols. VII–VIII, public-domain scans
catalogued via BnF). **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** France, unlike England, tried to
*legislate* the substitute in advance — and intermittency defeated it
anyway: each lucid interval re-personalised authority and re-opened the
ducal contest. Charles VI is the deeper case because the fiction was
punctured repeatedly rather than once. **[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL:
Famiglietti; Autrand, *Charles VI*.]**

## 4. The regency solutions and their consistent failure

**[CONSENSUS, open-sourced end to end]** The fact-pattern across the century
is now documented from open texts: **1422** — the lords reject Gloucester's
claim to the regency ("no justification... of priority of blood"), style
Bedford/Gloucester "Protector and Defensor" with patronage capped at minor
offices, real control vested in a quorate council, commissions "during the
King's pleasure" (Vickers, public domain, resting on the rolls; Kleineke,
History of Parliament, open); **1454** — the negative definition (§2),
terminable at pleasure; **1455–56** — the same limited terms, dismissal now
by king in parliament, the Prince of Wales's rights safeguarded, governance
questions reserved to the council (Lander, open access, quoting *Rot. Parl.*
v. 285–90); **France** — Troyes cl. 7 vesting the regency in Henry V while
the dual monarchy preserved each kingdom's laws (Fordham text, open), and
the 1393/1403 ordinances (§3). The pattern: every experiment was limited,
revocable, council-bound, and built to preserve the personal constitution it
temporarily replaced. The *framing* — repeated institutional experiments
that consistently failed to substitute for an adult functioning king — has
no single open scholarly statement (**NOT FOUND**; Watts/Griffiths/Guenée
gated) and is presented as the book's reading of an open fact-pattern, (c)
not (a).

## 5. The Elton thesis, in miniature

**[CONSENSUS on the debate; the "weak thesis" label is the book's]** The
thesis and its fortunes are carried by Collinson's open British Academy
memoir: the 1530s as the moment the English state "took leave of the Middle
Ages" — household government giving way to a Westminster bureaucracy, privy
council, and "chief national executive," with "Cromwell, not Henry… really
the government"; challenged at birth (Wernham, *EHR* 1956), besieged in
*Past & Present* 25–32 (1963–65: Harriss, Williams, Cooper; Elton's replies
— full citation chain verified against the OUP issue pages, texts gated),
and revised by Elton's own pupils (Starkey's privy-chamber counter;
Coleman & Starkey, *Revolution Reassessed*, 1986), with Elton conceding in
1991 that he may have "overstated [Cromwell's] systematic approach."
**[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The chapter needs only the weak thesis — a sustained
personal-to-institutional transition across the fifteenth and sixteenth
centuries — which no side of the debate contests; the label "weak thesis" is
the book's own shorthand and is marked as such. The draft engages the debate
as the ch06 Rogers framing engages the military-revolution debate: internal
to the mechanism, a quarrel over tempo and agency, not over direction.
**[UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL: Elton 1953; the five P&P pieces; Coleman &
Starkey; Starkey.]**

## 6. The modern personalisation

**[CONSENSUS, primary-documented; flags per §0.3]** The modern natural body
carries continental-scale political bodies, and the constitutional documents
say so:

- **The founder-sovereign, chartered.** Meta 2026 DEF 14A: Zuckerberg holds
  99.8% of Class B, **60.8% of total voting power**; Meta is a Nasdaq
  "controlled company" by its own disclosure. Alphabet 2026 DEF 14A: Page
  and Brin jointly **~52.7%** of votes via Class B. Ritter's open dataset:
  **48.4% of 2025 tech IPOs** carried dual-class structures (vs ~0% in
  1980) — the personal constitution as the *default charter* of the new
  continental institutions. [T1: SEC filings; open dataset.]
- **The extreme case.** Musk simultaneously heads six ventures (Tesla 19.8%
  beneficial ownership per the 2025 proxy; SpaceX control reported-only,
  §0.3). The Starlink/Crimea episode — in its corrected form: Ukraine asked
  Musk to enable coverage for the drone attack; he declined, citing
  escalation — is the natural body making a war-relevant decision no office
  constrained; the state's answer (DoD Starlink contracts; Starshield,
  "owned and controlled by the US government") is the improvised
  institutional substitute arriving on schedule. [Attributed; contested
  details flagged.] DOGE (EO of 20 Jan 2025; Musk's exit May 2025; disbanded
  Nov 2025 per OPM) as personalised authority *inside* the state — created
  by order, dissolved by interview, its claimed savings contested. [Dated
  episode; per the ch03 rule, drafted as such.]
- **Presidentialisation and routinisation.** Poguntke & Webb's thesis
  (gated; three-faces summary to verify against the text); the American
  Presidency Project's open table: Trump II running at **179 executive
  orders/year, the highest rate since FDR**. China: the 2018 term-limit
  removal (NPC: 2,958 for, 2 against, 3 abstentions — NPR, verified); Xi
  chairing six of eleven leading small groups (Miller, Hoover, open PDF) —
  "informal, off-the-books mechanisms" of personalised coordination, several
  upgraded to formal Commissions in 2018 (the routinisation datum).
  **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** Across systems, the same signature: authority
  migrating from office to person, then — under load — being re-clothed in
  improvised institutional dress.

## 7. The pending settlement — the indicators

**[CONSENSUS on the instruments; BOOK'S ARGUMENT on the reading]** The
improvised substitutes are now documentable as institutions:

- **Trust-and-safety professionalisation:** DTSP (founded Feb 2021), its
  framework now **ISO/IEC 25389**; and — the cleanest single citation —
  **DSA Article 41** *requiring* VLOPs to establish "a compliance function,
  independent from their operational functions," whose head "shall not be
  removed without prior approval of the management body": law compelling an
  internal counterweight to founder discretion. [T1: EUR-Lex.]
- **Institutional AI governance:** the UK AISI (renamed AI Security
  Institute, Feb 2025), NIST's CAISI, the EU AI Office (>125 staff);
  Anthropic's Long-Term Benefit Trust (Class T stock; trustees electing a
  board majority within four years — primary announcement); and the **OpenAI
  November 2023 episode** as the test case: the board fired the founder
  ("not consistently candid"), the founder returned in five days with a new
  board — the nonprofit board's formal power proving unenforceable against
  founder + employees + Microsoft. [Timeline verified; signatory counts
  flagged.] **[BOOK'S ARGUMENT]** The test case cuts both ways and the draft
  must say so: the substitute *existed* (a board that could fire the
  founder) and *failed* (he came back) — Charles VI's lucid interval in
  corporate form. Institutionalisation is pending, not achieved.
- **Succession as governance:** SEC Staff Legal Bulletin 14E (2009)
  reclassifying CEO succession from "ordinary business" to a shareholder
  governance issue — the regulator formalising the two-body problem. [T1.]

The chapter closes forward into ch10: the fifteenth century's answer to the
broken fiction was not a better regency ordinance but the bureaucratic
state — offices, records, salaried officials — and the fiscal-military
pressure that forced it is the next chapter's subject.
