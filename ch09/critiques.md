Objection 1 is the outline's named stress test; Objections 2–5 surfaced in
research. Each steelmanned before the answer (§6). Perplexity run logged at
the foot.

## Objection 1 — The Elton-revisionist complaint (named)

**Steelman.** The chapter hangs its historical anchor on a thesis its own
author half-abandoned. The Tudor Revolution was "challenged at birth"
(Wernham 1956), battered through five rounds of *Past & Present* (1963–65),
and dismantled in the 1980s by Elton's own pupils — Starkey showing the
privy chamber's personal monarchy persisting straight through the
"bureaucratic revolution"; Elton himself conceding in 1991 that he
"overstated" Cromwell's system-building. To rest a modern argument on the
1953 thesis is to build on a position no working Tudor historian now holds;
and the chapter's fallback — a "weak thesis" of gradual
personal-to-institutional transition — is a label the literature does not
use and so vague no one could deny it. Unfalsifiable rescue by dilution.

**The chapter's answer.** The chapter engages the debate *as the material*,
not as an obstacle (§5 of the doctrine: the debate itself is often the
material). It needs only the claim every combatant affirms: that between the
fifteenth and later sixteenth centuries English government moved, unevenly
and with reversals, from household-personal to institutional-bureaucratic
form — Harriss's medieval antecedents *push the transition earlier*, and
Starkey's persistent privy chamber shows it *incomplete*, but neither denies
the direction; they relocate and slow it. That is not dilution to vacuity:
the weak thesis still rules out real alternatives (that personal monarchy
was stable and sufficient; that no institutional substitute was being
built), and it is precisely what the chapter's two-bodies argument requires
— the fiction breaking under load and institutions accreting to carry what
the natural body could not. The "weak thesis" label is marked as the book's
own (c). And the debate's shape is turned to use, as with Rogers in ch06:
a quarrel over tempo and agency *inside* an agreed direction of travel.

## Objection 2 — Kantorowicz at second hand

**Steelman.** The chapter is named for a book it has not read. Kantorowicz
is gated; the memo's genealogy rests on two 1950s–60s reviews, a publisher's
page, and tertiary summaries. The chapter risks doing to Kantorowicz what
ch01 was warned against doing to Bull — building a foundation on
intermediaries — and worse, the two-bodies doctrine it actually deploys is
Plowden's Tudor lawyering, which Maitland already had (and mocked) in 1901.
What does Kantorowicz add that the chapter can honestly claim?

**The chapter's answer.** The load-bearing texts are not Kantorowicz's — and
that is the answer. The chapter's two anchor quotations (Plowden 212a–213;
*Rot. Parl.* v. 240–42) are open, public-domain, and verified verbatim; the
doctrine, the maxims (Blackstone), and the sceptical counterweight
(Maitland) are all directly readable. Kantorowicz supplies the *frame* — the
genealogy from liturgical to juristic kingship — and the memo carries that
frame only at the level the open reviews (Folz, Imbert) and open
scholarship (Ruiz on *corpus mysticum*) confirm, with every chapter-level
claim flagged [UNVERIFIED — RETRIEVAL]. Until the book is in the repo, the
draft cites "Kantorowicz's argument as summarised in the contemporary
reviews," never the chapters directly — the ch01 Baaz discipline, applied.
The exposure is bounded and declared; the primary evidence is first-hand.

## Objection 3 — The 1454 evidence proves resilience, not breakage

**Steelman.** The chapter reads the protectorate's "negative definition" as
the fiction failing. Read it the other way: the constitution *worked*. Faced
with a catatonic king, the political nation improvised a caretaker within
weeks, kept government running, preserved the succession, and wound the
expedient up on recovery — twice. The 1422 minority council governed
respectably for fifteen years. The two-bodies doctrine did exactly what a
legal fiction is for: it bridged the gap. The wars came from faction
(York–Somerset, the queen), not from any constitutional incapacity to
govern without a functioning king.

**The chapter's answer.** Distinguish *bridging* from *substituting* — the
chapter's claim is about the latter, and the documents make the distinction
themselves. The 1454 settlement is defined by what it refuses: no regent, no
"auctorite of governaunce," a "personell duete" only, at the king's
pleasure. It could keep the machine idling; it could not *execute* — could
not decide the Somerset question, could not command the affinity system,
could not do what ch05 shows the crisis demanded (fiscal-military
reconstruction of the English position). The proof is the sequel: each
recovery reversed the protectorate's acts and re-opened the contest, and
within a year of the second protectorate's end the question was settled at
St Albans by force — the one arbiter the fiction could not conscript. The
1422 counter-case is conceded and used: a *minority* has a certain future
king and a settled horizon; an adult king's *incapacity* has neither —
which is why the same constitutional device succeeded in one case and
failed in the other. The comparison is in the chapter, not against it.

## Objection 4 — The modern "two-body problem" is a category error

**Steelman.** A founder-CEO is not a king. Zuckerberg's 60.8% is a
*property* right, alienable and dilutable; the corporation is precisely the
institution that solved the two-body problem — it never dies, and its
officers are replaceable by charter. The modern examples cut the other way:
the OpenAI board *did* fire the founder; DOGE *was* dissolved; Starshield
*did* institutionalise Starlink's war role. The state and the market are
thick with functioning substitutes; there is no crisis of personalisation,
only normal governance friction dressed in Plowden's robes.

**The chapter's answer.** The corporation solved the two-body problem *in
form*; the dual-class charter re-opens it *in fact* — and the chapter's
evidence is the constitutional documents themselves, not resemblance. When
48.4% of new tech charters entrench one natural person's control (Ritter),
when Meta's own proxy declares it a "controlled company," when a board that
exercised its formal power to remove the founder saw him return in five
days, the *de jure* substitute exists and the *de facto* personal
constitution persists — which is precisely the fifteenth-century structure
(a fiction of institutional continuity; a reality of personal execution).
The objection's counter-examples are the chapter's own §7: they show the
substitutes being *improvised and tested* — DSA Article 41 compelling an
independent compliance function by law, the SEC reclassifying succession,
Starshield nationalising the discretion Musk had exercised. The chapter's
claim is calibrated exactly there: substitutes emerging, not yet mature —
"pending settlement," with the OpenAI episode cited as evidence for *both*
halves. The disanalogies (property not sacrament; markets not estates) are
stated per §6, and any passage leaning on the robes rather than the
structure is [ANALOGY-ONLY].

## Objection 5 — Contested episodes are doing rhetorical work

**Steelman.** The chapter's modern colour rests on shaky testimony: the
Starlink/Crimea story was *corrected after publication* by its own author;
Musk's account is self-serving; DOGE's savings figures are disputed; the
OpenAI letter counts are unverified; SpaceX's voting structure is not
public. A chapter about the dangers of personalised power is retailing
personalised anecdotes.

**The chapter's answer.** Conceded, and the memo already applies the triage
(§0.3): the Starlink episode appears only in its corrected
declined-to-enable form with both parties' statements attributed; the
SpaceX figure is excluded from assertion (reported-only); DOGE's savings are
cited as claimed-and-contested; the letter counts are dropped for CNN's
verified "hundreds." What remains load-bearing is not anecdote but filed
documents: proxy statements, the EO, the statute, the SEC bulletin, the
Ritter series. The anecdotes illustrate; the filings carry. This is the
ch03 [RE-CHECK AT DRAFT] discipline generalised — every live episode is a
dated episode, and the draft will re-verify each at writing.
