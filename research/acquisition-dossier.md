## 1. Philippe Contamine — *Guerre, État et société à la fin du Moyen Âge*

**ACCESS:** BUY / LIBRARY · **EVIDENCE:** C: review + record · **PRIORITY:** ANCHOR · **DECISION:** Buy

**Why this source matters.** This is the major institutional bridge between
late-medieval warfare and the consolidation of French royal capacity. It is
the most important unresolved dependency for the chapter on artillery, fiscal
organisation, and the transformation of armed service.

**Research digest.** Contamine reconstructs the armies of the French kings
from the opening of the Hundred Years War to the end of the fifteenth
century. The book is not merely a narrative of campaigns. Its object is the
changing social composition, legal status, payment, recruitment, command, and
administration of armed forces, and therefore the reciprocal relation between
war and royal government.

The central historical movement is from episodic feudal and contractual
mobilisation toward more durable royal military institutions. Companies,
garrisons, musters, taxation, ordinances, the compagnies d'ordonnance, the
francs-archers, and artillery become parts of a system whose continuity
depended on record keeping and revenue as much as battlefield technique. The
book therefore supplies the institutional mechanisms that a technological
account of cannon alone cannot provide.

For the artillery chapter, Contamine should be used to explain why a gun
train was a state capacity rather than simply a weapon. Procurement, powder
and shot, specialised labour, horses, transport, inspection, and recurrent
pay created a long logistical tail. The ability to maintain that tail helps
explain why the Valois monarchy could convert improvements in siege artillery
into a systematic reconquest, while individual lords could rarely reproduce
the same capability at scale.

For the later comparison with modern sovereignty, the book is valuable
because it avoids a teleological leap from war to the modern state. Its
evidence concerns a monarchy that accumulated capabilities through bargains,
privileges, offices, and layered jurisdictions. The result is stronger
central coordination without the disappearance of medieval social and
corporate structures.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Institutional claim.* Permanent or semi-permanent forces require fiscal, archival, and supervisory structures; their significance cannot be reduced to combat performance.
- *Artillery claim.* Effective siege artillery was embedded in a royal system of specialists, finance, transport, and command.
- *State-formation claim.* Late-medieval war helped enlarge the reach and regularity of royal government, but did so through negotiated institutions rather than instantaneous sovereignty.
- *Comparative claim.* The strongest causal language should connect military innovation to administrative capacity, not claim that cannon mechanically created the state.

**Critical cautions.** The complete monograph was not available in the
audited sessions. This digest is based on the bibliographic record, a
contemporary scholarly review, and the well-established place of the book in
the historiography. It should guide acquisition and argument design, but
page-specific claims and quotations must wait for the book itself.

**Short quotation bank.** "une contribution de premier ordre à l'histoire de
l'État moderne" — Raymond Cazelles's review, describing the book's
importance; review p. 293.

edition. No lawful complete open copy or Cambridge-alumni entitlement was

buy the digital or print edition matching the manuscript's citation scheme.

## 3. John Watts — *Henry VI and the Politics of Kingship*

**ACCESS:** CAMBRIDGE CORE · **EVIDENCE:** A: chapters in hand · **PRIORITY:** ANCHOR · **DECISION:** Do not buy

**Why this source matters.** Watts supplies the interpretive spine for
understanding Henry VI's reign: authority was constitutionally concentrated
in the king but politically produced through counsel, household, patronage,
and acceptance by the realm's leading interests.

**Research digest.** Watts rejects a simple story in which a minority
government ended, an adult king assumed normal power, and a malign court
faction then captured him. In the transition of 1435–45, the king's authority
was real in law but had to be activated and mediated. Subjects increasingly
drew on Henry's authorising power, while councillors managed the pace and
form of the transfer from minority institutions to personal rule.

The book's key analytic move is to treat kingship as a political office
embedded in expectations. Counsel was not the opposite of monarchy. Proper
kingship required a will directed toward the common weal, made effective by
advisers, officers, household connections, and distributive justice. The
problem under Henry was therefore not simply that magnates were too
powerful; it was that the focal institution that should coordinate them did
not work reliably.

Chapter 5 shows why the transition was slow, public, and incomplete. The
household regime that emerged was an 'artificial' solution: it made royal
authority usable, but concentrated access and patronage in ways that were
difficult to legitimate across territorial and aristocratic interests.
Chapter 6 then traces the court-centred regime of 1445–50 and the route
toward political crisis.

This framework disciplines causal claims about defeat in France. Military
loss mattered because it increased distributive strain, discredited
counsellors, and exposed the inability of royal institutions to reconcile
competing claims. It did not simply cause domestic collapse from the
outside. Watts therefore connects the French war to the politics of kingship
without reducing the English crisis to military determinism.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Constitutional claim.* The crown remained the unique source of legitimate public authority even when the king was personally ineffective.
- *Institutional claim.* Council and court were competing modes of organising access to royal authority, not merely good and bad factions.
- *Causal claim.* Henry's deficiencies mattered because English political society expected purposeful, distributive, and counselled kingship.
- *Manuscript use.* Use Watts to explain the political mechanism linking war, patronage, household rule, and the crisis of 1450.

**Critical cautions.** Do not turn Watts into a psychological diagnosis of
Henry. His argument is institutional and ideological. Pair it with Griffiths
for administrative density, Wilkinson only for the 1453 illness episode, and
Pollard/Curry for the military sequence.

**Short quotation bank.** "There seems to me to be little evidence that
Henry himself was the author" — Watts, chapter 5, p. 123.

**Acquisition route.** Confirmed alumni full text: Cambridge Core book. The
signed-in alumni session displayed full digital access. Chapters 5 and 6 are
saved in the source library (cambridge-core).

## 4. Walter Ullmann — *The Growth of Papal Government in the Middle Ages*

**ACCESS:** OPEN LIBRARY LOAN · **EVIDENCE:** B: loan verified · **PRIORITY:** ANCHOR · **DECISION:** Borrow first

**Why this source matters.** Ullmann supplies the juristic and ideological
backbone for medieval papal government: the papacy's claim to govern
followed from a descending conception of authority, elaborated through law,
office, and the corporate structure of the church.

**Research digest.** Ullmann presents papal government as a coherent
medieval theory and practice of rule, not an accidental collection of
clerical privileges. The papal office stood at the apex of a divinely
grounded order in which authority descended through a hierarchy. Canon law,
decretals, jurisdiction, and the institutional differentiation of the church
gave that theory administrative form.

The book's longue durée moves through late antique and Carolingian
inheritances, the reform papacy, the investiture conflict, and the mature
medieval language of plenitudo potestatis. Its importance lies in showing
that universal claims were governmental: they created procedures, appellate
jurisdictions, offices, and a translocal legal order, even when practical
enforcement was uneven.

For a chapter on medieval pluralism, Ullmann is indispensable but must be
used dialectically. Papal monarchy was one of the two most ambitious
universalising projects in Latin Christendom; its friction with empire,
kingdoms, bishops, religious orders, cities, and customary jurisdictions
demonstrates that universal claims did not produce a unitary state system.

The book is best paired with Morris and Tierney. Morris adds social,
pastoral, and institutional history to Ullmann's juristic emphasis. Tierney
shows how the same canonistic tradition furnished corporate and conciliar
limits on supreme authority.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Ideational claim.* Medieval governance included universal and translocal sources of legitimate authority.
- *Institutional claim.* Canon law and papal jurisdiction created real governmental capacities across territorial boundaries.
- *Pluralism claim.* Papal monarchy sharpened, rather than eliminated, conflict among overlapping authorities.
- *Genealogical claim.* Conciliar and corporate limits emerged within the same legal world that articulated plenary papal power.

**Critical cautions.** Ullmann's top-down 'descending' model has been
criticised for making ideology too systematic and for underweighting social
practice, negotiation, and local variation. Do not treat it as the
uncontested description of medieval politics; use it as a powerful account
of one governing logic.

**Quotation status.** No quotation is supplied because the complete text was
not in hand. Obtain the lawful loan or purchased copy before quoting.

**Acquisition route.** Free account-based controlled loan: Open Library
record. Borrow the 1955 scan, then capture page references and short
quotations in research notes. The loan cannot lawfully be converted into an
unrestricted PDF.

**Footnotes and evidence trail.**
1. Walter Ullmann, *The Growth of Papal Government in the Middle Ages* (London: Methuen, 1955).
2. Access finding: Open Library account-based loan; no Cambridge-alumni full-text entitlement was identified.
3. For balance, read Colin Morris, *The Papal Monarchy*, and Brian Tierney, *Foundations of the Conciliar Theory*.

## 5. Hedley Bull — *The Anarchical Society*

**ACCESS:** PREVIEW / BUY · **EVIDENCE:** C: preview · **PRIORITY:** HIGH · **DECISION:** Buy or library

**Why this source matters.** Bull anchors the book's modern conceptual
vocabulary: an international society can sustain order without world
government, through shared rules and institutions operating under anarchy.

**Research digest.** Bull distinguishes an international system — states
affecting one another — from an international society, in which states
recognise common interests and values, accept rules, and participate in
common institutions. This distinction lets the manuscript separate the
existence of interaction from the thicker question of legitimate order.

Order is defined through elementary goals: preservation of the society of
states, maintenance of the independence of member states, peace understood
as the normal condition rather than the abolition of war, and limits on
violence, agreement, and possession. Balance of power, international law,
diplomacy, war, and great-power management are the principal institutions
that reproduce this order.

Chapter 10 is especially valuable because Bull tests alternatives to the
state system, including a world of overlapping authorities often described
through the medieval analogy. The conclusion is methodologically useful: it
resists both complacent state-centrism and the easy claim that any erosion
of sovereignty means that the state system has already ended.

For the concluding chapter, Bull should be used as a benchmark. The
manuscript's 'new medievalism' argument becomes stronger when it specifies
which functions of international society persist, which are redistributed to
other actors, and whether order still depends on states even when authority
is fragmented.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Conceptual claim.* Anarchy is compatible with society and rule-governed order.
- *Institutional claim.* Balance, law, diplomacy, war, and great powers are practices through which order is made.
- *Comparative claim.* A neo-medieval order should be tested against concrete functions, not inferred from blurred borders alone.
- *Conclusion use.* Bull provides the strongest language for asking whether contemporary overlap is transformation, supplementation, or replacement.

**Critical cautions.** The requested verbatim use of chapter 10 and the
conclusion cannot be satisfied from a limited preview. Editions differ in
introductions and supplementary chapters; choose one edition and keep all
page citations consistent.

**Quotation status.** None supplied — text not in hand.

**Acquisition route.** Publisher/retail route (Bloomsbury title page).
Internet Archive records located in this pass were preview-only
(https://archive.org/details/anarchicalsociet00bull). A library ebook or
print copy is more dependable for quotation than waiting for a
controlled-loan slot.

## 6. Martin Wight — *Systems of States*

**ACCESS:** LOCATE / BUY · **EVIDENCE:** C: record + scholarship · **PRIORITY:** MEDIUM · **DECISION:** Library request

**Why this source matters.** Wight extends the analysis beyond the modern
European system by comparing historical state systems and asking what common
culture, legitimacy, and diplomatic practices allow a plurality of polities
to form a society.

**Research digest.** Edited after Wight's death, *Systems of States* gathers
historical and theoretical essays on the conditions under which multiple
political units recognise one another and sustain patterned relations.
Ancient Greece and modern Europe are central cases, but the comparative
purpose is to distinguish a states-system from suzerainty or empire.

A system is not simply a set of adjacent powers. It depends on mutual
recognition and often on a common cultural or civilisational framework that
supplies rules of membership, diplomacy, war, and legitimacy. This makes
Wight a useful precursor to Bull's more systematic account of international
society.

For the manuscript, Wight helps prevent the term 'medieval system' from
becoming a loose metaphor. The analytical question is whether kings,
princes, cities, ecclesiastical authorities, and empires constituted a
single system, several intersecting systems, or a hierarchical order that
only partly resembled a society of states.

Wight also sharpens the empire/state-system boundary. Overlap does not
itself imply pluralism: an imperial order may contain many rulers while
reserving ultimate rank and legitimacy to a centre. Conversely, a society of
states may operate with inequalities and hegemonic practices without
becoming an empire.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Comparative claim.* State systems are historically variable and should not be defined solely by modern sovereignty.
- *Cultural claim.* Common culture and legitimacy can be constitutive of a system's rules.
- *Taxonomic claim.* Differentiate a plurality of states from suzerainty, hierarchy, and empire.
- *Manuscript use.* Use Wight as the bridge between medieval cases and Bull's English School framework.

**Critical cautions.** The book is posthumously edited and essayistic. Treat
it as a comparative conceptual resource, not as a single continuously
demonstrated thesis. The complete text was not in hand.

**Quotation status.** None supplied — text not in hand.

**Acquisition route.** Internet Archive locate record only; no verified
borrowable scan. Use WorldCat/interlibrary loan or purchase a used copy.

## 7. Joseph R. Strayer — *On the Medieval Origins of the Modern State*

**ACCESS:** PREVIEW / BUY · **EVIDENCE:** C: preview · **PRIORITY:** HIGH · **DECISION:** Buy or library

**Why this source matters.** Strayer is the classic concise statement of how
persistent territorial rule, specialised institutions, and political loyalty
emerged from medieval conditions. It is the foil against which an
anti-teleological argument should define itself.

**Research digest.** Strayer identifies state formation through durable
political units, stable territorial attachment, impersonal and specialised
governing institutions, and a shift of loyalty toward political authority.
His chronology centres on the centuries after about 1100, when justice,
finance, archives, and administration became more continuous.

The church is both rival and model. Its translocal legal and administrative
sophistication demonstrated what organised government could do, while its
jurisdictional claims forced secular rulers to define and defend their own
competences. Royal courts and fiscal offices then helped make rule more
regular and less dependent on a ruler's household alone.

Strayer is useful precisely because his argument is compact and directional.
The manuscript can adopt the institutional indicators while resisting the
implication that medieval plurality was merely an unfinished state. A polity
may acquire durable courts and taxation while authority remains divided
among lords, towns, churches, estates, and customary communities.

The strongest use is diagnostic rather than celebratory: ask which of
Strayer's capacities existed, at what scale, and through what bargains. This
turns 'origins' into a research programme rather than a claim that the
modern state was already latent in every institutional innovation.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Definition claim.* Durability, territory, specialised institutions, and loyalty are practical indicators of state development.
- *Church–state claim.* Ecclesiastical government simultaneously competed with and instructed secular government.
- *Method claim.* Institutional growth can be measured without assuming a completed sovereign state.
- *Comparative claim.* Use Strayer's indicators to compare medieval and modern transformations while keeping differences in jurisdiction visible.

**Critical cautions.** The essay is deliberately synthetic and has attracted
criticism for teleology and a Western European trajectory treated as
general. **The authenticated Cambridge JSTOR gateway explicitly reported
that the institution did not have access to this JSTOR ebook.**

**Quotation status.** None supplied — text not in hand.

**Acquisition route.** Official digital edition: Princeton/De Gruyter. JSTOR
stable book URL: https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctt7t79c. Cambridge alumni

## 8. Malcolm Vale — *War and Chivalry*

**ACCESS:** IA CONTROLLED LOAN · **EVIDENCE:** B: loan + review · **PRIORITY:** HIGH · **DECISION:** Borrow

**Why this source matters.** Vale shows that chivalry was an operating
social and political culture of late-medieval war — not a decorative
survival left behind by infantry, guns, or royal administration.

**Research digest.** Vale studies warfare and aristocratic culture in
England, France, and Burgundy at the end of the Middle Ages. Court life,
military reputation, orders of chivalry, tournaments, ceremonial, ransom,
honour, and household service form a connected field in which martial
conduct created status and political relationships.

The book complicates a modernisation narrative. Infantry, mercenaries,
professional captains, and gunpowder weapons did not simply displace
aristocratic martial culture. Chivalric values adapted to new conditions and
continued to organise recruitment, command, alliance, commemoration, and the
meaning attached to violence.

For a chapter centred on Talbot, Vale explains why personal audacity and
reputation could have institutional effects. A commander's honour attracted
retainers, validated leadership, and shaped expectations about action. That
culture could coexist with administrative professionalism and still create
incentives that look irrational from a purely technical perspective.

For Burgundy, chivalric orders and court display were tools of statecraft.
They bound elites to the prince and represented political community. This
makes Vale a useful complement to Vaughan: one explains the cultural
machinery, the other the dynastic and administrative project.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Cultural claim.* Chivalry structured behaviour, reputation, and political association; it was not merely literary rhetoric.
- *Continuity claim.* Military technology and aristocratic culture evolved together rather than in a simple replacement sequence.
- *Talbot claim.* Honour and reputation help explain command decisions and the political resonance of battlefield conduct.
- *Burgundian claim.* Courtly institutions could integrate elites and project princely authority.

**Critical cautions.** Do not romanticise chivalry or treat it as a single
code. Its norms varied by context and could rationalise brutality,
self-interest, and status competition. The delivered PDF is Maurice Keen's
review, not Vale's book.

**Short quotation bank.** "a substantial and important study" — Maurice
Keen's review of Vale, *Medium Ævum* 52 (1983), p. 117.

**Acquisition route.** Verified controlled-loan scan:
https://archive.org/details/warchivalrywarfa0000vale. Borrow with a free

## 9. Christopher Allmand — *The Hundred Years War*

**ACCESS:** CAMBRIDGE CORE · **EVIDENCE:** B: entitlement verified · **PRIORITY:** HIGH · **DECISION:** Do not buy

**Why this source matters.** Allmand provides the broadest dependable
synthesis for the war's causes, institutions, military practices, financial
burdens, social effects, and political consequences.

**Research digest.** Allmand treats the Hundred Years War as more than a
sequence of battles. Dynastic claims, jurisdiction, territory, diplomacy,
taxation, manpower, military organisation, and political opinion are
integrated into a long conflict whose intensity and geography changed over
time.

The book is especially useful for showing how war was sustained. Assemblies
and political communities had to accept fiscal demands; armies depended on
contracts, wages, retinues, shipping, garrisons, and local cooperation;
occupation depended on administration as well as conquest. The war therefore
enlarged state activity while repeatedly exposing its limits.

Allmand also keeps the two kingdoms in view. English successes could create
commitments beyond the crown's fiscal and demographic base, while French
recovery depended on political reconstruction as well as military
improvement. The narrative of inevitable French centralisation and English
decline becomes a contingent process.

For the manuscript, this should be the chronological control source. More
specialised works — Pollard on Talbot, Watts and Griffiths on Henry VI,
Contamine and Curry on institutions, Rogers and DeVries on military change —
can then make narrower causal claims without carrying the whole narrative
burden.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Chronology claim.* Use Allmand to verify phases, turning points, and the changing scale of the conflict.
- *Capacity claim.* War mobilised taxation, administration, manpower, and political consent.
- *Occupation claim.* Holding territory required garrisons, finance, justice, and local collaboration.
- *Comparative claim.* English and French trajectories should be analysed relationally rather than as separate national stories.

**Critical cautions.** This is a synthetic interpretation. For contested
battle numbers, artillery effects, or individual decisions, return to
specialised studies and primary sources. Full alumni access was visible, but
the entire book was not downloaded in this pass.

**Quotation status.** None supplied — text not in hand.

**Acquisition route.** Confirmed alumni access: Cambridge Core book. The
signed-in session showed 'You have access.' IA versions were previews, so

## 10. Anne Curry — *The Hundred Years War* (book)

**ACCESS:** PREVIEW / BUY · **EVIDENCE:** C: preview · **PRIORITY:** MEDIUM · **DECISION:** Library or buy

**Why this source matters.** Curry offers a compact, analytically alert
synthesis by a leading specialist, useful for checking chronology,
participation, military organisation, and the changing historiography of
the war.

**Research digest.** Curry's synthesis explains the dynastic and territorial
issues behind the conflict, its intermittent phases, the organisation of
armies, and the interaction of campaigns with domestic politics. Her work is
particularly attentive to documentary evidence on soldiers, contracts,
garrisons, and the social breadth of participation.

Unlike older battle-centred narratives, Curry places operations within
systems of recruitment, finance, occupation, diplomacy, and political
choice. This makes the book a useful shorter counterpart to Allmand and a
guide to more specialised studies.

For the manuscript, Curry is most valuable as a corrective to inherited
labels. 'Hundred Years War' can imply a continuous, unified struggle;
'military revolution' can impose a single turning point. Curry instead
insists on phases, contingencies, and the changing meaning of war for
participants.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Chronology claim.* The war consisted of distinct phases rather than uninterrupted conflict.
- *Participation claim.* Contractual recruitment and garrison service broaden the analysis beyond noble commanders.
- *Method claim.* Administrative records can revise battle-centred or nationally teleological stories.
- *Manuscript use.* Use Curry alongside Allmand for orientation, then cite specialist work for decisive causal claims.

**Critical cautions.** Several books with this title and different editions
exist. Confirm the exact edition intended by the manuscript before fixing
page numbers. The IA records found were previews, not loans.

**Quotation status.** None supplied — text not in hand.

## 11. Kelly DeVries — *Guns and Men in Medieval Europe*; with DeVries and Smith

**ACCESS:** IA LOAN / PREVIEW · **EVIDENCE:** B: loan + article · **PRIORITY:** HIGH · **DECISION:** Borrow

**Why this source matters.** DeVries supplies a technologically informed but
anti-determinist account of medieval gunpowder, combat effectiveness, and
the institutional settings in which weapons mattered.

**Research digest.** *Guns and Men* collects studies of the relationship
between weaponry and the people, organisations, and tactical problems that
gave it effect. Early cannon were not a single stable technology: size,
mobility, powder, projectile, carriage, rate of fire, accuracy, and target
all changed, and effectiveness was contextual.

DeVries's wider argument is methodological. A weapon should not be evaluated
as an isolated machine or measured against modern expectations. Siege
artillery could be transformative against particular fortifications while
unreliable, immobile, or prohibitively expensive in other circumstances. The
relevant unit of analysis is a socio-technical system.

His open 1998 article directly challenges technological determinism in
Parker's state-formation claim while retaining a strong relationship between
gunpowder and the growth of early modern states. The causal chain runs
through expense, specialists, supply, and the capacity to sustain weapons —
not through an automatic superiority of guns.

DeVries and Smith's *Medieval Military Technology* is the broader reference
work. It can supply technical background on arms, armour, fortification,
artillery, ships, and siege practice, but the copy located was preview-only.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Technology claim.* Military effectiveness depends on the relationship among weapon, target, tactic, operator, and logistical system.

## 12. Clifford J. Rogers, ed. — *The Military Revolution Debate*

**Why this source matters.** This anthology is the historiographic control

**Research digest.** The volume assembles the central debate over whether

useful precisely because it exposes rival causal models. The manuscript can
use it to state a bounded thesis: artillery altered siege possibilities, but
outcomes depended on fiscal, logistical, political, and institutional
capacity.

- *Historiographic claim.* There is no single agreed date, mechanism, or consequence called 'the military revolution.'

credit, taxation, bargaining, and administration were mutually conditioning,

plural offices, corporate bodies, and emergency doctrines capable of
relocating authority.

representation, competence, and final judgment, but the medieval legal and
theological premises were distinct.

feeding and movement, and incapacity for government over roughly a year and
a half. He frames the episode as catatonic stupor while acknowledging the
retrospective nature of the evidence.

The article also connects Henry's posthumous cult to a surviving Latin
redaction of miracle depositions. Psychiatric and neuropsychiatric cases
appear within a devotional archive whose preservation and narrative form
served canonisation and healing claims.

For the manuscript, the article is useful for a compact description and a
citation to modern clinical vocabulary. It should not be used to explain the
whole reign. Watts and Griffiths remain necessary to show why the king's
illness had particular constitutional and political consequences.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Clinical description.* Henry's documented behaviour is consistent with a prolonged catatonic stupor.
- *Chronology claim.* The episode began after the loss at Castillon and disabled government into late 1454.
- *Archive claim.* Later miracle records provide evidence about Henry's cult and historical descriptions of mental disturbance.
- *Method claim.* Retrospective diagnosis must remain probabilistic and separate from political explanation.

**Critical cautions.** The piece is one page and belongs to 'Psychiatry in
history,' not a full clinical study. Historical symptoms are filtered
through chronicles and later records. Avoid asserting a definitive aetiology
or diagnosing the entire personality of Henry VI.

**Short quotation bank.** "did not talk, sat slumped, had to be fed and
moved" — Wilkinson, p. 177.

**Acquisition route.** Confirmed alumni full text: Cambridge Core article;

## 26. Anne Curry — "Guns and Goddams"

**ACCESS:** OPEN ACCESS · **EVIDENCE:** A: full text saved · **PRIORITY:** CRITICAL · **DECISION:** Acquired

**Why this source matters.** This is the highest-value open source for
testing whether Lancastrian Normandy belongs inside a military-revolution
narrative. It combines historiographic criticism with operational and
administrative evidence.

**Research digest.** Curry begins by questioning labels. 'Hundred Years War'
and 'military revolution' can organise research, but they can also impose
artificial unity and encourage historians to locate the decisive revolution
in their own period.

She tests Rogers's distinction between a fourteenth-century infantry
revolution and a fifteenth-century artillery revolution against Lancastrian
Normandy. English archers, dismounted men-at-arms, siege guns, garrisons,
occupation structures, and the reconquest of 1449–50 are considered within
their documentary setting.

The article's major contribution is to join weapons to government.
Lancastrian Normandy generated unusually rich administrative evidence
because occupation required captains, musters, wages, repairs, victualling,
revenue, and communication. Military capability was institutional capacity.

Curry's treatment of the French reconquest also prevents a cannon-only
explanation. Many places surrendered, were abandoned, or changed hands
through composition, treason, fear, and political collapse. Artillery
altered expectations and bargaining, but the mechanism included morale,
information, legitimacy, and the loss of an occupation system.

For the manuscript, this is the ideal source for a qualified thesis: the
artillery transformation was real, yet its decisive effects came through a
fiscal-administrative system and a cascading change in the perceived
defensibility of places.

**Claims it can support.**
- *Historiography claim.* 'Military revolution' is a contested and movable label.
- *Occupation claim.* English rule depended on a network of garrisons, records, revenue, and local cooperation.
- *Artillery claim.* Guns changed siege expectations, but surrender and abandonment were often more common than assault.
- *Causal claim.* The reconquest was a system collapse as well as a sequence of technical victories.
- *Source claim.* Administrative records reveal the everyday infrastructure of military power.

**Critical cautions.** Do not extract only the artillery passages and lose
the article's warning about categories. Distinguish Curry's own conclusions
from her summary of Rogers. Cite the published pagination, not the
repository PDF's file-page count.

**Short quotation bank.** "the military revolution has been a moveable
feast" — Curry, p. 171.

**Acquisition route.** University repository PDF:
https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/153723/1/Curry.pdf. Saved in the source library

# Part II — Cross-source synthesis

## A. War, technology, and state capacity

The strongest combined argument does not run 'cannon destroyed castles,
therefore kings created modern states.' Contamine, Curry, DeVries, Rogers,
Parker, Hall, and Stevenson instead support a layered causal chain. Weapons
changed the cost and expected duration of siege; those weapons required
skilled labour, transport, powder, projectiles, animals, credit, records,
and command; large political organisations were better placed to sustain
that system; and the perception that resistance was futile encouraged
surrender, treason, and abandonment before physical destruction.

Stevenson is crucial because the documentary list preserves different modes
of loss. Curry is crucial because she embeds those events in the
occupation's administrative system and warns against forcing them into a
universal label. DeVries and Hall constrain technological claims; Contamine
supplies the royal-military institutions; Parker and Rogers locate the
result inside the broader debate. The manuscript's defensible thesis is
therefore about a change in the political economy of fortification, not a
mechanically decisive gun.

## B. Kingship, office, and the collapse of coordination

Watts, Griffiths, Famiglietti, Wilkinson, and the separate Pollard digest
show why personal monarchy cannot be analysed as personal psychology alone.
The king was an office that authorised decisions, allocated patronage,
embodied justice, and focused counsel. Illness or incapacity became
politically consequential through custody, household, council, seals,
access, legitimacy, and the distribution of losses.

French defeat imposed costs on the Lancastrian regime and discredited its
managers, but domestic crisis arose because the regime could not convert
royal authority into broadly acceptable coordination. The comparison with
Charles VI strengthens this point: similar personal incapacity can produce
different sequences depending on the institutional settlement around the
crown.

## C. Universalism, corporation, and medieval pluralism

Ullmann, Morris, and Tierney together prevent two opposite errors. Medieval
Latin Christendom was not a flat field of local jurisdictions, because the
papacy articulated and sometimes exercised universal governmental authority.
Yet it was not a papal state, because bishops, chapters, orders, councils,
rulers, cities, and customary bodies possessed real jurisdictions. Conciliar
theory emerged within this hierarchical legal order, not outside it.

The analytical lesson for new medievalism is that overlap can be highly
legalised. Multiple authority does not necessarily mean disorder,
informality, or privatisation. It may be produced by sophisticated doctrines
of competence, appeal, corporation, representation, and exception.

## D. State systems, empires, and neo-medieval order

Wight and Bull establish the baseline: patterned interaction is not yet a
society, and anarchy does not preclude institutions. Friedrichs and Cerny
then describe modern unbundling and overlapping authority; Zielonka applies
a governed version of the model to the EU. The cases should be arranged
along at least two axes: degree of overlap and degree of institutionalised
order.

| Pattern | Authority structure | Expected order | Best source |
|---|---|---|---|
| International society | Territorial states with shared rules | Moderate–high | Bull; Wight |
| Neo-medieval governance | Overlapping legal and political competences | Variable but institutionalised | Friedrichs; Zielonka |
| Durable disorder | Unbundled functions and contested coercion | Low / uneven | Cerny |
| Composite empire | Asymmetric centres and negotiated territories | Potentially high | Vaughan; İnalcık |
| Papal monarchy | Universal hierarchy with corporate jurisdictions | Legalised but conflictual | Ullmann; Morris; Tierney |

## E. Composite polities and frontier formation

Vaughan's Burgundy and the Ottoman works show two non-state routes to
large-scale capacity. Burgundy accumulated territories through dynastic and
diplomatic mechanisms while preserving local privileges. The Ottoman polity
emerged in a frontier environment of fluid alliances and identities, then
developed a classical imperial system of household, provincial, fiscal,
legal, and military institutions.

These cases make the anti-flattening principle explicit: all overlapping
orders are not the same. Comparison should identify the units, legal claims,
resource systems, coercive organisations, and mechanisms of incorporation in
each case.

## Methodological note

This dossier is intentionally conservative about access and quotation. A
public catalogue record is not a full text; a preview is not a loan; an
authenticated platform landing page is not proof of item-level entitlement;
a review is not the reviewed book; and a controlled digital loan is not an
unrestricted download. These distinctions are preserved throughout so that
every quotation can later be traced to a lawful, identifiable copy.

The interpretive summaries are research digests: they identify an author's
principal problem, likely contribution, manuscript use, and main cautions.
Where the text was not in hand, the dossier does not pretend to replace
reading. Instead it gives the minimum defensible orientation needed to
decide what to acquire first.
